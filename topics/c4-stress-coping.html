üéì Foundational concepts
Stress
A non-specific response of the body to any demand made upon it (Selye), or as a transaction between the individual and the environment.
Arousal
The level of physical and psychological activation an individual experiences, existing on a continuum from deep sleep to intense excitement.
Anxiety
A temporary emotional state of subjective, consciously perceived feelings of apprehension and tension, associated with activation of the autonomic nervous system.
Cognitive Anxiety
The worry component of anxiety, characterized by negative thoughts, images and concerns.
Somatic Anxiety
The physiological component of anxiety, such as increased heart rate, trembling and muscle tension.
üìä Theories and models
Inverted-U Theory
A theory proposing that the relationship between arousal and performance follows an inverted U-shape, with optimal performance occurring at a moderate level of arousal.
Catastrophe Theory
A multidimensional model suggesting that when cognitive anxiety is high, a further increase in physiological arousal can lead to a sudden and dramatic decline in performance.
Appraisal
The process through which a person evaluates whether a particular encounter with the environment is relevant to their well-being, involving primary and secondary appraisal phases.

Coping
The constantly changing cognitive and behavioural efforts to manage specific external and/or internal demands that are appraised as taxing or exceeding the resources of the person.
Problem-focused Coping
Coping strategies that attempt to influence the person-situation relationship (the problem).
Emotion-focused Coping
Coping strategies that attempt to manage the emotional consequences of the stressor.
Self-talk
The internal dialogue individuals have with themselves, which can be used to direct attention, affect motivational state or generate emotion.

Deep understanding of stress, anxiety and coping mechanisms is essential for applying theory to real-world sporting scenarios‚Äîa core IB SEHS exam skill.

C.4.1 Arousal and anxiety

Understanding arousal and anxiety nature, and their physiological manifestation, is foundational for analysing their complex impact on athletic performance.

Key concepts

  • Stress (Selye): "Non-specific response of the body to any demand made upon it," encompassing negative (distress) and positive (eustress) forms. Can be categorized by duration and nature (time-limited, chronic, stressful event sequences).
  • Arousal vs Anxiety: Arousal is level of physiological and psychological activation; anxiety is emotional state characterized by feelings of apprehension and tension, involving both cognitive (worry) and somatic (physiological) components.
  • Autonomic Nervous System (ANS): Controls physiological response to stress. Sympathetic division activates "fight-or-flight" response (increased heart rate, adrenaline release); parasympathetic division calms the body.

Theories of arousal and performance

Drive Theory: Proposes simple linear relationship where performance increases proportionally with arousal. This theory has very little support in modern sport psychology.

Inverted-U Theory: Performance improves as arousal increases, but only up to an optimal point. Beyond this point, performance deteriorates. The optimal level of arousal can vary depending on the complexity of the task.

Catastrophe Theory: Multidimensional model explaining performance by analyzing the interaction between physiological arousal and cognitive anxiety.

  • When cognitive anxiety is low, performance follows a simple inverted-U pattern.
  • When cognitive anxiety is high, increasing physiological arousal can lead to a sudden, sharp, dramatic drop ("catastrophe") in performance.
  • Recovery from catastrophe requires significant reduction in physiological arousal.
"Choking" in sports: An athlete with high cognitive anxiety (e.g., worry about match point importance in tennis) who also experiences high physiological arousal (pounding heart, muscle tension) may suffer a catastrophic drop in performance, leading to a simple unforced error‚Äîexplained by Catastrophe Theory.
In exams, don't just describe the Inverted-U theory. Always critique its limitations: lack of credible evidence and failure to explain why performance declines can be sudden and dramatic. This shows higher-level understanding.

C.4.2 Coping

While stress and anxiety are inevitable in competitive sport, an athlete's ability to manage them through effective coping strategies often separates good performance from great performance.

Key concepts

  • Coping: Constantly changing cognitive and behavioural efforts to manage specific demands appraised as taxing or exceeding an individual's resources.
  • Appraisal: Cornerstone of coping process‚Äîhow an individual interprets the meaning of a situation.
  • Types of coping: Strategies broadly grouped into problem-focused (addressing the stressor itself), emotion-focused (managing the emotional response), and avoidance (disengaging from the stressor).

The appraisal process mechanism

Scenario: A footballer steps up to take the final kick in a penalty shootout to win the championship.

  1. Primary Appraisal: The player evaluates the situation‚Äî"What is at stake for me?" They think, "This kick decides the entire championship." They appraise the situation as highly stressful and as a significant challenge.
  2. Secondary Appraisal: Following this, the player evaluates their resources‚Äî"Can I control this?" They think, "I've practiced this exact shot a hundred times. I have the skill and know my spot." This is an assessment that they have the resources to cope.
  3. Coping Response: Based on appraisals, the player engages in coping. They might use a problem-focused strategy (following their pre-shot routine) and an emotion-focused strategy (taking a deep breath and using a positive self-talk cue like "My spot, my goal").
  4. Ongoing Reappraisal: The process is dynamic. As the player starts their run-up, they notice the goalkeeper leans slightly to the left. They instantly reappraise the situation and subtly adjust their shot placement, demonstrating that coping is a continuous, unfolding process.
Elite tennis player Justine Henin: By writing and verbalizing the phrase "Allez!" ("Let's go!"), she used a strategic form of self-talk to focus her mind and energize her effort at a critical moment, helping her win a Grand Slam tournament.
The effectiveness of a coping strategy is not inherent to the strategy itself but depends on its "goodness-of-fit" with the situation and the individual. A strategy helpful in one context (e.g., problem-solving during practice) may be ineffective in another (e.g., during fast-paced game where emotion-focused strategies are needed).

Paper 1A: Multiple-choice questions

Question 1
According to Hans Selye's early research, how is stress defined?
  • A. A purely negative psychological response to a perceived threat
  • B. The process of appraising a situation as exceeding one's coping resources
  • C. A specific physiological response tailored to the type of stressor encountered
  • D. A non-specific response of the body to any demand made upon it
‚úÖ Correct answer: D
Selye (1976) defined stress as the "non-specific response of the body to any demand made upon it," meaning the physiological response is the same regardless of the stressor. A is wrong‚ÄîSelye introduced "eustress" (positive) and "distress" (negative), so stress isn't purely negative. B describes the transactional model of stress (Lazarus and Folkman), not Selye's definition. C is wrong‚ÄîSelye's definition emphasizes that the response is "non-specific," not tailored to the stressor.
Question 2
An athlete experiencing a pounding heart and trembling before a race is exhibiting which component of anxiety?
  • A. Cognitive anxiety
  • B. Trait anxiety
  • C. Somatic anxiety
  • D. State appraisal
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Somatic anxiety is the physiological component of anxiety, including physical symptoms like increased heart rate, muscle tension and trembling. A (Cognitive anxiety) refers to the mental component (worry and negative thoughts), not physical symptoms. B (Trait anxiety) refers to a general predisposition to be anxious, not specific symptoms experienced in a particular situation (state anxiety). D (Appraisal) is the cognitive process of evaluating a stressor, not a component of the anxiety response itself.
Question 3
Which of the following is a key limitation of the Inverted-U theory?
  • A. It fails to account for the influence of cognitive anxiety on performance
  • B. It suggests that performance declines can be sudden and dramatic
  • C. It accurately predicts that every athlete has the same optimal level of arousal
  • D. It lacks a credible weight of evidence and does not provide an explanatory mechanism for the performance decline
‚úÖ Correct answer: D
Key Inverted-U limitations include "lack of credible weight of evidence on responses in sport" and that "descriptions of relationships do not provide explanatory mechanisms." While A is true, it's more specifically addressed by multidimensional theories like Catastrophe Theory. B is wrong‚Äîthis is a feature of Catastrophe Theory; the Inverted-U proposes gradual, symmetrical decline. C is wrong‚Äîthe theory can be adapted to suggest different optimal points for different individuals or tasks.
Question 4
An athlete creating a detailed competition plan and seeking information on their opponent's weaknesses is primarily using which type of coping strategy?
  • A. Emotion-focused coping
  • B. Avoidance coping
  • C. Distraction-oriented coping
  • D. Problem-focused coping
‚úÖ Correct answer: D
Problem-focused coping strategies aim to influence the person-situation relationship by addressing the problem directly. Planning and information seeking are classic examples. A (Emotion-focused) aims to manage emotional responses (e.g., through relaxation or self-talk), not solve the external problem. B (Avoidance) involves disengaging from the stressful situation‚Äîopposite of actively planning. C (Distraction-oriented) involves shifting attention away from the stressor, whereas this athlete is focusing intently on it.

Common errors

Confusing arousal with anxiety
Students often use these terms interchangeably. Arousal is a neutral state of physiological and psychological activation on a continuum from deep sleep to intense excitement. Anxiety is a negative emotional state characterized by worry and apprehension. An athlete can be highly aroused and feel excited and ready (positive), or highly aroused and feel anxious (negative).
Believing stress is always negative
The term "stress" often has negative connotation in everyday language. However, in sports psychology, Selye's concept includes "eustress," which is positive stress that can be motivating and exciting (e.g., thrill of competition). Not all stress is "distress."
Applying Inverted-U theory as a definitive law
Many students describe the Inverted-U theory without acknowledging its significant limitations. It's a simplistic model lacking strong empirical support and fails to explain why performance drops can be sudden and catastrophic. A high-level answer must evaluate the theory, not just state it.
Assuming problem-focused coping is superior to emotion-focused coping
Students may assume that actively solving a problem is always the best approach. However, coping strategy effectiveness depends on the situation's controllability. If a stressor is uncontrollable (e.g., referee's bad decision), emotion-focused coping (e.g., managing your anger) is more effective than trying to change something that cannot be changed. The key concept is "goodness-of-fit."
Viewing appraisal of stress as a single event
The appraisal process (primary and secondary) is often taught as a linear sequence. However, it's a dynamic and ongoing process of reappraisal. Athletes constantly re-evaluate the situation and their coping resources as a competition unfolds and circumstances change.
Misunderstanding the "catastrophe" in catastrophe theory
The "catastrophe" is not just a decline in performance. It's a specific decline type that is sudden, dramatic, and occurs when high cognitive anxiety is combined with high physiological arousal. Furthermore, a key feature is that recovery does not follow the same path back; arousal must be lowered significantly for performance to return.
