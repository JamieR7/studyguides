üìö Section 1

Quick reference: key terms

Arousal & Anxiety
Arousal
The level of physical and psychological activation ranging from deep sleep to intense excitement.
Cognitive Anxiety
The mental component of anxiety, characterized by negative expectations, worry, and apprehension.
Somatic Anxiety
The physical manifestation of anxiety, such as increased heart rate, blood pressure, or muscular tension.
Individual Zone of Optimal Functioning (IZOF)
A theory stating that athletes have a specific, personal range of arousal where performance is maximized.
Catastrophe Theory
A model predicting that high cognitive anxiety causes a sudden, dramatic collapse in performance as somatic arousal passes an optimal threshold.
Stress & Coping
Stressor
A stimulus or situation that causes psychological strain, which can be interpreted as either positive (eustress) or negative (distress).
Coping Strategies
Deliberate cognitive or behavioral efforts used to manage internal or external demands that exceed an individual's resources.
Problem-focused Coping
Strategies that target the stressor itself to alter the situation, such as information seeking or time management.
Emotion-focused Coping
Strategies aimed at regulating the emotional distress associated with the stressor, such as relaxation or self-talk.
Avoidance-focused Coping
Strategies involving physical or psychological withdrawal from the stressor, such as procrastination or quitting.
Self-talk
An internal dialogue‚Äîintentional or unintentional‚Äîused to trigger technical movements or regulate emotional states.
So What? Mastery of these terms prevents the "vague phrasing" that frequently leads to mark loss. For example, using "stress" as a synonym for "somatic anxiety" ignores the specific physiological activation requirements of the 2026 syllabus. Precise terminology is the primary tool for earning marks in Paper 2's "Explain" and "Analyse" questions.

Content summary

C.4.1 Arousal and anxiety (Syllabus p. 48)

Arousal is the physiological "engine" of performance. However, without management, high arousal coupled with high cognitive anxiety creates technical and psychological collapse.

Key concepts

  • Drive Theory, Inverted U Theory, IZOF, Catastrophe Theory: Different models explaining the arousal-performance relationship.
  • Somatic vs. Cognitive Anxiety: Physical symptoms versus mental worry components.
  • The "Big Five" Personality Traits (C.1.1):
  1. Openness: Willingness to try new training methods.
  2. Conscientiousness: Tendency for goal-directed, socially acceptable behavior.
  3. Extraversion: Drawing energy from social interaction (team environments).
  4. Agreeableness: How well an individual relates to others.
  5. Neuroticism: The extent to which an individual perceives situations as distressing (highly linked to anxiety).

Mechanism of Catastrophe Theory

  1. Condition: Cognitive anxiety (worry) must be high.
  2. Ascension: As somatic arousal increases toward an optimal threshold, performance remains high.
  3. The Catastrophe: If arousal exceeds the threshold, performance "crashes" or ends prematurely. This is a sudden vertical drop, not a gradual curve.
Sporting Application: In a championship tennis match, a player with high worry (cognitive) may experience a "catastrophe" after a double fault increases their heart rate (somatic) beyond their limit, leading to a complete technical breakdown and loss of the set.
TIP: Examiners penalize students who treat "Arousal" and "Anxiety" as synonyms. Arousal is a neutral state of activation; anxiety is a negative emotional interpretation of that activation.

C.4.2 Coping (Syllabus p. 49)

Coping strategies are the tools used to maintain homeostasis. Their effectiveness is strictly dictated by the "controllability" of the stressor.

Key concepts

  • Problem-focused, Emotion-focused, and Avoidance-focused coping
  • Self-talk; Relaxation; Social support
  • Maladaptive Strategies: These hinder goal attainment and include venting (unpleasant emotional outbursts), self-blame, and mental/physical disengagement (withdrawal).

How Self-Talk Functions

  1. Recall: Accessing positive past experiences.
  2. Acronyms: Using "cue words" (e.g., "SMOOTH") to trigger focus.
  3. Instruction: Describing a movement in one word (e.g., "SNAP") to simplify complex tasks.
Sporting Application: A swimmer uses problem-focused coping by adjusting their goggles (controllable stressor) or emotion-focused coping (self-talk) to manage the fear of a stronger opponent (uncontrollable stressor).
NOTE: Seeking support is the most effective coping strategy for athletes. It can be problem-focused (seeking technical advice) or emotion-focused (seeking comfort).
So What? The 2026 syllabus emphasizes that problem-focused coping is superior for controllable stressors. Using an emotion-focused strategy (like relaxation) for a controllable issue (like incorrect equipment) is inefficient and may lead to performance decline.

Paper 1A: Multiple-choice questions

Question 1
Which personality trait from the "Big Five" is most associated with an athlete perceiving a situation as distressing?
  • A. Conscientiousness
  • B. Neuroticism
  • C. Extraversion
  • D. Openness
‚úÖ Correct answer: B
Why B is correct: Neuroticism describes the tendency to maintain emotional balance or perceive distress.
Why A is wrong: Conscientiousness relates to goal-direction, not distress perception.
Why C is wrong: Extraversion relates to social energy, not emotional distress.
Why D is wrong: Openness relates to curiosity and trying new things, not anxiety.
Question 2
According to Catastrophe Theory, what must be high for a sudden performance collapse to occur?
  • A. Self-confidence
  • B. Somatic arousal only
  • C. Cognitive anxiety
  • D. Task orientation
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Why C is correct: The theory explicitly requires high cognitive anxiety (worry) for the vertical "crash" to occur as somatic arousal rises.
Why A is wrong: High self-confidence would prevent a catastrophe, not cause it.
Why B is wrong: Somatic arousal alone does not cause catastrophe; cognitive anxiety must also be high.
Why D is wrong: Task orientation relates to motivation (C.3), not the catastrophe mechanism.
Question 3
A golfer using a relaxation technique to lower their heart rate before a putt is using which type of coping?
  • A. Problem-focused
  • B. Avoidance-focused
  • C. Emotion-focused
  • D. Maladaptive
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Why C is correct: Relaxation regulates the emotional/physiological response to the stressor but does not change the stressor (the putt) itself.
Why A is wrong: Problem-focused coping would involve changing the situation (e.g., adjusting grip), not managing the emotional response.
Why B is wrong: Avoidance would mean withdrawing from the putt, not preparing for it.
Why D is wrong: Relaxation is adaptive, not maladaptive; it helps performance.
Question 4
Which of the following is considered a maladaptive coping strategy?
  • A. Seeking social support
  • B. Logical analysis
  • C. Venting unpleasant emotions
  • D. Thought control
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Why C is correct: Venting is explicitly listed in the syllabus (p. 49) as a disengagement strategy that is maladaptive.
Why A is wrong: Seeking social support is one of the most effective adaptive coping strategies.
Why B is wrong: Logical analysis is a problem-focused adaptive strategy.
Why D is wrong: Thought control (e.g., self-talk) is an emotion-focused adaptive strategy.

Catastrophe Theory and performance prediction
Explain how Catastrophe Theory predicts performance when cognitive anxiety is high.
üìã Model answer
When cognitive anxiety is high, increases in somatic arousal initially improve performance to an optimal point. [1 mark]

If arousal increases beyond this point, a "catastrophe" occurs. [1 mark]

Evaluate the effectiveness of problem-focused vs. emotion-focused coping strategies in a competitive match.
üìã Model answer
Problem-focused coping (e.g., tactical changes) is highly effective for controllable stressors. [1 mark]

Mathematical skills

In Topic C.4, mathematical skills (Tool 3) provide the objective evidence required to validate psychological theories. Quantitative measures such as Heart Rate Variability (HRV) and Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) are essential for "triangulation"‚Äîpairing objective data with subjective questionnaires to improve assessment validity.

Key Formula: Percentage Change
Percentage Change = ((New Value - Old Value) / Old Value) √ó 100

Worked example: Heart Rate Variability (HRV) Change

Scenario: An athlete's HRV (measured in milliseconds) drops during a high-pressure penalty.

  • Pre-test: 85 ms
  • Post-test: 52 ms

Step 1: Calculate difference: 52 - 85 = -33

Step 2: Divide by original: -33 / 85 = -0.3882

Step 3: Multiply by 100: -0.3882 √ó 100 = -38.8%

Final Answer: 38.8% decrease in HRV

So What? Objective measurements like HRV are vital because self-report questionnaires (subjective) are prone to social desirability bias. However, heart rate alone is an insufficient marker as it cannot distinguish between high intensity exercise and psychological anxiety.

Common errors

Describing Catastrophe Theory as a "Curve"
This is a critical failure. The decline is sudden and vertical. Describing it as a gradual decline like the Inverted U will result in zero marks for that point.
Confusing Arousal and Anxiety
Treating them as synonyms. Arousal is activation; anxiety is the emotional interpretation. Using "stress" as a synonym for "somatic anxiety" ignores the specific physiological activation requirements of the 2026 syllabus.
Misapplying Coping Types
Suggesting an athlete use "Problem-focused" coping for weather or opponent ability (uncontrollable). Problem-focused coping only works for controllable stressors.
Colloquial "Stress" Definitions
Defining stress as "being busy" rather than the scientific definition: "a stressor causing psychological strain." The 2026 syllabus is a scientific curriculum; colloquialisms are the fastest way to lose marks.
Over-reliance on Subjective Data
Ignoring the requirement to use objective markers (HRV/GSR) when analyzing stress. Self-report questionnaires alone are prone to social desirability bias and must be paired with objective measures.
Failure to identify Maladaptive Strategies
Forgetting that venting and self-blame are explicit syllabus components (p. 49) and are maladaptive. Students often incorrectly identify these as effective coping methods.
So What? From an examiner's perspective, these errors persist because students rely on "common sense" language. The 2026 syllabus is a scientific curriculum; colloquialisms are the fastest way to lose marks.

Linking questions (Networked Knowledge)

The 2026 syllabus demands the ability to link C.4 concepts to the wider curriculum.

How does arousal and somatic anxiety (C.4.1) link to Topic A.1.1 Inter-system Communication?
Arousal and somatic anxiety are physical manifestations of the Autonomic Nervous System. When a stressor is detected by chemoreceptors (sensing changes in blood acidity/CO‚ÇÇ), the medulla oblongata sends nervous signals via the sympathetic nervous system to increase heart rate and ventilation. This physiological "cascade" is what we measure as somatic anxiety in C.4.1.
How does high stress (C.4) link to Topic B.3.1 Causes of Injury?
High stress is a major internal risk factor for injury. Psychological strain leads to attentional narrowing (C.2.2), causing an athlete to miss external cues (like an opponent's position). Simultaneously, stress increases muscle tension (B.1.3), which reduces joint flexibility and increases the risk of acute trauma (B.3.1.2).
So What? Evaluating the athlete holistically‚Äîlinking the medulla oblongata (physiology) to attentional narrowing (psychology) and muscle tension (biomechanics)‚Äîis the hallmark of a Level 7 student.
