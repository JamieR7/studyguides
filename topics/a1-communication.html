Quick Reference: Key Terms

Mastering precise terminology is a critical first step for success in any IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science examination. The correct application of scientific terms demonstrates a clear and unambiguous understanding of complex physiological concepts.

üîó Inter-system Communication
System Integration
The process whereby different body systems work together to perform a function, recognizing that they do not operate in isolation.
Neuroendocrine System
The combined and integrated functions of the nervous and endocrine systems to control and regulate physiological processes.
Hormone
A chemical messenger produced by an endocrine gland and transported via the bloodstream to a target organ to exert a specific effect.
Up-regulation
An increase in the number of receptors on the surface of target cells, making the cells more sensitive to a hormone or other agent.
Down-regulation
A decrease in the number of receptors on the surface of target cells, making the cells less sensitive to a hormone or other agent.
Feedback
Information sent from a sensor back to a control center, allowing for the continuous monitoring and regulation of a physiological variable.
‚öñÔ∏è Maintaining Homeostasis
Homeostasis
The maintenance of a constant and stable internal environment within the body, despite changes in external conditions.
Negative Feedback Loop
A regulatory mechanism in which a change in a physiological variable triggers a response that counteracts the initial change, returning the variable to its set point.
Insulin
A hormone secreted by the pancreas that lowers blood glucose levels by promoting its uptake into cells and its conversion to glycogen.
Glucagon
A hormone secreted by the pancreas that raises blood glucose levels by stimulating the breakdown of glycogen in the liver.
Glycogen
The storage form of glucose in animals, primarily found in the liver and muscles.

Content Summary

The Principle of Inter-system Communication

Although each body system has a unique function, integration between systems is essential for homeostasis, and for health and performance to be developed and maintained.

üîÑ Communication Pathway

  • Stimulus & Receptor: An internal or external change (stimulus) is detected by a specialized sensor (receptor)
  • Afferent Pathway: The receptor sends a signal to a control center, most often the brain or a specific endocrine gland
  • Integration: The control center processes the incoming signal and determines the appropriate response
  • Efferent Pathway: The control center sends an output signal, often a nerve impulse or a hormone, to an effector
  • Effector & Response: The effector (e.g., a muscle or gland) carries out the response, which alters the original condition

During the initial phase of a warm-up, proprioceptors in the muscles and joints sense movement. They send nerve signals to the brain (nervous system), which in turn signals the cardiovascular center to increase heart rate and the endocrine system to begin releasing adrenaline. This demonstrates direct communication between the muscular, nervous, cardiovascular, and endocrine systems to meet the rising demand for oxygen and fuel.

Data Booklet Reference: Familiarize yourself with the diagrams of the major anatomical systems, particularly the endocrine system, provided in the Sports, exercise and health science data booklet. You may need to identify glands or organs from these diagrams in an exam.

The Mechanism of Homeostasis

The body uses negative feedback loops as the primary mechanism for maintaining homeostasis. Each body system can be up-regulated or down-regulated depending on environmental conditions.

ü©∏ Blood Glucose Regulation

  • High Blood Glucose: After a meal, blood glucose rises and is detected by beta cells in the pancreas
  • Insulin Release: The pancreas releases insulin into the bloodstream
  • Cellular Uptake: Insulin promotes glucose uptake by cells and stimulates liver to convert glucose to glycogen
  • Restoration: As glucose is removed, its concentration returns to normal range and insulin secretion decreases
  • Low Blood Glucose: During exercise, blood glucose falls and is detected by alpha cells in the pancreas
  • Glucagon Release: The pancreas releases glucagon, which stimulates the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose
Important Concept: Homeostasis is not a static, unchanging state. It is a highly dynamic process of equilibrium where physiological variables are maintained within a narrow, acceptable range through constant monitoring and adjustments.

Question 1
Which statement best describes homeostasis?
  • A. A static state where all physiological variables are kept at a fixed value
  • B. The process of hormonal communication between the brain and the body
  • C. The maintenance of a stable internal environment within a narrow range
  • D. The breakdown of glucose to provide energy for muscular contraction
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: This is the precise definition of homeostasis. It emphasizes the "stable" internal environment and the concept of a "narrow range," highlighting its dynamic nature rather than being fixed.

Why A is wrong: Homeostasis is a dynamic process, not a static state. Variables fluctuate around a set point.

Why B is wrong: Hormonal communication is a mechanism used to achieve homeostasis, but it is not the definition of homeostasis itself.

Why D is wrong: This describes a part of cellular respiration (glycolysis), which is a metabolic process, not the overarching concept of maintaining internal stability.
Question 2
Following the consumption of a large meal rich in carbohydrates, which hormonal response would be expected?
  • A. Increased glucagon secretion from the pancreas
  • B. Increased insulin secretion from the pancreas
  • C. Increased adrenaline secretion from the adrenal gland
  • D. Decreased insulin secretion from the pancreas
‚úÖ Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: A carbohydrate-rich meal leads to a rise in blood glucose levels. The pancreas detects this increase and secretes insulin to promote the uptake of glucose by cells and its storage in the liver, thereby lowering blood glucose back to its homeostatic range.

Why A is wrong: Glucagon is secreted in response to low blood glucose to raise it, which is the opposite of the situation described.

Why C is wrong: Adrenaline is primarily released in response to stress or exercise, not directly in response to high blood glucose from a meal.

Why D is wrong: Insulin secretion would increase, not decrease, to manage the influx of glucose into the blood.
Question 3
What is the term for the integration of the nervous and endocrine systems to maintain physiological balance?
  • A. Cardiorespiratory system
  • B. Musculoskeletal system
  • C. Homeostatic system
  • D. Neuroendocrine system
‚úÖ Correct Answer: D
Why D is correct: The term neuroendocrine specifically refers to the interaction and integration of the nervous system (neuro-) and the endocrine system (-endocrine). This system is essential for homeostasis and coordinating responses to stimuli.

Why A is wrong: The cardiorespiratory system refers to the heart, blood vessels, and lungs working together for gas transport.

Why B is wrong: The musculoskeletal system refers to the bones, muscles, and connective tissues responsible for movement.

Why C is wrong: "Homeostatic system" is a general, non-specific term. While the neuroendocrine system is a key part of maintaining homeostasis, D is the more precise and correct term.
Question 4
A decrease in the number of hormone receptors on a target cell, making it less sensitive to that hormone, is known as what?
  • A. Up-regulation
  • B. Negative feedback
  • C. Down-regulation
  • D. System integration
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: Down-regulation is the specific process where target cells reduce the number of receptors in response to high concentrations of a hormone, thereby decreasing their sensitivity to it.

Why A is wrong: Up-regulation is the opposite process, where receptor numbers increase, enhancing sensitivity.

Why B is wrong: Negative feedback is a control mechanism for the entire homeostatic loop, not the specific change in receptor numbers on a cell.

Why D is wrong: System integration is the broad concept of body systems working together, not a specific cellular process.

Question 3
Methodology Evaluation
A student designed an investigation to see if listening to calming music affects heart rate. The student measured their own heart rate using a smartwatch while sitting quietly for 5 minutes. Then, they listened to calming music for 15 minutes and measured their heart rate again at the end. They concluded that calming music lowers heart rate.
Part (a)

Evaluate the methodology of this investigation.

üìã Model Answer:
The methodology has significant limitations affecting its validity and reliability.

Limitation 1 (Lack of Control): There is no control group or condition; the student did not perform a trial sitting for 15 minutes without music to compare against. The observed change could be due to simply sitting for a longer period, not the music itself.

Limitation 2 (Reliability/Validity): The use of a single measurement from a consumer-grade smartwatch may lack accuracy and reliability. The validity of such devices can be questionable, and a single data point is not sufficient.

Limitation 3 (Sample Size): The experiment was conducted on only one subject (n=1). This makes it impossible to generalize the findings to a wider population, as individual responses can vary greatly.

Improvement: To improve the study, the student should use a larger sample of participants, randomly assign them to either a music (experimental) or no-music (control) group, and take multiple, more precise heart rate measurements throughout the period.

Mathematical Skills for A.1

The ability to quantify, analyze, and interpret data is essential. Calculating percentage change is a fundamental skill for measuring physiological changes.

Percentage Change = ((Final Value - Initial Value) / Initial Value) √ó 100

Worked Example

Scenario: A researcher is monitoring heart rate response to mild exercise.

  • ‚Ä¢ Initial resting heart rate: 60 bpm
  • ‚Ä¢ Final heart rate after exercise: 90 bpm

Calculation Steps:

  1. Substitute values: Percentage Change = [(90 - 60) / 60] √ó 100
  2. Calculate difference: Percentage Change = [30 / 60] √ó 100
  3. Perform division: Percentage Change = 0.5 √ó 100
  4. Complete calculation: Percentage Change = 50

Final Answer: The heart rate increased by 50% from its resting value.

Common Errors to Avoid

‚ùå Error: Homeostasis Means 'Static'
A frequent misconception is viewing homeostasis as a fixed, unchanging state. In reality, it is a highly dynamic process of equilibrium. Physiological variables are maintained within a narrow, acceptable range through constant adjustments, not at one exact number.
‚ùå Error: Treating Body Systems as Isolated Units
A significant error is to describe a physiological response by only referring to one system. Always think about how systems communicate and work together through system integration. The neuroendocrine system exemplifies this interconnection.
‚ùå Error: Blindly Trusting Wearable Technology Data
The validity, reliability, and accuracy of wearable device data can be questionable. Demonstrate critical thinking by acknowledging the limitations of such technology rather than treating its output as infallible scientific fact.
‚ùå Error: Confusing Correlation with Causation
Just because two variables change together does not mean one causes the other. Scientific research often identifies strong correlations, but proving direct causation requires controlled experiments that eliminate confounding variables.
‚ùå Error: Confusing Up-regulation with Down-regulation
Up-regulation is an increase in receptor numbers (making cells more sensitive), typically in response to low hormone concentrations. Down-regulation is a decrease in receptor numbers (making cells less sensitive), typically in response to high hormone concentrations.
‚ùå Error: Negative Feedback is 'Bad'
The term "negative" does not imply something harmful. It simply means the response opposes or negates the original stimulus. This is the body's primary and most essential mechanism for maintaining stability and health.

Linking Questions Across Topics

How does Topic A.1 Communication link to Topic A.2 Fuelling for health and performance?
The link is fundamental and is best exemplified by blood glucose regulation. Topic A.1 explains the communication system (the endocrine system and its hormones, insulin and glucagon) and the control mechanism (negative feedback). Topic A.2 describes the fuel itself (glucose/carbohydrates) and its use in the energy systems. When an athlete fuels during exercise (A.2), the endocrine system (A.1) must precisely regulate the release of insulin and glucagon to ensure this fuel is transported from the blood to the working muscles efficiently, without causing dangerous spikes or drops in overall blood glucose. The communication system of A.1 is what makes the fuelling strategies of A.2 physiologically possible and effective.
How do the feedback mechanisms in Topic A.1 Communication relate to the causes of injury in Topic B.3 Injury?
Feedback mechanisms from Topic A.1 are critical for injury prevention. Proprioceptors in muscles and joints constantly send feedback to the central nervous system about body position, muscle tension, and joint angles. This communication allows for unconscious adjustments in movement patterns to maintain stability and prevent joints from moving into dangerous ranges of motion. A breakdown in this neuromuscular communication‚Äîfor example, due to fatigue‚Äîcan lead to poor movement mechanics. This faulty feedback can cause the abnormal application of forces on tissues, a primary cause of musculoskeletal injury as described in Topic B.3. Therefore, the integrity of the body's internal communication system is a key factor in mitigating injury risk.
How does the concept of system integration from Topic A.1 Communication help explain the biopsychosocial nature of recovery from injury (Topic B.3)?
Topic A.1 establishes that biological systems do not function in isolation. This principle extends to the biopsychosocial model of injury recovery. An injury (the biological component) triggers a stress response via the neuroendocrine system (A.1), leading to the release of hormones like cortisol. These hormones can impact mood and motivation (the psychological component). Furthermore, the athlete's social environment (e.g., support from a team) can influence their psychological state, which in turn can modulate their hormonal stress response. This demonstrates a complex, multi-directional communication network where biological communication systems (A.1) are influenced by, and in turn influence, psychological and social factors, all of which are critical for a holistic recovery process.
