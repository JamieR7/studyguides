üíª Information processing

The process of focusing on relevant information while ignoring irrelevant information.

The time in which the response to a second stimulus is significantly slowed because a first stimulus is still being processed.

A model of motor control used for very fast movements (under 60-70 ms) where the skill is executed without recourse to feedback.

Content summary

Topic C.2 investigates how humans acquire, refine and perform skills, providing frameworks for optimizing training and performance under pressure.

C.2.1 Motor learning processes

Understanding the underlying processes of skill acquisition is crucial for coaches, teachers and athletes aiming to optimize training effectiveness.

Learning vs Performance

  • Learning: Relatively permanent change in performance brought about by experience.
  • Performance: Temporary occurrence, fluctuating over time‚Äîwhat we observe and measure.
  • Mechanism: Performance is observable action at single point in time, influenced by temporary factors. Learning is inferred internal change resulting in consistently improved performance potential.
Golfer putting practice: Early learner attempts are scattered and inconsistent. After significant practice, putts become clustered around the hole, demonstrating relatively permanent capability change. Individual attempts are performances; sustained improvement over time is evidence of learning.
Performance is what we observe and measure; learning is the underlying change we infer from sustained performance improvements.

Transfer of learning

  • Positive transfer: Practice of one task facilitates learning/performance of another.
  • Negative transfer: Practice of one task inhibits learning/performance of another.
  • Zero transfer: No effect.
  • Mechanism: Transfer refers to how previous experiences influence new skill learning, occurring between different skills, from practice to performance, or between limbs (bilateral transfer).

Information processing (Welford's model)

  1. Input/Sensation: Performer receives environmental information through senses (sight of ball, opponent sound).
  2. Perception: Brain interprets sensory information, comparing it to long-term memory (LTM) past experiences.
  3. Decision: Performer decides what action to take based on situation perception, involving short-term memory (STM).
  4. Efferent organization: Brain sends motor commands to relevant muscles to carry out selected response.
  5. Output: Performer executes movement.
  6. Feedback: Information about movement outcome is received, used to adjust future actions.
While models like Welford's present stages linearly, many processes are carried out simultaneously in reality, not in simple sequential chain.

Psychological Refractory Period (PRP)

  • Mechanism: According to Broadbent's filter model, we have limited information processing capacity. Selective attention acts as filter, allowing only important stimuli to pass into limited-capacity system. PRP occurs because of single-channel processing‚Äîresponse to second stimulus is delayed until first has been dealt with.
  • Hick's Law: As number of stimulus-response choices increases, reaction time increases linearly.
Rugby dummy pass: Initial ball movement is first stimulus (S1); defender begins responding. Actual pass in different direction is second stimulus (S2). Defender's reaction to S2 is delayed because still processing S1, illustrating psychological refractory period.

Fitts & Posner phases of learning

  1. Cognitive phase: Initial stage where learner tries to make sense of skill. Performance is inconsistent with many crude errors.
  2. Associative phase: Practice stage where learner refines skill. Movements become more consistent and coordinated.
  3. Autonomous phase: Final stage where skill is performed automatically with little conscious thought. Performer can focus on strategy and tactics.

Motor control models

  • Open-loop control: Mode for fast movements (under 60-70 ms) executed as pre-programmed sequence without feedback. Like firing a cannon‚Äîonce initiated, runs to completion without alteration. Example: boxing jab too fast for feedback to modify mid-action.
  • Closed-loop control: Mode for slower movements relying on feedback to make real-time adjustments. Like steering a car‚Äîdriver constantly uses visual feedback to compare current position to intended path and make corrections.
  • Schema Theory (Schmidt): Proposes we develop generalized motor programmes. Includes recall schema (for initiating action) and recognition schema (for evaluating movement feel). Explains how we adapt generalized motor programme (throwing) to novel situations (different objects to different targets).

Pedagogical approaches

  • Linear pedagogy: Traditional, teacher-centred approach characterized by reductionist perspective‚Äîskills broken into basic parts and learned in structured, sequential progression (cognitive ‚Üí associative ‚Üí autonomous stages). Focuses on repetitive drills to perfect specific technique with prescriptive feedback.
  • Non-linear pedagogy: Learner-centred approach based on ecological dynamics, involving exploratory learning and encouraging individualized solutions by manipulating constraints (individual, task, environment). Creates representative learning environments (e.g., small-sided games) where learner is encouraged to self-organize and discover effective movement patterns.

Dynamical Systems Theory (DST)

  • Core concept: Views motor learning as process where movement patterns "self-organize" based on multiple component interaction.
  • Constraints: Factors shaping movement‚Äîindividual (height, motivation), task (rules, equipment), environmental (weather, surface).
  • Perception-action coupling: Direct link between what an individual perceives and the action they subsequently take.
  • Affordances: Opportunities for action that an environment provides to an individual.
  • Mechanism: Moves away from brain-as-computer metaphor. Proposes coordinated movement emerges from dynamic learner‚Äìenvironment interaction. Individual, task and environment constrain available movement solutions; performer perceives affordances to achieve their goal.

C.2.2 Attentional control

Managing attention effectively in competitive environments is essential for executing skills under pressure and avoiding performance breakdown.

Dimensions of attention

  • Concentration: Ability to invest mental effort in what is most important in any given situation; central point of attention.
  • Selective attention: Athlete's ability to "zoom in" on task-relevant information while ignoring potential distractions.
  • Divided attention: Ability to perform or coordinate two or more actions simultaneously.

Types of attentional focus

Categorized along two dimensions‚Äîwidth (broad/narrow) and direction (external/internal)‚Äîcreating four types:

  1. Broad-external: Rapidly assessing situation (ice hockey player surveying defensive setup).
  2. Broad-internal: Analyzing and planning (volleyball coach developing game plan).
  3. Narrow-external: Focusing exclusively on one or two external cues (tennis player tracking ball).
  4. Narrow-internal: Mentally rehearsing upcoming performance or focusing on specific feeling (gymnast visualizing routine).

Attentional flexibility: Skilled athletes shift focus between these four quadrants as situation demands.

Arousal and attentional narrowing

  • Arousal: Level of physical and psychological activation, from low (relaxed) to high (intense).
  • Attentional narrowing: Tendency for individual's focus to narrow as arousal level increases.
  • Mechanism: Increased arousal causes physiological changes (increased heart rate, muscle tension) and psychological shift where focus becomes more limited. Can be beneficial (filtering distractions like crowd noise during free throw). However, if arousal becomes too high, narrowing can become excessive, leading to "tunnel vision" where athlete misses important peripheral cues.

External vs internal focus benefit

  • Internal focus: Attention directed towards own body movements ("snap your wrist").
  • External focus: Attention directed towards intended movement effect on environment ("focus on the target").
  • Evidence: Large body of evidence shows external focus results in more effective performance and faster learning. Focusing on movement outcome allows motor system to self-organize more naturally and efficiently, leading to greater accuracy, consistency and efficiency without conscious mechanical control.
Kayaker external focus study: Experienced kayakers performed 100m wild water sprint fastest when given distal external focus instruction ("focus on marker on far bank")‚Äîfaster than proximal external focus ("concentrate on paddle") and control condition with no instruction.

Model answer
The psychological refractory period (PRP) is the delay in a person's response to a second stimulus (S2) when presented very soon after a first stimulus (S1). [1 mark]

Linear vs non-linear pedagogical approaches
A coach is teaching a 10-year-old child how to shoot a basketball. Distinguish between linear and non-linear pedagogical approaches the coach could use.
üìã Model answer
A linear and non-linear pedagogical approach would differ significantly in how the coach structures the learning environment.

Linear approach: A linear approach is teacher-centred and reductionist. The coach would break the basketball shot down into its component parts. [1 mark] They might have the child practice just the "correct" stance and ball release without a hoop (static blocked practice). [1 mark] The coach would provide explicit instructions and prescriptive feedback, focusing on achieving an idealized "perfect" technique through repetition. [1 mark]

Non-linear approach: In contrast, a non-linear approach is learner-centred and based on ecological dynamics. [1 mark] Instead of prescribing a single technique, the coach would manipulate constraints to guide the learner's discovery. Common errors

Confusing learning and performance
Students often use these terms interchangeably. Performance is single, observable, temporary event (scoring one free throw). Learning is inferred, relatively permanent change in performance potential, demonstrated by consistent performance over time.
Assuming information processing is strictly linear
While Welford's model presents stages sequentially for clarity, it's a simplification. In reality, these processes often occur simultaneously. Avoid describing the model as rigid, step-by-step process without acknowledging this limitation.
Mistaking ability for skill
A person may have natural ability (genetic trait, like good coordination), but a skill is learned through practice. Skill involves ability plus correct technique selection to achieve a goal. This distinction matters for coaches: you cannot train innate ability, but you can design practice to develop skill. Mistaking the two leads to ineffective training design.
Believing high arousal is always bad
While excessive arousal can harm fine motor skills like archery through attentional narrowing, high arousal is beneficial for sports requiring strength and power (sprinting, weightlifting). The optimal arousal level depends on the task.
Thinking internal focus is best for learning
It may seem intuitive to focus on body movements ("keep your elbow straight"), but evidence overwhelmingly supports external focus use (focusing on movement's outcome) for both more effective performance and more efficient learning.
Treating all transfer of learning as positive
Students sometimes forget that prior experience can hinder new skill learning (negative transfer). For example, badminton wrist action might negatively transfer to tennis, where a firmer wrist is required.
Overlooking feedback in closed-loop control
The defining feature of closed-loop control is its reliance on feedback. The movement is constantly adjusted by comparing current state feedback (memory trace) to desired state (perceptual trace). Open-loop control operates without feedback.

Linking questions

How can attentional control principles (C.2.2) help prevent negative effects of attentional narrowing linked to mental toughness (C.1.2)?
Mental toughness involves managing stress and maintaining focus under pressure. Strategies from C.2.2, such as pre-performance routines and trigger words, are practical tools athletes can use to regulate arousal, prevent excessive attentional narrowing and maintain optimal focus, thereby demonstrating mental toughness.
How can video technology (B.2.3) be used to provide feedback and monitor progress through learning phases (C.2.1)?
In cognitive phase, video can show learner the discrepancy between their movement and a model. In associative phase, it can be used for detailed frame-by-frame analysis to refine technique. For autonomous phase athletes, post-performance video review can help identify subtle tactical errors without disrupting automatic skill execution.
How can constraints in dynamical systems theory (C.2.1) relate to injury risk (B.3)?
Individual constraints (e.g., fatigue, previous injury, biomechanical limitations) interact with task constraints (e.g., training load, technique demands) and environmental constraints (e.g., surface quality) to influence movement patterns. Poor constraint interaction can lead to maladaptive movement patterns increasing injury risk. Understanding this helps coaches manipulate constraints to promote safer, more effective movement solutions that reduce injury likelihood.
