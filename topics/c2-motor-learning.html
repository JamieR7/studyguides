Quick reference: key terms

In the International Baccalaureate (IB) Sports, Exercise and Health Science (SEHS) examination, "precision of communication" is a vital marking criterion. Mastery of terminology is a strategic necessity, as it allows a candidate to distinguish between transient behavioral changes and stable neurological adaptations. Responses that fail to use these terms with surgical accuracy often fail to access the top markbands, regardless of the student's conceptual understanding.

Learning Processes
Skill
A specific action that has been learned and has a predetermined outcome or goal.
Learning
A relatively permanent change in performance brought about by experience or past practice.
Performance
A temporary occurrence fluctuating over time, often used as an immediate measure of the learning process.
Ability
Inherited perceptual and motor attributes (traits) that enable an individual to perform specific skills.
Psychological Refractory Period (PRP)
The time delay in responding to a second stimulus because a first stimulus is still being processed by the central nervous system.
Memory & Input
Selective Attention
The individual process of focusing on relevant information while ignoring irrelevant environmental "noise."
Signal Detection Theory
A framework explaining the probability of detecting a signal based on the interaction between sensitivity (d-prime) and personal bias (criterion).
Short-term Memory (STM)
A limited-capacity storage system where information is briefly held for approximately 10 seconds before being lost or rehearsed.
Attentional Control
Concentration
An athlete's deliberate decision to invest mental effort on task-relevant cues in a given situation.
Attentional Narrowing
The tendency to focus on a limited set of cues as arousal levels increase, potentially ignoring peripheral information.
Association
Focusing attention on internal bodily sensations and cues related to the activity, such as breathing rate or muscle tension.
Dissociation
The process of diverting attention away from physical sensations toward external stimuli unrelated to the activity.
Mastering these definitions serves as the essential foundation for describing the complex information-processing mechanisms and pedagogical theories detailed in Section 4.

Content summary: C.2.1 Motor Learning & C.2.2 Attentional Control

C.2.1 Motor Learning Processes

Motor learning examines how athletes acquire and refine movement patterns. Strategically, coaches must distinguish between temporary performance fluctuations and the permanent skill acquisition that defines learning.

The Learning/Performance Distinction

Performance is a temporary occurrence, fluctuating over time due to factors like fatigue or luck (p.448). Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior resulting from practice or experience.

Welford's Information Processing Model (p.451)

  1. Input/Sensation: Exteroceptors (vision/audition) or interoceptors (vestibular/joint receptors) relay info.
  2. Perception: The brain interprets and makes sense of the sensory information.
  3. Decision: The brain compares the current information held in STM with past experiences in LTM (a continuous loop) to choose a response.
  4. Efferent Organization: The CNS prepares the specific motor commands.
  5. Output: The actual execution of the movement.
  6. Feedback: Results (intrinsic/extrinsic) are used to adjust future attempts.

Pedagogical Approaches

  • Linear Pedagogy: Teacher-centered; uses repetitive, "reductionist" drills to facilitate basic technique acquisition (p.449).
  • Non-linear Pedagogy: Learner-centered; uses "Self-Organization" where athletes adapt movements based on task and environmental constraints (p.460).

Motor Programmes

Open-loop control is used for very fast movements (60‚Äì70 ms) that cannot be altered mid-action. Closed-loop control uses the "perceptual trace" (memory of the movement feel) to allow for adjustments during the action (p.459).

HICK'S LAW: Reaction time increases as the number of stimulus-response choices increases. This is a linear relationship when reaction time is plotted against the log of stimulus-response couplings (p.455).

C.2.2 Attentional Control

Proficiency requires an athlete to focus on task-relevant cues while filtering out irrelevant "noise," which can be external (crowd) or internal (anxiety).

Dimensions of Attention

  • Concentration: Deliberate mental effort on the task.
  • Selective Attention: Filtering irrelevant distractions to focus on specific cues (p.476).
  • Divided Attention: Coordinating multiple actions simultaneously (e.g., dribbling while scanning for teammates).

Attentional Focus (p.482)

Focus is categorized by Width (Broad/Narrow) and Direction (Internal/External). High-level performance is often associated with a Broad-External focus for assessment and a Narrow-External focus for execution.

Arousal & Narrowing

High arousal causes "Attentional Narrowing," reducing the amount of available information for the decision-making process (p.485).

MEMORY LIMITS: Short-term memory has a capacity of 7¬±2 "bits" of information. While capacity is fixed, "chunking" (grouping items) allows each bit to contain more information (p.453).

Internal processes eventually manifest as observable athletic skill on the field.

  • A. The time between a stimulus and the start of a movement.
  • B. The delay in responding to a second stimulus while the first is being processed.

Why B is correct: PRP is specifically the slowed response to S2 due to the "single-channel" bottleneck. A is reaction time; C is memory capacity; D refers to Hick's Law.
Question 2
In Signal Detection Theory, what occurs during an "error of omission"?
  • A. A signal is perceived when one does not exist.
  • B. A signal is detected correctly despite high background noise.
  • C. A signal is missed when it was actually present.
  • D. Sensitivity (d-prime) is increased due to high arousal.
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Why C is correct: An error of omission (p.452) is missing a signal that occurred. A describes an error of commission (false alarm).
Question 3
According to Miller (1956), how can an athlete increase the information held in STM?
  • A. By increasing the fixed capacity beyond 9 bits.
  • B. By utilizing "chunking" to group information into single bits.
  • C. By avoiding selective attention to process all stimuli.
  • D. By relying solely on the sensory information store.
‚úÖ Correct answer: B
Why B is correct: Chunking allows more data to be stored within the 7¬±2 bit capacity limit (p.454).

Paper 2 (Extended Response)

Question 1 [6 marks]
Discuss the pedagogical choices a coach might make when teaching a novice basketball player.
üìã Model answer
A coach may use Linear Pedagogy initially to build confidence through structured, "reductionist" drills, which allow the novice to master basic technique in a stable environment (p.469). However, they should transition to Non-linear Pedagogy to encourage "self-organization." This allows the learner to find individualized movement solutions based on task constraints (e.g., varying hoop height). This "exploratory learning" increases skill adaptability and ensures the player can perform in the "dynamic" environment of a real game, avoiding the limitations of isolated rote repetition.
Question 2 [4 marks]
Explain Schmidt's Schema Theory in the context of a tennis serve.
üìã Model answer
Schmidt proposed two schemas: the recall schema, used to select and initiate the serve based on past experience, and the recognition schema, which allows the player to "feel" the movement via the perceptual trace and make adjustments (p.459). The player uses the recognition schema to evaluate the outcome and update the motor programme for future serves.

Mathematical skills

Data analysis provides an objective measure of an athlete's improvement and consistency.

Response Time = Reaction Time + Movement Time

Worked Example (Sprinter)

A sprinter has a reaction time (gun to blocks) of 0.15s and a movement time (blocks to 10m) of 1.85s.

Total Response Time = 0.15s + 1.85s = 2.00s.

Standard Deviation (SD) & Performance Variability

SD is used to assess consistency. A large SD in a basketball player's shooting indicates high performance variability, whereas a low SD suggests a stable, "learned" skill (p.468).

Common errors

Learning vs. Performance
Mistaking a single successful attempt (performance) for permanent learning.
Reaction vs. Response
Confusing reaction time (stimulus to start) with total response time (stimulus to completion).
Internal Focus Hindrance
Assuming internal focus is always superior; for elite performers, internal focus on body parts can actually hinder efficiency compared to an external focus (p.485).
Signal Detection 'Noise'
Defining noise only as external sound; it also includes internal thoughts like anxiety.
Memory Interruption
Forgetting that chunking changes the amount of info per bit, not the total number of bits available in STM (7 ¬± 2).
Open vs. Closed Loops
Assuming all skills can be adjusted mid-action; very fast skills (under 70ms) are strictly open-loop.
üîó Section 8

Linking questions

Link C.2 to B.1 (Biomechanics): How do "Degrees of Freedom" interact with self-organization in non-linear pedagogy?
Point: During early learning, athletes often "freeze" degrees of freedom (limiting joint movement) to simplify the task. As they self-organize through non-linear practice, they "release" these degrees of freedom, coordinating them into efficient levers to achieve mechanical advantage (p.469).
Link C.2 to C.1 (Psychology): How does "Mental Toughness" influence a performer's ability to resist "Attentional Narrowing"?
Point: Mental toughness involves managing stress and arousal levels. An athlete with high mental toughness can prevent the excessive narrowing of their attentional field under pressure, maintaining a complete "picture" of relevant environmental cues (p.485).
