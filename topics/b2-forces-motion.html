Content summary

Topic B.2 applies mechanical principles to human movement, from Newton's laws to fluid dynamics, providing the scientific framework for analysing and optimizing performance.

B.2.1 Newton's laws of motion

Newton's three laws form the bedrock of biomechanics, explaining how forces create or change motion in all sporting contexts.

Core principles

  • Vector vs scalar: Vectors have magnitude and direction (velocity, force); scalars have magnitude only (mass, speed).
  • Linear vs angular motion: Linear motion is movement along a straight line; angular motion is rotational.
  • Newton's First Law (Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Newton's Second Law (Acceleration): Force equals mass times acceleration (F = ma); greater force or smaller mass produces greater acceleration.
  • Newton's Third Law (Action‚ÄìReaction): For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force acting on a different object.
  • Impulse‚Äìmomentum relationship: Impulse (force √ó time) equals change in momentum; athletes maximize impulse by applying large forces over extended time.
  • Friction: Resistive force between surfaces; can be beneficial (traction) or detrimental (energy loss).
  • Stability: Influenced by centre of mass height, base of support size, line of gravity position, and mass.
Impulse in action: A high jumper approaches with horizontal momentum, then applies large vertical force to the ground over an extended time (flexing then explosively extending the take-off leg), generating large vertical impulse that changes momentum from horizontal to vertical, propelling them upwards.
Newton's Third Law: A sprinter pushing backwards and downwards on the blocks (action) receives an equal and opposite forwards and upwards force from the blocks (reaction), which accelerates them down the track.
Mass vs weight: Mass is the amount of matter (kg), constant everywhere. Weight is the gravitational force on that mass (N), varying with gravitational field strength. An athlete has the same mass on Earth and the Moon but weighs less on the Moon.
Maximizing stability: Lower centre of mass, increase base of support, keep line of gravity within base. Wrestlers adopt a low, wide stance; basketball players balance stability (holding ground) with instability (changing direction quickly).

B.2.2 Fluid mechanics

Understanding how air and water exert forces on moving bodies is critical for optimizing performance in swimming, cycling, projectile sports and any activity involving fluid resistance.

Projectile motion and fluid forces

  • Projectile motion factors: Initial velocity, angle of projection, and height of release determine trajectory once an object is airborne.
  • Centre of mass trajectory: Once airborne, the centre of mass follows a fixed parabolic path determined by the release parameters; limbs can move around it but the path itself cannot change.
  • Drag force: Resistive force opposing motion through fluid, comprising surface drag (friction with fluid), form drag (pressure differential due to shape), and wave drag (energy lost creating surface waves).
  • Lift force: Force perpendicular to motion direction, generated by pressure differences explained by Bernoulli's principle (faster fluid flow = lower pressure).
  • Magnus effect: Spinning projectile experiences lift force due to rotation creating velocity and pressure differentials; causes curve balls, swerving kicks, and spin-induced deviation.
Magnus effect in football: A player striking a ball off-centre generates sidespin; the side rotating into oncoming air experiences higher pressure (slower velocity), while the side rotating away experiences lower pressure (faster velocity), creating a sideways Magnus force that curves the ball around a defensive wall.
Drafting: Positioning directly behind a competitor creates slipstream, an area of lower pressure and reduced air resistance, significantly reducing energy required to maintain speed‚Äîcritical in cycling and triathlon.
The IB Data Booklet provides formulas for drag and lift forces; you must be able to select and apply them to solve quantitative problems in Paper 1B.

B.2.3 Movement analysis and applications

A phases-of-movement approach provides systematic frameworks for deconstructing skills, identifying errors, and developing improvement strategies.

Phases of movement

  1. Preparatory phase: Athlete positions body and prepares for force production (e.g., tennis backswing).
  2. Force production phase: Synchronized sequence generating and transferring force (e.g., kinetic chain from legs through trunk to racquet).
  3. Critical instant: Exact moment of contact or release determining accuracy, power, effectiveness (e.g., ball contact in tennis).
  4. Follow-through: Continuation after critical instant, decelerating limb safely, preventing injury, maintaining balance (e.g., arm continuation after ball release).
Continuous skill analysis: Front crawl stroke has cyclical phases‚Äîentry (hand enters water), pull (backwards propulsion), push (completing propulsion and exiting), recovery (arm moves forward over water). Analysing joint angles and timing in each phase identifies inefficiencies creating drag or reducing propulsion.
Discrete vs continuous: Discrete skills have clear start/end (throw, jump). Continuous skills are repetitive and cyclical with arbitrary start/end points (running, cycling, swimming).

Common errors

Confusing mass and weight
Mass is amount of matter (kg), intrinsic and constant. Weight is gravitational force on that mass (N), varying with gravitational field. Athlete's mass is constant; weight changes with location.
Assuming optimal angle is always 45¬∞
45¬∞ is optimal only when launch and landing heights are identical. Shot putter (release above landing) needs <45¬∞; basketball shot (release below target) needs >45¬∞.
Believing athletes can alter centre of mass path mid-flight
Once airborne, centre of mass trajectory is fixed by gravity and air resistance, following parabolic path. Athletes can move limbs around centre of mass but cannot change its path.
Misunderstanding "zero gravity"
Orbiting astronauts aren't in zero gravity‚ÄîEarth's gravity remains strong. They feel weightless because they and spacecraft are in continuous free fall around Earth.
Confusing impulse with force
Large force doesn't automatically mean large momentum change. Impulse = force √ó time; to maximize momentum change (jump, throw), athletes apply force for as long as possible.
Misinterpreting Newton's Third Law
Action and reaction forces act on different objects, not the same object (where they'd cancel out). Sprinter pushes blocks (action); blocks push sprinter (reaction)‚Äîthe unopposed reaction accelerates the sprinter.

Linking questions

How does understanding friction and drag (B.2.1/B.2.2) link to enhancing training (A.3)?
Friction and drag principles are applied to create specific resistance training methods enhancing muscular strength and power. Athletes use resistance bands, parachutes (increasing air drag), or train in water/sand (utilizing fluid/frictional resistance) to increase force muscles must overcome, applying overload principle and leading to greater physiological adaptations.
How does impulse‚Äìmomentum relationship (B.2.1) link to causes of injury (B.3)?
The impulse‚Äìmomentum relationship is fundamental to injury prevention when absorbing impacts. Landing from a jump with significant momentum, athletes increase stopping time by bending knees, ankles, hips; because impulse (FŒît) is constant for given landing, increasing time decreases average impact force on joints/tissues. This "cushioning" dissipates force safely, reducing acute injury risk.
