Quick reference: key terms

Mastering specialist vocabulary is foundational for analyzing and applying psychological skills concepts with precision in exam contexts.

üéØ Goal setting
Psychological States
A term used to describe the athlete's state of mind, including concepts like confidence and anxiety.
Psychological Skills
The more tangible and learnable steps taken by an athlete to improve their psychological state, such as goal setting or self-talk.
Outcome Goals
Goals that relate to the outcome of an event and usually involve a comparison with others, such as winning a race.
Performance Goals
Goals that relate to a specific product of performance and are relatively independent of others. They are self-referenced, such as swimming a certain race time.
Process Goals
Goals that relate to the processes that a performer will focus on during the performance, such as focusing on technique or strategy.
Task-Involved Goal
A type of achievement goal with an emphasis on demonstrating self-referenced competence and personal improvement.
Ego-Involved Goal
A type of achievement goal with a focus on demonstrating superior competence relative to others.
SMARTER Goals
An acronym for a goal-setting framework: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-based, Exciting, Review.
üß† Imagery
Imagery
"Using all the senses to recreate or create an experience in the mind," sometimes known as mental practice or mental simulation.
Lang's Bio-informational Theory
A theory proposing that the same neural pathways in the brain used in performing skills are activated during the use of vivid imagery.
PETTLEP Model
A seven-element framework for motor imagery-based interventions: Physical, Environment, Task, Timing, Learning, Emotion, Perspective.
Internal Perspective (Imagery)
Viewing the execution of a skill from the inside looking out, as if through your own eyes.
External Perspective (Imagery)
Viewing the execution of a skill from the outside, as if watching yourself on a recording.

Content summary

Psychological skills are integral to sporting excellence, providing athletes with mental tools to manage pressure, direct focus and enhance motivation‚Äîtrainable competencies like physical skills.

C.5.1 Goal setting

Goal setting is a cornerstone sport psychology technique, serving as a powerful mechanism for motivating athletes, directing effort and enhancing persistence.

Core concepts

  • Psychological States vs Skills: Vital to distinguish between psychological states (athlete's condition: confidence, anxiety) and psychological skills (tangible techniques: goal setting, used to improve those states).
  • Types of goals: Broadly categorized into outcome goals and learning-focused goals.

Goal types breakdown

Outcome Goals: Focus on event result and comparison with others (e.g., winning a championship).

Learning-Focused Goals: Prioritize knowledge and skill acquisition rather than outcome. Further broken down into:

  • Performance Goals: Focus on achieving self-referenced performance standard (e.g., achieving personal best time).
  • Process Goals: Focus on specific techniques and strategies required to execute skill (e.g., maintaining high elbow during swim stroke).

Achievement Motivation Theory

Proposes two primary goal orientations:

  • Task-involved: Motivation derived from personal mastery and improvement.
  • Ego-involved: Motivation derived from demonstrating superiority over others.

SMARTER framework

Popular acronym for setting effective goals:

  • Specific
  • Measurable
  • Achievable
  • Realistic
  • Time-based
  • Exciting
  • Review

Psychological Skills Training (PST) three-phase process

  1. General Education Phase: Initial phase clarifying what psychological skills are, how they can be trained, and what benefits they provide. Establishes expectations for athlete, coach and psychologist.
  2. Acquisition Phase: Focuses on learning and understanding specific psychological strategies and skills. Involves thorough needs analysis to tailor intervention to individual athlete.
  3. Practice Phase: Final phase aiming to automate skills through practice, systematically integrate them into performance situations, and teach athlete to self-regulate and adapt them over time.
The swimmer applying SMARTER: A swimmer with current 100m freestyle time of 58.5 seconds:
Specific: Lower my 100m freestyle PB to 57.9 seconds.
Measurable: Timed trials every Friday to track progress.
Achievable: 0.6-second improvement is challenging but realistic for the season.
Realistic: I have coaching support and training plan in place.
Time-based: Achieve this by final regional meet in 12 weeks.
Exciting: This time would qualify me for national championships.
Review: Review logbook and progress with coach every two weeks to adjust process goals.
The Goal-Setting Paradox: Research indicates elite athletes often report feeling less satisfied after achieving a very difficult, higher goal compared to an easier one. This paradox stems from the psychological 'comedown' or deflation an athlete experiences after prolonged intense striving, making the final achievement feel anticlimactic.

C.5.2 Imagery

Imagery is a powerful psychological skill where athletes use all senses to create or re-create mental experiences. This mental simulation bridges mind and body, allowing skill, strategy and emotional response practice without physical movement.

Core concepts

Paivio's (1985) Framework: Categorizes imagery into two primary functions, each with two applications:

  • Motivational: Used to regulate arousal and motivation (e.g., imagining winning). Can be Specific (goal-oriented) or General (arousal-related).
  • Cognitive: Used to rehearse skills and strategies (e.g., imagining perfect golf swing). Can be Specific (skills) or General (strategy).

Lang's Bio-informational Theory: Proposes imagery works because it activates the same neural pathways in the brain used during actual physical performance. The more vivid and multi-sensory the image, the more effective the stimulation.

PETTLEP Model: Seven-element framework

Designed to make imagery interventions more effective by making them functionally equivalent to real performance:

  1. Physical: The athlete should adopt the physical stance and wear the same attire as in actual performance.
  2. Environment: The imagery should take place in a location resembling the performance environment as closely as possible.
  3. Task: The athlete should focus on the specific demands and details of the task they are performing.
  4. Timing: The imagery should be performed in real-time, matching the actual duration of the skill or event.
  5. Learning: The imagery content should be adapted as the athlete's skills develop, ensuring it remains relevant and challenging.
  6. Emotion: The athlete should try to experience the same emotions and feelings that would be present during actual performance.
  7. Perspective: The athlete should choose the most effective visual perspective, typically internal (first-person) or external (third-person).
The judo athlete using PETTLEP: A judo athlete preparing for competition mentally rehearses:
They stand on a judo mat wearing full judo kit (Physical) in the competition hall (Environment). They mentally execute a specific throwing technique against opponent, focusing on grip and body position (Task). The rehearsal is done in real-time, matching throw speed (Timing). They incorporate a new adjustment to technique they've been working on (Learning). They try to feel pre-fight adrenaline and focus required (Emotion). They visualize the throw from their own eyes, feeling movement and balance (Perspective).
Internal vs External Perspective: Internal perspective means seeing performance through your own eyes, as if you are actually doing it‚Äîoften better for feeling kinesthetic sense of movement. External perspective is like watching yourself on video from the outside‚Äîuseful for seeing aesthetic quality and overall form of execution.

Question 7 4 MARKS
The goal-setting paradox
In the context of the goal-setting paradox, discuss why an elite athlete might feel less satisfied after achieving a very difficult goal.
üìã Model answer
The goal-setting paradox suggests elite athletes can feel less satisfaction after achieving very difficult goals due to several interrelated factors.

Deflation after achievement: The achievement may be followed by feeling of deflation, where the end result feels anticlimactic after a long and intense period of focus and effort. [1 mark]

Process vs outcome satisfaction: Primary satisfaction for many elite athletes is derived from the process of striving and overcoming challenges, rather than the final outcome itself. Once the goal is met, this source of motivation and engagement disappears. [1 mark]

Pressure for higher goals: Achieving a major goal immediately raises pressure and expectation to set an even higher goal, preventing the athlete from fully enjoying current success. [1 mark]

Exhaustion factor: Immense psychological and physical investment required to reach difficult goal can lead to exhaustion, which may overshadow feelings of satisfaction upon completion. [1 mark]
Question 8 6 MARKS
PETTLEP model application
Explain how the PETTLEP model of imagery can help an athlete improve their performance.
üìã Model answer
The PETTLEP model helps improve performance by making imagery more realistic and functionally equivalent to actual performance, enhancing its effect on brain's neural pathways.

The Physical element requires athlete to be in correct stance and attire, which enhances kinesthetic feel of movement. [1 mark]

The Environment element, using actual performance venue or similar setting, provides realistic visual and auditory cues that make mental simulation more vivid. [1 mark]

The Task element ensures imagery mirrors specific attention demands of skill, helping athlete focus on relevant cues and decision-making. [1 mark]

By performing imagery in real Timing, athlete rehearses rhythm and pace of skill, crucial for fluent execution under pressure. [1 mark]

The Learning element ensures imagery content is adapted as athlete's skill develops, keeping mental practice relevant and challenging. Incorporating Emotion helps athlete practise managing feelings of anxiety or excitement, allowing arousal level regulation for optimal performance. [1 mark]

Finally, choosing correct Perspective (internal or external) allows athlete to focus on either kinesthetic feel of movement or technical form, tailoring imagery to specific needs. [1 mark]

Common errors

Psychology is a quick fix
A common misconception is that sport psychology interventions produce instant results. In reality, psychological skills, like physical skills, require systematic training, commitment and time to develop. The three-phase PST model (education, acquisition, practice) highlights this is a long-term learning process, not a rapid solution.
It is only pertinent for elite athletes
While elite athletes make extensive use of sport psychology, its principles are applicable to performers at all levels, from youth sport to recreational exercise. Goal setting, for example, can enhance motivation and enjoyment for anyone engaging in physical activity, not just professional competitors.
It is only for "problem" athletes
Sport psychology is not solely for addressing issues like low confidence or high anxiety. Its primary function is often performance enhancement, helping already successful athletes develop their mental skills to gain competitive edge and perform more consistently under pressure.
Believing "do your best" is an effective goal
While well-intentioned, "do your best" goals are non-specific and difficult to measure. Research consistently shows that setting specific, difficult but realistic goals leads to higher levels of performance. Vague instructions lack focus and clear benchmarks needed to mobilize effort effectively.
Confusing outcome and process goals
Students often overemphasize outcome goals (e.g., winning), which are not entirely within athlete's control and can increase anxiety. A correct understanding involves recognizing importance of process goals (e.g., focusing on technique), which are controllable and direct athlete's focus towards actions required for successful performance.
Psychology is not useful in a sport context
This misconception ignores overwhelming evidence that mental state plays critical role in performance. Research shows psychological factors can account for significant percentage of performance variance. Skills like imagery and goal setting have direct impact on motivation, focus and ability to execute skills under pressure.

Linking questions

How can imagery (C.5.2) be used to reduce the risk of overtraining (A.3.1)?
Thinking points: Overtraining involves excessive training loads and inadequate recovery, affecting mood and motivation. Imagery can manage these emotional/psychological responses. Imagery can be used for motor learning and skill development without adding physical load, thereby assisting in recovery. Effective imagery uses multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) and can help regulate arousal, emotion and physiological responses, potentially preventing injury. The PETTLEP model can assist in developing imagery that addresses specific performance contexts, including recovery and injury prevention scenarios.
To what extent can an athlete use training in psychological skills (C.5) to regulate their heart rate (C.4.1, A.1.1, A.1.2)?
Thinking points: Psychological skills can regulate physiological responses such as heart rate, often linked to anxiety and arousal. Athletes can use imagery and other psychological skills to lower or maintain heart rate in situations like pre-performance routines or free throws in basketball. The PETTLEP model can create imagery scenarios that help athlete practise emotional and physiological control. Concepts like biofeedback can be integrated with psychological skills to help athlete gain conscious control over physiological functions.
