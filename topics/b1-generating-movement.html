Quick reference: key terms

ü¶¥ Anatomical position & movement
Axial Skeleton
Comprises the bones of the head and trunk, serving mainly to protect vital organs such as the brain, heart and lungs.
Appendicular Skeleton
Consists of the bones of the limbs and their girdles, providing the primary structural basis for movement.
Sagittal Plane
A vertical plane that divides the body into right and left sections; movements such as flexion and extension occur within this plane.
Flexion
A movement that decreases the angle of a joint, bringing two adjacent body segments closer together.
üß¨ Connective tissues & joints
Tendon
A tough band of fibrous connective tissue that connects muscle to bone, transmitting muscle force to the skeleton.
Ligament
A short band of tough fibrous connective tissue connecting bones or cartilages at a joint, providing joint stability.
Synovial Joint
A freely movable joint characterized by a fluid-filled cavity separating the articulating bone surfaces.
üí™ Muscular function
Motor Unit
A single motor neuron and all the muscle fibres it innervates; it contracts as one functional unit according to the all‚Äëor‚Äënone principle.
Isotonic Contraction
A muscular contraction where tension is generated and the muscle changes length, either concentrically (shortening) or eccentrically (lengthening).
Agonist
The prime mover muscle that contracts to create the primary desired movement at a joint.
üìè Levers
Fulcrum
The fixed point about which a lever rotates, usually a joint in the human body.
Mechanical Advantage
The ratio of the effort arm to the load arm in a lever system, indicating how effectively force is amplified.

Content summary: generating movement

B.1.1 Anatomical position, planes and movement

This subtopic defines the standard reference position and the planes and axes used to describe movement precisely in sport and exercise.

Anatomical foundations

  • The skeleton is divided into the axial skeleton, which mainly protects vital organs, and the appendicular skeleton, which primarily enables movement.
  • Positional terminology such as superior/inferior, proximal/distal and anterior/posterior provides a standardized way to describe relative locations of body parts.
  • Movements occur in three main planes (sagittal, frontal, transverse) and about corresponding axes; terms like flexion, extension, abduction and adduction specify the joint actions occurring in each plane.
In a tennis serve, elbow and wrist flexion‚Äìextension occur in the sagittal plane, abduction of the serving arm occurs in the frontal plane, and trunk rotation to generate power takes place in the transverse plane. Using this terminology, a coach can break down the skill and give precise technical feedback.
Be prepared to label or describe movements in unfamiliar sport images. Practise naming the plane, axis and specific joint actions for skills across different sports.

B.1.2 Structure and function of connective tissues and joints

Movement depends on the interaction of bones, connective tissues and joints, which balance mobility with stability.

Connective tissues and joints

  • Bone, ligaments, cartilage, fascia and tendons have distinct structures that either stabilize joints or permit movement.
  • Joints are classified as fibrous (immovable), cartilaginous (slightly movable) and synovial (freely movable), each reflecting a different balance between stability and mobility.
  • Synovial joints, such as ball‚Äëand‚Äësocket and hinge joints, contain features like articular cartilage, synovial fluid and a capsule that allow extensive movement with low friction.
The knee joint acts as a synovial hinge joint: articular cartilage reduces friction, ligaments such as the ACL and PCL provide stability, and tendons like the quadriceps tendon transmit muscle force to move the tibia during running and jumping.
When comparing joint types, always link structure to function, for example how the deep socket and strong ligaments of the hip sacrifice range of motion in favour of stability.

B.1.3 Muscular function

Muscles are the active force generators that move and stabilise the skeleton via motor unit recruitment and different contraction types.

Motor units and contractions

  • A motor unit is a motor neuron and its muscle fibres; once threshold is reached, all fibres in that unit contract maximally according to the all‚Äëor‚Äënone principle.
  • Motor units vary by fibre type (Type I, IIa, IIx) and are recruited according to the size principle, with smaller fatigue‚Äëresistant units activated first as force demands increase.
  • Muscle contractions include isometric (no change in length), isotonic concentric (shortening), isotonic eccentric (lengthening under tension) and isokinetic (constant speed, specialized equipment).
  • Agonist and antagonist muscle pairs coordinate movement through reciprocal inhibition, where the antagonist relaxes as the agonist contracts.
In a squat, the quadriceps perform an eccentric contraction during the lowering phase to control descent, an isometric contraction when holding at the bottom, and a concentric contraction during the upward phase to extend the knees and stand.
In exam answers, always specify both the contraction type (concentric or eccentric) and whether the muscle is acting as agonist or antagonist in the particular movement described.

B.1.3 (HL) Sliding filament theory

Higher level students must explain how interactions within the sarcomere convert chemical energy from ATP into force and movement.

Sliding filament mechanism (HL)

  1. A nerve impulse reaches the neuromuscular junction, triggering calcium ion release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the muscle fibre.
  2. Calcium binds to troponin, causing tropomyosin to move away from active sites on actin and exposing them.
  3. Energized myosin heads attach to the exposed actin binding sites, forming cross‚Äëbridges.
  4. ATP hydrolysis powers the myosin heads to pivot, pulling actin towards the centre of the sarcomere in a power stroke and shortening the sarcomere.
  5. A new ATP molecule binds to myosin, causing it to detach from actin before being re‚Äëenergized to repeat the cycle while calcium and ATP remain available.
In a fatigued marathon runner, depleted ATP and disrupted calcium handling slow cross‚Äëbridge cycling and reduce the number of active cross‚Äëbridges, leading to reduced force production and sensations of muscular stiffness.
When explaining the sliding filament theory, explicitly mention the roles of calcium, ATP, actin, myosin, troponin and tropomyosin in a logical sequence.

B.1.4 Levers in movement and sport

The musculoskeletal system operates as a set of levers where bones are rigid bars, joints are fulcrums and muscles provide effort to move loads.

Lever classes and mechanical advantage

  • Levers are classified by the relative positions of fulcrum (F), effort (E) and load (L): Class 1 (E‚ÄëF‚ÄëL or L‚ÄëF‚ÄëE), Class 2 (F‚ÄëL‚ÄëE) and Class 3 (F‚ÄëE‚ÄëL).
  • Class 1 levers, such as nodding the head, can provide either a mechanical advantage or disadvantage depending on fulcrum position.
  • Class 2 levers, such as a calf raise, place the load between fulcrum and effort and always provide a mechanical advantage (>1) for force production.
  • Class 3 levers, such as the elbow during a bicep curl, place the effort between fulcrum and load, producing a mechanical disadvantage (<1) but allowing large, fast movements.
In rowing, the oar acts as a Class 1 external lever with the oarlock as fulcrum, the rower‚Äôs hands applying effort and the water resistance acting as load; adjusting grip and oar length changes the effective mechanical advantage.
For any lever question, first identify the fulcrum, effort and load, then state the lever class and whether it provides mechanical advantage or disadvantage.

Mathematical skills

For Topic B.1, the key quantitative skill is calculating mechanical advantage for lever systems in order to judge whether a lever favours force or speed.

Mechanical advantage = Length of effort arm √∑ Length of load arm

Worked example

A lifter performs a bicep curl with a 15 kg dumbbell. Effort is applied 4 cm from the elbow (fulcrum) and the load is 28 cm from the fulcrum.

  1. Effort arm = 4 cm; load arm = 28 cm.
  2. MA = 4 √∑ 28.
  3. MA = 0.143 (no units).

An MA less than 1 indicates mechanical disadvantage, typical of Class 3 levers that prioritise speed and range of motion.

Common errors

Tendons vs ligaments
Tendons connect muscle to bone and transmit force, whereas ligaments connect bone to bone and stabilise joints; confusing them leads to inaccurate explanations of injuries.
Agonist and antagonist roles
Muscle roles depend on the specific movement; identify the joint action first, then decide which muscle is the agonist and which is the antagonist for that action.
Eccentric contraction = relaxation?
An eccentric contraction involves active tension while the muscle lengthens under load, acting as a braking mechanism rather than a relaxed state.
Lever class confusion
Use the F‚ÄëL‚ÄëE mnemonic for what is in the middle: Fulcrum (Class 1), Load (Class 2), Effort (Class 3) to classify levers correctly.
Flexion direction assumptions
Flexion always means decreasing joint angle, not necessarily moving forwards; for example, knee flexion moves the lower leg backwards.
All‚Äëor‚Äënone applied to whole muscle
The all‚Äëor‚Äënone principle applies to individual motor units, not entire muscles; whole‚Äëmuscle force is graded by recruiting different numbers and types of motor units.

Linking questions

How does malnutrition affect muscular function? (B.1.3 and A.2.2)
Inadequate carbohydrate and fat intake limits ATP resynthesis needed for cross‚Äëbridge cycling, while deficiencies in micronutrients such as calcium and electrolytes disrupt excitation‚Äìcontraction coupling and nerve transmission, reducing force output and increasing fatigue.
Can precise anatomical language speed up skill learning? (B.1.1 and C.2.1)
During the cognitive stage of motor learning, instructions like ‚Äúincrease knee flexion‚Äù or ‚Äúfully extend the elbow‚Äù give clear, unambiguous cues that help learners build accurate movement schemas and progress more quickly to the associative stage.
How can changing external levers affect skill acquisition? (B.1.4 and C.2.1)
Modifying external levers, such as using a shorter, lighter tennis racket, reduces required torque and rotational inertia so learners can focus on technique; as skill improves, lever length can be increased to match performance demands.
