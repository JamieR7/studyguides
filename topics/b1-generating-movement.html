Quick Reference Key Terms

Mastering the key terminology of any scientific discipline is the first and most critical step toward academic success. For the IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science (SEHS) exams, a strong command of the vocabulary is the foundation for building clear, precise, and high-scoring responses. These terms are the building blocks of your understanding, allowing you to communicate complex physiological and biomechanical concepts with confidence and accuracy. The following list compiles essential terms from Topic B.1. While the IB syllabus does not provide a formal glossary for these, the definitions provided here are standard in the field and align with the expectations of the curriculum. Knowing the terms is the first step; the next is understanding how they are assessed.

Skeletal System & Anatomy
Axial Skeleton
The part of the skeleton that consists of the bones of the head and trunk of a vertebrate.
Appendicular Skeleton
The portion of the skeleton of vertebrates consisting of the bones that support the appendages.
Anatomical Position
A standardized reference position for the body, where it is erect, with feet slightly apart, palms facing forward, and thumbs pointing away from the body.
Anatomical Planes
Imaginary flat surfaces (sagittal, frontal, transverse) that pass through the body to describe locations and movements.
Joints & Connective Tissues
Connective Tissues
Tissues that support, connect, or separate different types of tissues and organs in the body, including bone, ligaments, cartilage, fascia, and tendons.
Articulations (Joints)
The junctions between two or more bones. They are classified by structure as fibrous, cartilaginous, or synovial.
Synovial Joints
The most common type of joint in the body, characterized by a joint cavity filled with synovial fluid, which allows for a wide range of movement.
Muscular System
Motor Unit
A single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates.
All-or-None Principle
A principle stating that the strength by which a nerve or muscle fibre responds to a stimulus is independent of the strength of the stimulus. If the stimulus exceeds the threshold potential, the nerve or muscle fibre will give a complete response; otherwise, there is no response.
Reciprocal Inhibition
The process where muscles on one side of a joint relax to accommodate the contraction of muscles on the other side of that joint.
Isotonic Contraction
A muscle contraction that produces movement at a joint; it can be concentric (muscle shortens) or eccentric (muscle lengthens).
Isometric Contraction
A muscle contraction in which the muscle generates force but does not change in length.
Sliding Filament Theory
The theory explaining the mechanism of muscle contraction based on muscle proteins (actin and myosin) sliding past each other to generate movement.
Levers
Lever
A rigid bar that pivots around a fixed point (the fulcrum) and is used to transmit force.
Mechanical Advantage
The ratio of the output force (load) to the input force (effort) in a lever system.

Content Summary: B.1 Generating Movement in the Body

This foundational topic in biomechanics explores how the human body is structured to create purposeful and powerful movement. It delves into the intricate relationship between the skeletal framework, the joints that allow for motion, and the muscular system that provides the force. By understanding these components, we can begin to analyze and appreciate the complexity of human performance.

B.1.1 Anatomical position, planes and movement

Overview

This sub-section establishes the universal language for describing the human body and its motion. It introduces the anatomical position as a standard reference point, and the planes and axes that define the direction of movement. Mastering this terminology is essential for accurately describing and analyzing any physical activity.

Core Concepts

  • The human skeleton is divided into the axial (skull, vertebral column, ribs) and appendicular (limbs and girdles) components, each with distinct functions.
  • Standard positional terminology (e.g., superior/inferior, proximal/distal, anterior/posterior) is used to describe the relative locations of body parts.
  • Movement terms (e.g., flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, rotation) describe actions that occur within specific anatomical planes (sagittal, frontal, transverse) and around corresponding axes.
  • (HL Only) Anthropometry, the measurement of body segments and proportions, is applied to the ergonomic design of equipment to improve efficiency and performance.
Sporting Application
A gymnastics coach uses precise anatomical terms like "flexion at the hip in the sagittal plane" and "abduction of the shoulder in the frontal plane" to give a floor routine performer clear and unambiguous feedback to refine their technique.

B.1.2 Structure and function of connective tissues and joints

Overview

Movement is only possible because of the way our bones are connected. This sub-section examines the connective tissues that provide stability (ligaments, bone) and permit movement (cartilage, tendons), as well as the different types of joints, or articulations. The focus is on how the structure of each joint directly relates to its specific function and range of motion.

Core Concepts

  • Connective tissues like bone, ligaments, cartilage, fascia, and tendons have distinct structures that relate to their functions of increasing stability and permitting movement.
  • The three main types of articulations‚Äîfibrous (immovable), cartilaginous (slightly movable), and synovial (freely movable)‚Äîhave different structural characteristics.
  • Synovial joints are further classified by their shape (e.g., hinge, ball and socket), which dictates the amount of stability and the specific types of movement they allow.
Sporting Application
In tennis, the synovial ball and socket joint of the shoulder allows for circumduction, a wide range of motion necessary for a powerful serve, while the hinge joint of the elbow primarily allows for flexion and extension.

B.1.3 Muscular function

Overview

Muscles are the engines of the body, converting chemical energy into mechanical force to produce movement. This section covers how muscles contract, the different types of contractions used for stability and motion, and how motor units are recruited to control the force of these contractions.

Core Concepts

  • Muscles generate force through different types of contractions: isometric (static), isotonic concentric (shortening), and isotonic eccentric (lengthening).
  • A motor unit, consisting of a motor neuron and the muscle fibers it innervates, contracts based on the all-or-none principle.
  • Muscle force is graded by recruiting different types of motor units (Type I, IIa, IIx) depending on the intensity and duration of the activity.
  • Muscles typically work in antagonistic pairs (agonist and antagonist) through reciprocal inhibition to produce smooth, controlled movements.
  • (HL Only) The sliding filament theory describes how muscle contraction occurs at a microscopic level through the interaction of actin and myosin proteins, a process requiring calcium and ATP.
Sporting Application
During a bicep curl, the biceps brachii performs an isotonic concentric contraction on the way up and an isotonic eccentric contraction on the way down, while the triceps brachii acts as the antagonist.

B.1.4 Levers in movement and sport

Overview

The musculoskeletal system functions as a series of levers, allowing us to multiply force or speed to perform athletic movements. This section explores the three classes of levers and how their components‚Äîfulcrum, effort, and load‚Äîare arranged both within the human body and in sporting equipment to create a mechanical advantage or disadvantage.

Core Concepts

  • Movement is created by three classes of levers, defined by the relative positions of the effort, fulcrum, and load.
  • The arrangement of a lever determines its mechanical advantage (force multiplication) or mechanical disadvantage (speed/range of motion multiplication).
  • Levers inside the body (e.g., the elbow joint during a throw) create movement, while levers outside the body (e.g., a tennis racket, a rowing oar) can be used to enhance performance.
Sporting Application
In rowing, the oar acts as a lever to propel the boat. The rower's hands provide the effort, the oarlock is the fulcrum, and the resistance of the water on the oar blade is the load.
