Quick Reference: Key Terms

Mastering the precise language of Sport, Exercise, and Health Science is fundamental to achieving excellence in your examinations. These terms are conceptual tools that allow you to articulate complex physiological principles with clarity and precision.

üí™ Training Principles
Progressive Overload
The principle of gradually increasing the training stress (frequency, intensity, or duration) placed on the body to stimulate further adaptation and improvement.
Specificity
The principle that training adaptations are specific to the type of exercise performed, the muscle groups involved, and the energy systems utilized.
Reversibility
The principle that fitness gains are lost when training is stopped or significantly reduced; also known as detraining.
Periodization
The structured planning of training into distinct time blocks (macrocycles, mesocycles, and microcycles), each with a specific goal, to optimize performance and prevent overtraining.
Non-functional Overreaching
An excessive training load leading to a temporary performance decrement, from which it can take several weeks or months to recover.
Overtraining Syndrome
A state of prolonged performance decrement resulting from an excessive accumulation of training and non-training stress, often taking several months or even years to resolve.
üèÉ Health & Adaptation
Energy Balance
The relationship between energy intake (from food and drink) and energy consumption (for bodily functions and physical activity), which determines whether body weight is maintained, gained, or lost.
Osteoporosis
A medical condition where bones become brittle and fragile from loss of tissue, typically as a result of hormonal changes, or deficiency of calcium or vitamin D, the risk of which can be reduced by an active lifestyle.
Hypertension
Abnormally high blood pressure, a condition whose risk can be reduced through regular physical activity.
üòì Fatigue & Recovery HL ONLY
Fatigue
A complex phenomenon originating at different levels of the motor or energy pathway that results in an inability to maintain a desired intensity of exercise.
Acidosis
A condition characterized by an increased concentration of hydrogen ions (and thus a lower pH) in the blood and muscle tissue, often associated with high-intensity exercise and contributing to fatigue.
Hyperthermia
An elevated core body temperature that can impair cardiovascular function and lead to performance decrements and fatigue, particularly during exercise in hot environments.
Recovery
The multifaceted process of restoring physiological and psychological function to pre-exercise levels, which is critical for adaptation and subsequent performance.

Comprehensive Content Summary

A.3.1 Qualities of Training

Effective training is not a matter of chance; it is a science built on established principles. Understanding these principles is critical for designing programmes that achieve the desired adaptations.

üîë Key Training Principles

  • Specificity: Adaptations are specific to the demands of the training. A marathon runner must train the aerobic system, while a powerlifter must train the neuromuscular system for maximal force production.
  • Progressive Overload: To continue improving, the body must be subjected to a gradually increasing load through frequency (how often), intensity (how hard), or duration (how long).
  • Recovery: When adaptations occur. The body needs adequate time to repair and strengthen itself between training sessions.
  • Variety: Varying the training routine prevents boredom, maintains motivation, and stimulates new adaptations.
  • Reversibility: When training ceases, physiological adaptations and performance gains are lost over time.

üìÖ The Mechanism of Periodization

  • Macrocycle: The longest-term plan, typically covering a full year or an entire season (e.g., an Olympic cycle).
  • Mesocycle: A medium-term training block within the macrocycle, usually lasting several weeks to a few months. Each has a specific focus.
  • Microcycle: The shortest training block, typically a week long, with structured training sessions and recovery days.

A swimming coach designing a 12-week mesocycle for a 1500m freestyle specialist:

Weeks 1-4 (General Preparation): High-volume, low-intensity swimming to build aerobic base
Weeks 5-8 (Specific Preparation): Increased intensity, slightly reduced volume with race-pace intervals
Weeks 9-11 (Pre-Competition): High-intensity, race-specific training with reduced volume
Week 12 (Taper): Drastically reduced volume and intensity for full recovery and peak performance
Critical Distinction: Non-functional overreaching (NFO) involves severe, short-term performance decline with recovery possible in weeks to months. Overtraining syndrome (OTS) is much more severe and chronic, where performance plummets and may not recover even with extended rest (months to years), often with systemic physiological and psychological symptoms.

A.3.2 Benefits to Health of Being Active

An active lifestyle is a cornerstone of physical and mental well-being, directly influencing the body's ability to maintain homeostasis and function optimally.

üíö Key Health Benefits

  • Energy Balance: Regular physical activity increases energy expenditure, critical for maintaining healthy body weight and preventing obesity.
  • Reduced Disease Risk: Significantly lowers risk of chronic diseases:
    • Osteoporosis: Weight-bearing activity stimulates bone density
    • Obesity: Helps maintain negative energy balance
    • Hypertension: Lowers resting blood pressure
    • Cardiovascular Diseases: Improves heart function and circulation
    • Type 2 Diabetes: Enhances insulin sensitivity
Prescribing Exercise for Specific Populations (HL): When prescribing exercise for health, careful consideration must be given to unique needs. Children/adolescents need focus on motor skill development; older adults require programs to improve balance and maintain independence; pregnant individuals need modifications to avoid high-risk activities while maintaining cardiovascular health.

An exercise programme for an older adult to reduce osteoporosis risk:

3 days/week: Brisk walking for 30 minutes (weight-bearing cardiovascular)
2 days/week: Resistance exercises using light weights (squats, lunges, overhead press)
Daily: Balance exercises like Tai Chi or single-leg stands to reduce fall risk

A.3.3 Fatigue and Recovery HL ONLY

Fatigue is a complex physiological event with multiple origins, limiting an athlete's ability to sustain performance. Recovery is equally complex, involving physiological and psychological restoration.

‚ö° Sources of Fatigue

  • Fuel Depletion: PCr depletion (short, high-intensity) and glycogen depletion (prolonged endurance)
  • Metabolic By-products: Acidosis (H‚Å∫ accumulation) and inorganic phosphate accumulation interfere with muscle contraction
  • Thermoregulation Issues: Dehydration and hyperthermia strain cardiovascular system and impair CNS function
  • Ion Imbalances: Disrupted Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ gradients reduce muscle membrane excitability

üîÑ Recovery Indicators

  • Physiological: Restoration of normal heart rate, blood pressure, blood lactate levels
  • Symptomatic: Reduction in muscle soreness (DOMS), feeling of physical restoration
  • Psychological: Restored motivation, positive mood, feeling of preparedness
Recovery Strategies Evaluation:
Nutritional: Carbohydrate + protein timing post-exercise (strong evidence); creatine supplementation (effective for high-intensity recovery)
Physical: Cold water immersion (mixed evidence, may reduce perceived soreness); compression garments (inconsistent evidence, possible placebo); myofascial release/foam rolling (may reduce perception of soreness, limited physiological benefit)
Sleep: Arguably the most critical recovery strategy. During sleep, the body releases anabolic hormones like growth hormone, essential for tissue repair and adaptation. Travel, especially across time zones, disrupts circadian rhythms and can severely compromise sleep quality and recovery.

Question 1
Which training principle is best demonstrated when a weightlifter increases the weight they are lifting from 80 kg to 85 kg?
  • A. Specificity
  • B. Reversibility
  • C. Progressive Overload
  • D. Variety
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: Progressive overload is the gradual increase of stress placed upon the body during exercise. By increasing the weight from 80 kg to 85 kg, the lifter is increasing the intensity, which is a direct application of this principle to stimulate further strength adaptations.

Why A is wrong: Specificity relates to the type of exercise being relevant to the desired adaptation (e.g., lifting weights to get stronger). While relevant, the act of increasing the weight itself is an example of overload.

Why B is wrong: Reversibility describes the loss of fitness when training is stopped, which is not depicted in this scenario.

Why D is wrong: Variety involves changing the exercises or training methods to avoid boredom and stimulate new adaptations. This scenario shows an increase in load for the same exercise, not a change in the exercise itself.
Question 2
An athlete experiences a significant decline in performance and mood that persists for several months despite reducing their training load. This condition is best described as:
  • A. Non-functional overreaching
  • B. Reversibility
  • C. Acute fatigue
  • D. Overtraining syndrome
‚úÖ Correct Answer: D
Why D is correct: Overtraining syndrome is a chronic condition characterized by a long-term performance decrement, mood disturbances, and other physiological issues that are not resolved by short periods of rest. The multi-month duration described is the key indicator of OTS.

Why A is wrong: Non-functional overreaching also involves a performance decline but is typically resolved within several weeks to a couple of months of rest. The extended timeline points towards the more severe OTS.

Why B is wrong: Reversibility is the planned or unplanned loss of training adaptations due to a lack of stimulus, not a condition caused by excessive training stress.

Why C is wrong: Acute fatigue is the temporary tiredness experienced during and immediately after a single exercise session, which recovers quickly.
Question 3 HL ONLY
During a 400-meter sprint, which of the following is a primary contributor to peripheral fatigue?
  • A. Depletion of liver glycogen stores
  • B. Accumulation of hydrogen ions
  • C. Dehydration
  • D. Depletion of fat stores
‚úÖ Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: A 400-meter sprint is a high-intensity event that relies heavily on anaerobic glycolysis for energy. This process produces lactic acid, which dissociates into lactate and hydrogen ions (H‚Å∫). The accumulation of H‚Å∫ lowers muscle pH (acidosis), which inhibits key glycolytic enzymes and interferes with calcium's ability to bind to troponin, thus impairing muscle contraction and causing fatigue.

Why A is wrong: Liver glycogen depletion is a major factor in fatigue during prolonged, multi-hour endurance events, not a short sprint lasting less than a minute.

Why C is wrong: Significant dehydration takes longer to develop and is a factor in longer-duration events, particularly in the heat. It is not a primary cause of fatigue in a 400m sprint.

Why D is wrong: The body has vast fat stores that are not a limiting factor for exercise of any duration. The rate of energy release from fat is too slow for a 400m sprint.
Question 4 HL ONLY
Which of the following describes a key consideration when prescribing exercise for older adults?
  • A. Maximizing training volume to accelerate adaptation
  • B. Focusing primarily on high-intensity interval training
  • C. Incorporating exercises that challenge balance to reduce fall risk
  • D. Prioritizing sport-specific skills over general fitness
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: A major health risk for older adults is falling, which can lead to fractures and a loss of independence. Therefore, exercise programmes for this population should explicitly include components that improve balance and proprioception, alongside strength and cardiovascular fitness.

Why A is wrong: Older adults have a reduced capacity for recovery, so maximizing training volume is inappropriate and would likely lead to injury or overtraining. Progression must be gradual.

Why B is wrong: While HIIT can be beneficial, it is an advanced modality. A programme for older adults, especially beginners, should first establish a base of fitness with moderate-intensity exercise.

Why D is wrong: For the general health and well-being of older adults, the priority is maintaining functional independence and reducing disease risk through general fitness, not developing sport-specific skills.

Question 2 HL ONLY 4 MARKS
Physiological Factors Contributing to Marathon Fatigue
Explain four distinct physiological factors that can contribute to the onset of fatigue during a marathon.
üìã Model Answer:
Four physiological factors contributing to fatigue during a marathon are:

1. Muscle and Liver Glycogen Depletion: The body has limited carbohydrate stores. As these are used up over the course of the marathon, the ability to produce ATP at the required rate diminishes, forcing the athlete to slow down. [1 mark]

2. Dehydration: Significant fluid loss through sweating reduces total blood volume. This increases cardiovascular strain, as the heart must beat faster to maintain cardiac output, and impairs the body's ability to transport oxygen and nutrients. [1 mark]

3. Hyperthermia: Prolonged exercise generates a large amount of metabolic heat. If this heat cannot be dissipated effectively, core body temperature rises, which can impair central nervous system function and enzyme activity, leading to reduced performance and central fatigue. [1 mark]

4. Accumulation of Metabolic By-products: While less of a factor than in sprinting, the accumulation of inorganic phosphate (Pi) from ATP breakdown can interfere with calcium release and cross-bridge cycling within muscle fibres, contributing to peripheral fatigue over the long duration of the race. [1 mark]

Common Errors & Misconceptions

‚ùå Error: Confusing Overtraining with Non-Functional Overreaching
Non-functional overreaching (NFO) is severe fatigue that can be resolved with several weeks or months of recovery. Overtraining syndrome (OTS) is much more severe and chronic, where performance decline is prolonged (many months or years) and accompanied by significant physiological and psychological disturbances. The key distinction is duration, severity, and recovery time.
‚ùå Error: Believing 'More is Always Better' Regarding Training Load
This ignores the principles of recovery and reversibility. The body adapts and gets stronger during rest periods, not during training itself. Continuously increasing training load without adequate recovery leads to NFO or OTS. A well-designed, periodized plan with strategic recovery is far more effective.
‚ùå Error: Applying a Single Recovery Strategy Universally (HL)
The effectiveness of a recovery strategy is highly specific to the type of exercise performed, the individual athlete, and the desired outcome. For example, while cold water immersion might reduce perceived soreness, nutritional strategies focusing on glycogen and protein resynthesis may be more critical for physiological recovery. There is no single "best" recovery method.
‚ùå Error: Viewing Fatigue as Only a Peripheral (Muscular) Event (HL)
Fatigue is often simplified to "the muscles getting tired." In reality, it is complex with both peripheral (e.g., acidosis, glycogen depletion in muscle) and central (e.g., altered neurotransmitter levels in brain, hyperthermia affecting CNS) origins. High-scoring answers recognize fatigue can originate anywhere from the brain to the muscle fibre.
‚ùå Error: Misinterpreting Energy Balance as Simply 'Calories In vs. Calories Out'
While the equation is simple, the concept is complex. "Energy consumption" includes basal metabolic rate, thermic effect of food, and non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT), not just exercise. Furthermore, the quality of calories (macronutrient and micronutrient content) is as important for health as the quantity.
‚ùå Error: Assuming All Benefits of Activity Require High Intensity
Regular, moderate-intensity physical activity provides significant protection against numerous chronic diseases like hypertension and type 2 diabetes. The key for general health is consistency and achieving sufficient total volume of activity, not necessarily high intensity.

Making Connections Across Topics

How does A.3.1 Qualities of Training link to B.3 Injury?
The principles of training from Topic A.3.1 are fundamental to injury prevention strategies. Progressive overload is critical‚Äîif training load is increased too rapidly without allowing for adaptation, it can cause cumulative trauma and overuse injuries. Prehabilitation exercises are a direct application of the specificity principle, targeting weaknesses to prevent future injuries. Incorporating adequate recovery into a periodized plan is perhaps the most important intervention for preventing injuries caused by overtraining.
How does A.3.3 Fatigue and Recovery (HL) link to C.3 Motivation?
The relationship is bidirectional. High autonomous motivation may lead an athlete to push through early signs of fatigue or adhere more strictly to recovery protocols. Conversely, the physiological state of fatigue can directly impact motivation. An athlete in a state of non-functional overreaching or overtraining syndrome often experiences a significant drop in motivation and mood, making it psychologically difficult to continue training. The perception of fatigue itself is a psychological construct that interacts with an athlete's motivational climate and goal orientation.
How does A.3.2 Benefits to health of being active link to A.2 Fuelling?
These topics are intrinsically linked through the concept of energy balance. A.3.2 explains that physical activity increases energy expenditure, which helps prevent obesity. A.2 provides the other side: energy intake through macronutrients. An active lifestyle is most beneficial for health when supported by appropriate fuelling. Furthermore, A.2 introduces Low Energy Availability (LEA) and RED-S‚Äîa critical link where an individual may be highly active but under-fuelled, leading to negative health consequences that negate the benefits of their activity.
