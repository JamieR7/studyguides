1. Quick Reference Key Terms

In sports, exercise, and health science, the precise use of terminology is crucial. Clear definitions allow for accurate communication and a deeper understanding of how the body responds and adapts to the stresses of training. Mastering these key terms is a foundational step in building your expertise. The following fundamental terms for Topic A.3 are grouped by concept to aid your learning.

Training Principles
Specificity
A training principle that directs programme design to be relevant to the specific demands of the sport or activity.
Progressive Overload
The principle of gradually increasing the frequency, intensity, or duration of training to stimulate adaptation.
Recovery
A training principle, also known as the rest principle, that is essential for adaptation and preventing overtraining.
Variety
A training principle that involves changing training activities to maintain motivation and provide a different physiological stimulus.
Reversibility
The training principle stating that fitness adaptations are lost when training is stopped or significantly reduced.
Periodization
The structuring of a training programme into distinct time periods, known as macrocycles, mesocycles, and microcycles.
Health & Adaptation
Adaptive Responses
The physiological changes that occur within the body as a result of a consistent training stimulus.
Non-functional Overreaching
A state of excessive fatigue and performance decline resulting from a poorly managed training programme, which requires an extended period of recovery.
Overtraining
A chronic maladaptive state resulting from an imbalance between training stress and recovery, characterized by severe physiological and psychological symptoms.
Energy Balance
The relationship between energy intake (from food), energy consumption (from metabolic processes and activity), and energy storage.
Fatigue & Recovery (HL Only)
Fatigue
A complex state originating from various levels of the motor or energy pathway that impairs performance.
Hyperthermia
An elevated core body temperature that can impact the cardiovascular system and lead to performance impairment.

4. Content Summary

A.3.1 Qualities of training

Well-designed training programmes are the strategic foundation upon which athletic performance and health improvements are built. They are not random assortments of exercises but are guided by established scientific principles. Understanding these principles allows athletes and coaches to manipulate training variables to optimize positive physiological adaptations while minimizing the risk of injury, fatigue, and maladaptation.

Key Concepts:
  • Training programmes are guided by core principles: specificity, progressive overload, recovery, variety, and reversibility (p.XX).
  • Periodization is the method of structuring training into long-term (macrocycles), medium-term (mesocycles), and short-term (microcycles) plans to manage training load for peak performance (p.XX).
  • Training stimulates adaptive responses, but these are highly individual due to factors like genetics, age, and hormonal status (p.XX).
  • Poorly designed programmes that neglect recovery can lead to non-functional overreaching or, more seriously, overtraining syndrome (p.XX).

Mechanism Explained: Progressive Overload

  1. Apply Stimulus: The body is exposed to a training stress (e.g., lifting a certain weight, running a certain distance) that is greater than what it is accustomed to.
  2. Fatigue & Recovery: The immediate response is fatigue. During the subsequent recovery period, the body repairs itself.
  3. Adaptation (Supercompensation): The body adapts to a level slightly higher than its previous state to be better prepared for the next time it encounters that same stress.
  4. Increase Stimulus: To continue eliciting adaptation, the training stress must be gradually increased by manipulating frequency (how often), intensity (how hard), or duration (how long).
Sporting Example: A marathon runner uses periodization to prepare for a major race. Their entire year-long training plan is the macrocycle. This macrocycle is divided into distinct mesocycles, each with a specific focus:
  • General Preparation Mesocycle: Focus is on building an aerobic base with high-volume, lower-intensity running.
  • Specific Preparation Mesocycle: Volume decreases slightly while intensity increases, incorporating race-pace runs and interval training specific to the demands of the marathon.
  • Competition Mesocycle: Includes a "taper" where training volume is significantly reduced in the final weeks (microcycles) to allow for full recovery and supercompensation, ensuring the runner is in peak condition on race day.

Important Note:

Applying these principles enhances athletic performance, but the fundamental act of being physically active also confers profound benefits to general health.

A.3.2 Benefits to health of being active

Physical activity is a cornerstone of public health, acting as a powerful tool for both the prevention and management of chronic disease. An active lifestyle is not merely about athletic achievement; it is fundamental to human well-being. This section explores the physiological mechanisms through which regular exercise mitigates disease risk, supports healthy bodily function, and contributes to a higher quality of life.

Key Concepts:
  • Maintaining a healthy energy balance‚Äîwhere energy intake matches energy expenditure‚Äîis crucial for preventing obesity (p.XX).
  • Physical activity has positive effects on the muscular and immune systems, enhancing function and resilience (p.XX).
  • An active lifestyle significantly reduces the risk of developing chronic diseases, including osteoporosis, obesity, hypertension, cardiovascular diseases, and type 2 diabetes (p.XX).
  • HL Only: Prescribing exercise for specific populations like children, older adults, or pregnant individuals requires careful consideration of their unique physiological needs and safety (p.XX).

Mechanism Explained: Reducing Type 2 Diabetes Risk

  1. Energy Expenditure: Regular physical activity increases daily energy consumption, which helps to maintain a healthy body weight and prevent the accumulation of excess body fat, a primary risk factor for type 2 diabetes.
  2. Improved Insulin Sensitivity: Exercise stimulates muscle cells to become more sensitive to insulin.
  3. Enhanced Glucose Uptake: This increased sensitivity allows the muscles to more effectively take up glucose from the blood for energy, both during and after exercise. This action helps to regulate blood sugar levels and reduces the body's overall demand for insulin.
Real-World Example: An older adult who incorporates a structured walking program (e.g., 30 minutes, 5 days a week) can achieve significant health benefits. The weight-bearing nature of walking helps to maintain bone density, reducing the risk of osteoporosis. The cardiovascular demands of brisk walking improve heart health and blood vessel elasticity, which can lower or control hypertension.
Pro Tip:
HL Note:

While an active lifestyle provides numerous benefits, high-level training introduces the challenges of fatigue, making the process of recovery essential for sustained adaptation and performance.

A.3.3 Fatigue and recovery (HL Only)

For the competitive athlete, fatigue is an inevitable consequence of pushing physiological limits, while recovery is the critical process that allows the body to repair, adapt, and become stronger. Fatigue is not a simple phenomenon but a complex interplay of central and peripheral factors that limit performance. Understanding the multifaceted nature of fatigue and the science behind effective recovery is paramount for sustainable athletic progress and avoiding maladaptation.

Key Concepts:
  • Sources of fatigue are multi-factorial and include fuel depletion (phosphocreatine, glycogen), metabolic by-products (acidosis, inorganic phosphate), ion variations, and thermoregulatory stress (dehydration and hyperthermia) (p.XX).
  • Recovery is assessed through a combination of physiological (e.g., blood lactate), symptomatic (e.g., muscle soreness), and psychological (e.g., readiness) indicators (p.XX).
  • Recovery can be accelerated through nutritional strategies (e.g., protein/carb timing, creatine, polyphenols), specific recovery techniques (e.g., compression, thermotherapy), and optimizing sleep quality and quantity (p.XX).

Mechanism Explained: Dehydration, Hyperthermia, and Cardiovascular Drift

  1. Fluid Loss: During prolonged exercise, the body sweats to cool itself, leading to a loss of fluid from the blood plasma.
  2. Reduced Blood Volume: This dehydration causes a decrease in total blood volume, which in turn reduces the amount of blood returning to the heart (venous return) and the amount of blood the heart can pump with each beat (stroke volume).
  3. Increased Heart Rate: To maintain the necessary blood flow to working muscles (cardiac output), the heart rate must increase. This gradual upward "drift" of heart rate during steady-state exercise is known as cardiovascular drift.
  4. Performance Impairment: This process, often exacerbated by a rising core body temperature (hyperthermia), places significant strain on the cardiovascular system, leading to increased perceived exertion and impaired endurance performance.
Sporting Example: A professional cyclist competing in a multi-day stage race employs a multi-faceted recovery protocol. Immediately after a stage, they consume a recovery drink with a precise ratio of carbohydrates and protein to begin replenishing glycogen and repairing muscle. In the evening, they may use compression garments to reduce muscle soreness, have a team chef prepare a nutrient-dense meal, and prioritize at least eight hours of high-quality sleep in a cool, dark environment to maximize hormonal and cellular repair processes.

Formula: Percentage Change = ((New Value - Original Value) / Original Value) * 100

Worked Example:

7. Common Errors

Confusing Progressive Overload with Simply 'Training Harder'
A common mistake is thinking that progressive overload just means increasing intensity every session. It is a systematic, planned increase in frequency, intensity, or duration over a longer period (a mesocycle), balanced with recovery.
Ignoring the Principle of Reversibility
Students often forget that adaptations are not permanent. When training stops or is significantly reduced, fitness gains (e.g., increased VO2 max, muscular strength) will decline over time.
Mistaking Overtraining for Overreaching
Non-functional overreaching is a short-term state of excessive fatigue that can be reversed with a few days or weeks of recovery. Overtraining is a chronic syndrome with more severe physiological and psychological symptoms that can take months to resolve.
Assuming 'Energy Balance' is a Simple Static Equation
Students may treat energy balance as a simple 'calories in = calories out' calculation. It is a dynamic process where the type of macronutrient intake and expenditure (e.g., aerobic vs. resistance training) has different effects on body composition and hormonal responses.
(HL Only) Attributing All Fatigue to a Single Cause
Fatigue is multi-factorial. It's an error to blame it solely on 'lactic acid' (acidosis). Depending on the activity, fatigue can stem from phosphocreatine depletion, glycogen depletion, neural factors, or hyperthermia.
(HL Only) Viewing Recovery as Only Passive Rest
Students may think recovery is just 'not training'. Effective recovery involves active strategies, such as proper nutrition (protein/carb timing), sleep hygiene, and techniques like thermotherapy, to accelerate physiological repair processes.

Recognizing these distinctions is key, as is the ability to see how concepts from this topic connect to the broader SEHS syllabus.
