Quick reference: key terms

In the 2026 IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science (SEHS) syllabus, the mastery of precise terminology is the cornerstone of securing Assessment Objective 1 (AO1) marks. Examiners frequently note that candidates lose easy marks by providing vague descriptions rather than the specific, technical definitions required by the markscheme. These definitions serve as the prerequisite for the higher-level analysis (AO3) demanded in Paper 2.

Personality & Traits
Personality
Individual differences in characteristic patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving, typically understood as an interaction between genetic traits and the environment (p.45).
Trait-based approach
A perspective that presents personality traits as relatively enduring and stable characteristics across different situations (p.45).
The Big Five
A common approach to assessing personality through five core dimensions: openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism (p.45).
Social Learning Theory (HL)
A situational approach suggesting individuals learn behaviours and attitudes through the observation and imitation of others in their social environment (p.46).
Mental Toughness
Mental Toughness
An aspect of personality explaining how individuals manage pressure, encompassing appraisal of challenges, commitment, confidence, perceived control, and resilience (p.46).
Resilience
A component of mental toughness referring to an individual's ability to "bounce back" or persist through adversity (p.46).
Self-fulfilling prophecy (HL)
The theory that a sportsperson's perceived self-confidence results in greater persistence and effort, increasing the probability of eventual success (p.46).
Learned helplessness (HL)
A state associated with an individual's self-perceived lack of control over their future outcomes (p.46).
Stress/Coping
Arousal
The level of physical and psychological activation that impacts how individuals manage their intensity during performance (p.49).
Anxiety
A negative emotional state induced by high activation levels, characterized by fear, worry, and despondency (p.49).
Stressor
A stimulus or situation that causes psychological strain, which can be perceived as either positive or negative (p.50).
Coping Strategies
Specific efforts (problem-focused, emotion-focused, or avoidance-focused) used to manage stressors (p.50).
These technical definitions form the essential building blocks for understanding the assessment structure of the 2026 exam.

Content summary: Individual Differences (C.1.1 & C.1.2)

Psychological constructs explain the "performance gap"‚Äîwhy athletes with similar physiological profiles often achieve different outcomes.

C.1.1 Personality

Personality is the unique intersection of nature and nurture.

  • Trait-based approach: Enduring, stable characteristics (p.45).
  • The Big Five (OCEAN): Openness (originality), Conscientiousness (goal-direction), Extraversion (social energy), Agreeableness (relationships), and Neuroticism (distress perception) (p.45-46).
  • Social Learning Theory (HL): Imitation is dictated by the "regard" for the role model (p.46).
  • Malleability (HL): Personality can change through significant experience and coaching over time (p.46).

C.1.2 Mental Toughness

  • Components: Appraisal of challenges, commitment, confidence, perceived control, and resilience (p.46).
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy (HL): Confidence ‚Üí persistence ‚Üí success.
  • Learned Helplessness (HL): Perceived lack of control over future outcomes (p.46).

Mechanism Steps: The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy (HL)

  1. Confidence Appraisal: The athlete perceives they have the ability to succeed.
  2. Increased Persistence: This perception triggers greater effort in training and competition.
  3. Outcome Success: High effort increases the statistical probability of eventual achievement (p.46).
Sporting Example
A marathon runner high in Conscientiousness adheres strictly to a training macrocycle. When facing "the wall" at mile 20, their Mental Toughness allows them to appraise the pain as a challenge. Their Resilience prevents them from succumbing to Learned Helplessness, ensuring they maintain pace to the finish.
Examiner Tip: Candidates must distinguish between learning (relatively permanent change in behavior) and performance (temporary occurrence fluctuating over time) (p.47).

Paper 2 Extended Response

Question 1 4 MARKS
Self-fulfilling prophecy in injury rehabilitation (HL)
Explain the "Self-fulfilling prophecy" in injury rehabilitation.
üìã Model answer
The self-fulfilling prophecy posits that perceived self-confidence leads to greater persistence. If an injured athlete believes they can return to peak fitness (confidence), they will exhibit higher effort during grueling rehab sessions. This increased dedication increases the statistical probability of a successful recovery (p.46).
Question 2 8 MARKS
Big Five traits and sport
Analyse how "The Big Five" traits influence sport choice and team success.
üìã Model answer
Personality traits influence the environments athletes seek. An individual high in Extraversion may prefer team sports like rugby for social energy, whereas high Introversion might lead to individual sports like marathon running. Conscientiousness drives adherence to training cycles, vital for long-term success. Within teams, Agreeableness fosters cooperation and reduces conflict. However, high Neuroticism may lead an athlete to perceive competition as distressing, potentially requiring mental toughness training to prevent performance declines (p.45-46).

Mathematical skills

"Tool 3: Mathematics" is essential for validating psychological research.

Worked Example:
  • Mean: Sum of scores / n.
  • Standard Deviation (SD): Indicates the spread of data. A small SD (e.g., 2.1) shows high consistency.
  • Coefficient of Variation (CV): (SD / Mean) √ó 100.
  • Significance: Differences are significant if p < 0.05 (95% confidence).
Note: All calculations must be rounded to 2 decimal places (e.g., 2.67%).

Common errors

Confusing Somatic vs. Cognitive Anxiety
Examiners frequently note that candidates fail to distinguish between physical symptoms (HR) and mental worry.
Personality as a Predictor
Candidates often incorrectly claim a specific profile guarantees success. The syllabus states: "There is no personality profile that predicts sport performance" (p.46).
Forgetting "Appraisal"
When listing the 4Cs, candidates often omit "Appraisal of Challenges" (p.46).
Malleability Misconception
HL students often wrongly assume personality is fixed. It is malleable over long periods (p.46).
Thirst as Dehydration Indicator
Thirst is a delayed and unreliable indicator of fluid loss (p.35).
Correlation vs. Causation
High Mental Toughness scores correlating with success does not prove that MT caused the success; external variables (coaching/talent) may be involved.
Amotivation vs. Controlled
Candidates confuse a lack of motivation with being motivated by external rewards (p.48).
Ignoring Units
Failing to include "%" or "bpm" in calculations results in lost marks.
