Quick reference: key terms

Precise understanding of psychological terminology allows you to articulate complex concepts accurately‚Äîessential for high marks in all question types.

üé≠ Personality theories
Personality
'Those relatively stable and enduring aspects of individuals which distinguish them from other people, making them unique but at the same time permit a comparison between individuals' (Gross, 1992).
Trait
Enduring patterns of thoughts, feelings and behaviours that are relatively stable over time.
The "Big Five" Personality Traits
Five major dimensions of personality: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism.
Interactionist View
Perspective that behaviour results from constant interaction between person and environment, summarized by B = f(P, E).
Social Learning Theory (SLT)
Theory that individuals acquire behaviours, attitudes and skills by observing and imitating others, plus through direct experience and reinforcement.
üí≠ Cognitive concepts
Self-Efficacy
The expectation an individual holds regarding their capability to perform a specific task or achieve a goal.
Attribution Theory
Theory examining how people explain successes and failures based on ability, effort, task difficulty and luck.
Learned Helplessness
Acquired condition where an individual perceives no control over task outcomes, leading them to cease trying.
Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
Concept where beliefs and expectations about oneself or others influence actions in ways that cause those expectations to become reality.
üí™ Mental toughness
Mental Toughness
Personality aspect explaining how individuals manage challenging and pressurized situations; associated with unshakeable self-belief, resilience and determination.
Perfectionism (Adaptive)
Perfectionism emphasizing high standards without excessive concern over mistakes.
Perfectionism (Maladaptive)
Perfectionism coupling high standards with excessive concern over mistakes and others' opinions.

Content summary

Topic C.1 examines psychological characteristics distinguishing individuals and their impact on sporting performance and health outcomes.

C.1.1 Personality

Understanding personality helps predict behaviour patterns, though no single personality profile guarantees elite athletic success.

Core concepts

  • Personality definition: Unique, relatively stable and enduring characteristics distinguishing individuals.
  • "Big Five" model: Five broad dimensions‚ÄîOpenness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (emotional stability).
  • LOTS data categories: L-data (lifetime history), O-data (observations), T-data (test data), S-data (self-reported).
  • Interactionist perspective: B = f(P, E)‚Äîbehaviour as function of person‚Äìenvironment interaction.
  • Social Learning Theory: Behaviour acquisition through observation, imitation, direct experience and reinforcement.
  • Self-efficacy antecedents: Performance accomplishments, vicarious experience, verbal persuasion, physiological/emotional state.
  • Perfectionism types: Adaptive (high standards without excessive worry) vs maladaptive (high standards with fear of failure).
  • Personality types: Type A (competitive, ambitious), Type B (tolerant, relaxed), Type D (distressed, negative emotions) linked to health outcomes.

How self-efficacy develops

  1. Performance accomplishments: Athlete successfully executes skill, providing direct proof of ability and building strong belief.
  2. Vicarious experience (modelling): Observing similar-ability peer succeed suggests "If they can do it, so can I."
  3. Verbal persuasion: Credible source (coach) expresses confidence in athlete's ability, enhancing self-belief.
  4. Physiological/emotional state: Athlete interprets arousal (increased heart rate) as readiness and excitement rather than anxiety, positively impacting confidence.

How interactionist view explains behaviour

  1. Individual possesses core personality traits (P) predisposing certain behaviours.
  2. Individual enters specific situation/environment (E) with its own demands and norms.
  3. Behaviour (B) results from trait‚Äìenvironment interaction. High-agreeableness player might still exhibit aggression during competitive match because situation demands it.
  4. Cognitive factors mediate this interaction: individual's skills, situation interpretation, and outcome expectations influence how traits express as behaviour.
Performance profiling (Butler et al., 1993): Olympic boxers and coaches independently rated physical/psychological qualities. "Mean discrepancy" between athlete self-perception and coach perception was calculated. In three of four cases where discrepancy was low (aligned assessment), the boxer won an Olympic medal. Successful boxers (A, D, I) had mean discrepancy scores below 1.0, demonstrating clear correlation between coach‚Äìathlete alignment and success.

C.1.2 Mental toughness

Mental toughness is a multidimensional construct determining how effectively athletes cope with pressure, adversity and challenging situations.

Core concepts

  • Mental toughness characteristics: Unshakeable self-belief, resilience, determination, effective pressure/adversity coping.
  • 4Cs model: Challenge, Control, Commitment, Confidence.
  • Development methods: Psychological Skills Training (PST), mindfulness, self-compassion.
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: Cycle where success/failure expectations influence behaviour making that outcome more likely.
  • Learned helplessness: Acquired state of perceived lack of control over outcomes, causing cessation of effort.
  • Attribution theory dimensions: Locus of stability (stable/unstable), locus of causality (internal/external), locus of control (controllable/uncontrollable).
  • Health outcomes: Mental toughness associated with improved sleep quality and fewer depression symptoms.

How attribution affects confidence

  1. Athlete experiences success or failure (winning/losing race).
  2. They attribute outcome to a cause: winning attributed to superior ability (internal, stable) and dedicated effort (internal, controllable).
  3. Internal and stable attribution for success leads to increased pride and expectation of future success.
  4. Athlete's confidence and training motivation are enhanced. Conversely, attributing failure to lack of ability (internal, stable) damages confidence.

Positive self-fulfilling prophecy cycle

  1. Athlete develops strong belief in abilities and expects success (high self-confidence).
  2. Belief fuels motivation, leading to increased persistence and greater practice/competition effort.
  3. Increased effort and positive mindset improve performance and increase successful outcome probability.
  4. Success reinforces initial belief, strengthening self-confidence and creating virtuous cycle.
Olympic gold medallists (Sarkar et al., 2015): Elite athletes didn't avoid stress; they used stressful experiences to fuel motivation and effort. They demonstrated tendency to perceive demanding competitive situations as growth opportunities rather than threats. This mindset is core to the 'Challenge' aspect of the 4Cs model, showcasing how mentally toughest athletes thrive in high-pressure environments.
Developing mental toughness: Not innate‚Äîcan be developed through systematic training. Psychological Skills Training (PST) programmes combining goal-setting, imagery, self-talk and arousal regulation have shown effectiveness in enhancing mental toughness components.

Paper 1A: Multiple-choice questions

Question 1
According to the interactionist perspective, behaviour is primarily determined by:
  • A. Individual's stable and enduring personality traits
  • B. Specific situation or environment an individual is in
  • C. Dynamic interaction between traits and environment
  • D. Process of observing and imitating role models
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Lewin's formula B = f(P, E) explicitly states behaviour (B) is a function (f) of person‚Äìenvironment interaction (P, E). A describes purely trait-based approach; B describes purely situational approach; D describes Social Learning Theory.
Question 2
An athlete attributes their victory to superior ability and hard work. According to Weiner's attribution theory, which combination does this represent?
  • A. External, Stable, Uncontrollable
  • B. Internal, Unstable, Controllable
  • C. Internal, Stable, Controllable
  • D. External, Unstable, Controllable
‚úÖ Correct answer: C
'Ability' and 'hard work' are within the athlete (Internal). Superior ability is Stable; hard work (effort) is within Control. A would be biased refereeing; B's unstable element contradicts 'superior ability'; D would be opponent having an 'off' day (external).

‚úÖ Correct answer: C
Vicarious experience involves learning/gaining confidence by observing others (modelling). Seeing peer succeed suggests "I can too." A is verbal persuasion; B is performance accomplishments; D is managing physiological/emotional state.

The LOTS acronym categorizes personality data methods. The 'T' stands for:

Attribution theory and learned helplessness
An elite swimmer fails to qualify for major finals despite strong training performances. She increasingly states, "It doesn't matter how hard I train, I'm just not lucky on race day." Using attribution theory and learned helplessness, explain psychological factors contributing to poor competitive performance.
üìã Model answer
The swimmer attributes failure to an external factor (luck) outside personal control. [1 mark]

She attributes it to an unstable factor ('on race day'), suggesting changeability. However, consistently blaming an uncontrollable factor like luck means failing to take performance responsibility. [1 mark]

This attribution pattern, if repeated, can lead to learned helplessness‚Äîa state of perceived lack of control over outcomes. [1 mark]

Believing results are solely due to luck disconnects effort from outcome, decreasing competitive motivation and persistence. [1 mark]

This "actions have no effect" belief can become self-fulfilling: expecting failure due to bad luck reduces competitive focus and effort, making failure more likely. [1 mark]

This creates a state where she feels doomed to competitive failure regardless of actual physical ability or training preparation, explaining the training‚Äìcompetition performance discrepancy. [1 mark]
Question 8 5 MARKS
Personality measurement ethics and emotion effects
Outline two ethical issues in measuring athlete personality and, using one "big five" dimension, predict emotion effects on performance.
üìã Model answer
Confidentiality: Personality data is sensitive. Ethical obligation ensures questionnaire results remain confidential and aren't used improperly (e.g., team selection decisions without athlete consent). [1 mark]

Labeling: Using personality test results to label athletes (e.g., "lacks conscientiousness") can be detrimental, potentially creating negative self-fulfilling prophecy or causing coaches to treat athlete differently, unfairly limiting development opportunities. [1 mark]

Neuroticism is the dimension associated with negative emotion tendency (anxiety, worry, stress). High-neuroticism athletes may experience negative emotions more intensely pre-competition. [1 mark]

These negative emotions (high anxiety) can impair performance by causing increased muscle tension, attentional narrowing (missing important cues), and failure-worry distractions. [1 mark]

Conversely, high emotional stability (low neuroticism) athletes remain calmer under pressure. Positive emotions (excitement, confidence) can improve performance by promoting optimal arousal, broadening attention and increasing motivation. [1 mark]

Common errors

Confusing self-efficacy with self-esteem
Self-efficacy is task-specific belief in capabilities ("I can make this free throw"); self-esteem is global self-worth evaluation. Athletes can have high self-efficacy for specific skills but low overall self-esteem, or vice versa. They're not interchangeable.
Believing personality is entirely fixed
While trait theories describe personality as relatively stable, it can and does change over time. Interactionist and social learning perspectives emphasize that experiences and environment shape behaviours and even underlying traits.
Assuming a single "elite athlete personality"
No ideal personality profile for sporting success exists. Psychologists haven't identified particular traits predicting sporting success; no one personality profile associates with elite performance.
Treating mental toughness and self-compassion as opposites
Mental toughness doesn't require relentless self-criticism. Self-compassion can be vital for developing mental toughness, helping athletes manage setbacks, regulate emotions and persevere without destructive self-judgment.
Misinterpreting self-serving bias in attribution
While people often attribute success internally and failure externally (self-serving bias), this isn't a healthy long-term strategy. Presenting this bias as positive without acknowledging that failure to take responsibility for poor performances prevents learning and improvement is an error.
Oversimplifying Social Learning Theory
Social learning isn't just "monkey see, monkey do." The theory includes cognitive constructs. Individuals don't just imitate role models; their beliefs, expectancies and personal standards mediate the process. They judge behaviour and consequences before adoption.

Linking questions

How does mental toughness (C.1.2) link to stress and anxiety (C.4)?
Mental toughness enhances stress and pressure coping ability. Components like confidence, control and commitment buffer against negative stress effects. Mentally tough individuals perceive stressful situations as challenges (the 'C' in 4Cs) rather than threats, maintaining performance under pressure and avoiding debilitating high anxiety effects.
How does self-compassion (C.1.2) link to injury and recovery (B.3)?
Significant sports injury is a major psychological distress source. High self-compassion athletes cope better with recovery frustrations. Instead of harsh self-criticism for injury, they use kindness and mindfulness to manage emotional response, adhere better to rehabilitation programmes and maintain motivation, potentially leading to more effective recovery.
How does self-efficacy (C.1.1) link to training adaptations (A.3)?
High self-efficacy for training tasks predicts greater training adherence, effort intensity and persistence through challenging sessions. Athletes believing they can successfully complete demanding training programmes are more likely to maintain consistency, apply appropriate overload and achieve desired physiological adaptations. This psychological factor directly influences training effectiveness and long-term performance gains.
