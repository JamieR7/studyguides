üß† Topic C.3: Motivation

As the Lead Examiner for the 2026 syllabus transition, I have designed this guide to bridge the gap between theoretical knowledge and the rigorous demands of the new assessment model. Motivation is not merely "wanting to win"; it is a complex psychological construct that dictates athlete persistence, learning, and long-term participation. In the IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science (SEHS) course, success in Topic C.3 requires more than a casual understanding‚Äîit demands the precise application of terminology and the ability to evaluate behavioral outcomes within specific sporting contexts.

üìö Section 1

Quick reference: key terms

In Paper 1 and 2, the markscheme rewards the exact terminology found in the syllabus. Precise definitions are the foundation of scoring; mislabeling a construct (e.g., using "self-drive" instead of "intrinsic motivation") will result in lost marks.

Motivation
The direction and intensity of one's effort (p.493). (Determines whether an athlete is attracted to a situation and how much energy they expend).
Intrinsic Motivation
Motivation coming from within the person, involving excitement, fun, and the chance to improve skills (p.494). (The primary driver for long-term training adherence and enjoyment).
Extrinsic Motivation
Motivation resulting from external rewards such as money, trophies, or praise (p.494). (Can foster initial participation but may undermine intrinsic drive if perceived as controlling).
Achievement Motivation
A personality trait representing the balance between the motive to achieve success and the motive to avoid failure (p.496). (Dictates the risk level of tasks an athlete chooses to attempt).
Task Orientation
A focus on mastery of the task, learning new skills, and exerting effort for self-improvement (p.497). (Leads to adaptive motivational patterns and persistence during injury).
Ego Orientation
A focus on demonstrating superior ability compared to others and winning with minimal effort (p.497). (Can lead to maladaptive patterns and high anxiety if the athlete's perceived ability is low).
Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
A macro-theory of human motivation that proposes individuals are motivated by the need to satisfy three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness (p.504-505). (Provides a framework for moving from controlled to autonomous motivation).
Amotivation
A state of lacking any intention to act or a diminished sense of drive (p.511). (The primary psychological driver behind athlete dropout).
Autonomy
The psychological need to feel a sense of volition and freedom in one's actions (p.505, 508). (Influences whether an athlete feels "pushed" by a coach or "driven" by choice).
Competence
The need to feel capable of performing tasks and acquiring the skills necessary for success (p.505, 508). (Bolstered by positive, skill-based feedback that reinforces ability).
Relatedness
The need to relate to others and feel a sense of belonging or acceptance within a group (p.505). (Impacts team cohesion and perceived social support).
Motivational Climate
The psychological environment created by the coach or teacher regarding the definition of success (p.517). (Directly influences the athlete's goal orientation and emotional state).
Mastery Climate
An environment where success is evaluated based on personal improvement and effort (p.517). (Reduces performance-related boredom and increases task persistence).
Performance Climate
An environment where success is defined by social comparison and outperforming others (p.517). (Increases the risk of dropout in athletes with lower skill levels).
The Examiner's View: Mastering these terms is the "entry fee" for higher-order questions. You must move beyond simple definitions to explain how these constructs result in specific adaptive or maladaptive behavioral patterns.

Application Examples for Topic C.3

Evaluate Need Achievement Theory (McClelland-Atkinson):

  • Strength: Provides a framework for predicting task choice (e.g., why high achievers seek 50/50 challenges) (p.496).
  • Strength: Successfully identifies the role of situational factors (incentive value of success) in achievement behavior (p.496).
  • Limitation: The theory is primarily a personality-based model and may underestimate the impact of the current environment (climate) on behavior (p.497).
  • Limitation: Does not reliably predict actual performance levels, only the tendency to seek or avoid achievement situations (p.497).

Explain the relationship between high ego orientation and anxiety:

High ego orientation leads to anxiety when the athlete also has low perceived ability. Because their success is defined by outperforming others, they perceive the situation as a threat to their self-worth when they believe they lack the skill to win, leading to increased stress and avoidance behaviors (p.500, 520).

üìñ Section 4

Content summary

C.3.1 Achievement Motivation

Achievement motivation is the interaction between personality (Motive to achieve success vs. avoid failure) and the environment (p.496).

Mechanism

Personality Factors + Situational Factors (Probability of success/Incentive value) \( \rightarrow \) Resultant Tendency \( \rightarrow \) Emotional Reactions \( \rightarrow \) Achievement Behavior (p.496).

  • High Achievers: Select challenging tasks, display high effort, and focus on the pride of success.
  • Low Achievers: Avoid challenges, opting for tasks that are either extremely easy (guaranteed success) or extremely difficult (failure is not shameful because it was expected) (p.496).
In swimming, an individual sport where success is highly visible, a low achiever may avoid racing a peer of similar ability to avoid the risk of public failure and subsequent shame (p.501).

C.3.2 Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

SDT focuses on the quality of motivation and the satisfaction of psychological needs (p.505).

The Continuum

Ranges from Amotivation (lack of intent) \( \rightarrow \) Extrinsic Regulation (external rewards) \( \rightarrow \) Introjected Regulation (guilt/ego-pressure) \( \rightarrow \) Identified Regulation (seeing value in the task) \( \rightarrow \) Integrated Regulation (task aligns with values) \( \rightarrow \) Intrinsic Motivation (pure enjoyment) (p.505, 512).

AHL ONLY: The Six Mini-Theories (p.508)
  1. Cognitive Evaluation Theory: Focuses on how external events (rewards) affect intrinsic motivation via competence and autonomy.
  2. Organismic Integration Theory: Describes the extrinsic motivation continuum.
  3. Causality Orientations Theory: Individual differences in tendencies toward autonomy or control.
  4. Basic Psychological Needs Theory: The necessity of autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
  5. Goal Contents Theory: Distinguishes between intrinsic (growth) and extrinsic (wealth) goals.
  6. Relationships Motivation Theory: Focuses on relatedness in high-quality relationships.

C.3.3 Motivational Climate

The climate is the environment created by the coach/teacher (p.517).

  • Mastery Climate: Success is defined by personal progress (Task-oriented).
  • Performance Climate: Success is defined by social comparison (Ego-oriented).

The TARGET Approach

Coaches manipulate Task (differentiation), Authority (leadership roles), Recognition (private), Grouping (mixed-ability), Evaluation (self-referenced), and Time (flexibility) to foster a mastery climate (p.519).

So What? A performance climate is strongly correlated with higher dropout rates among adolescents, particularly those with low perceived competence (p.511, 515).

The player defines success solely by outperforming others [1].

üìã Model answer
Task: Use differentiated drills so every player experiences success relative to their own starting point [1].

Authority: Give players leadership roles in drills to satisfy the need for autonomy [1].

Recognition: Provide private feedback on effort and personal bests rather than public comparison [1].

Grouping: Use mixed-ability groups to foster relatedness rather than ability-based "tiers" [1].

Evaluation: Base assessments on individual improvement/effort scores [1].

Time: Allow flexible time for players to master a skill before moving to the next [1].

Common errors

Confusing Task Orientation with Intrinsic Motivation
Task orientation is a trait (how you define success), while intrinsic motivation is a state/type of motivation (why you do it).
Amotivation Misconception
Students often describe this as "low motivation." Correctly, it is the absence of intent or drive (p.511).
The "Ego is Evil" Fallacy
Assuming high ego orientation is always negative. Correction: High ego orientation is beneficial if paired with high task orientation and high perceived ability (p.500, 521).
Relatedness as "Socializing"
Relatedness is a psychological need for belonging and acceptance, not just "having friends" or being chatty (p.505).
Introjected vs. Identified Regulation
Introjected is controlled (driven by guilt/shame), while Identified is autonomous (driven by the athlete's own values) (p.512).
The Additive Principle
Misunderstanding that extrinsic rewards do not always add to intrinsic motivation; they can actually decrease it if the reward is perceived as controlling (p.503).
Climate vs. Orientation
Confusing the Motivational Climate (the environment created by the coach) with Goal Orientation (the individual's internal disposition).

Linking questions

Link 1: Personality (C.1) & Achievement Motivation (C.3.1): How does the interactionist view (B=f(P,E)) explain an athlete's choice of task?
Thinking Points: Personality (P) provides the motive to succeed/avoid failure, while the Environment (E) provides the probability of success. Interaction determines the "Resultant Tendency" (p.420, 496).
Link 2: Motivational Climate (C.3.3) & Arousal/Anxiety (C.4): Why does a performance climate increase pre-competition anxiety?
Thinking Points: In an ego-climate, success is comparative. If an athlete's perceived ability is low, they feel a threat to their self-esteem, which induces high arousal and cognitive anxiety (p.520).
