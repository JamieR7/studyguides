Quick reference: key terms

Mastering motivation terminology is foundational for understanding core concepts and articulating them precisely in examinations.

üî• Core motivation concepts
Motivation
The direction and intensity of one's effort.
Intrinsic Motivation
Motivation from within a person, driven by fun, enjoyment and the chance to improve skills.
Extrinsic Motivation
Motivation from external rewards (money, trophies) or less tangible rewards (praise, status).
üèÜ Achievement motivation theories
Achievement Motivation
An individual's desire to succeed, balanced against their fear of failure.
Task Orientation
A focus on mastering tasks, learning new skills, exerting effort and achieving self-improvement.
Ego Orientation
A focus on demonstrating superior ability compared with others and winning in competitions.
üå± Self-Determination Theory concepts
Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
Theory of human motivation positing individuals are driven to satisfy three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence and relatedness.
Amotivation
A state of lacking motivation, which can come from perceived lack of competence, or lack of interest, relevance or value in the activity.
Autonomy
The psychological need to feel in control of one's own decisions and behaviour.
Competence
The psychological need to feel effective and capable of accomplishing tasks and mastering skills.
Relatedness
The psychological need to connect with others and feel a sense of belonging within a group or community.
Motivational Climate
The psychological environment, often created by a coach or teacher, influencing an individual's goal orientation and motivational responses.

üìñ Section 4

Content summary

Topic C.3 examines foundational motivation theories, self-determination and motivational climate creation to enhance athletic performance and sustained participation.

C.3.1 Achievement motivation

Foundational theories explore the interplay between personality and situational factors, providing frameworks for analysing motivation in sporting contexts.

Key concepts

  • Motivation definition: The direction and intensity of individual's effort.
  • Intrinsic vs extrinsic: Internal factors (fun, enjoyment) vs external factors (rewards, praise).
  • Need Achievement Theory (McClelland and Atkinson): Motivation as balance between motive to achieve success (Ms) and motive to avoid failure (Maf).
  • Goal Orientation Theory: Individuals motivated by achieving goals‚Äîtask-oriented (self-improvement focused) or ego-oriented (outperforming others focused).

Need Achievement Theory mechanism

  1. Personality factors: Individual possesses innate motive to achieve success and motive to avoid failure.
  2. Situational factors: They assess probability of success and incentive value in given situation.
  3. Resultant tendency: They decide to approach success or avoid failure. High achievers (Ms > Maf) approach challenges, especially 50% success probability. Low achievers (Maf > Ms) avoid such challenges, preferring very easy or very hard tasks.
  4. Emotional reactions: Approaching success focuses on pride feeling; avoiding failure focuses on shame feeling.
  5. Achievement behaviour: Culminates in observable behaviour. High achievers seek challenging situations and persist; low achievers avoid challenges and demonstrate less persistence.
Achievement motivation in action: A high-achieving athlete seeks challenging competitions against equal-ability opponents to test skills. A low-achieving athlete might only compete in certain-win events or events so difficult that no one expects success, thus avoiding true ability tests.
High and low achievers are defined not by ability, but by response to challenge. Both groups want pride and minimized shame, but use different strategies.

C.3.2 Self-determination

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a contemporary, highly influential theory emphasizing the interaction between inner drives and external environment.

Key concepts

  • SDT approach: Organismic-dialectic approach emphasizing individual inner drive‚Äìexternal environment interaction.
  • Three basic psychological needs: Autonomy, competence and relatedness.
  • Self-determination continuum: Motivation spectrum from amotivation (no motivation) to intrinsic motivation (most self-determined form).
  • Six mini-theories: Comprehensive components forming the SDT meta-theory.

The self-determination continuum

  1. Amotivation: Lowest self-determination level; lack of intent to act. Example: Harper has no desire for physical activity.
  2. Extrinsic regulation: Behaviour controlled by external rewards/punishments. Example: Lee walks because doctor advised.
  3. Introjected regulation: Behaviour driven by internal pressures like guilt or avoiding disapproval. Example: Student plays on school team only because parents want it.
  4. Identified regulation: Individual consciously values behaviour and sees it as important. Example: Attending aerobics because it's important to look good.
  5. Integrated regulation: Behaviour fully assimilated with individual's sense of self and values. Example: Tal is a runner getting personal satisfaction from achieving goals.
  6. Intrinsic motivation: Highest self-determination level; behaviour performed for inherent fun and enjoyment. Example: Rafa plays tennis and swims for pure fun.
Autonomy-supportive coaching: A youth baseball team with coach complete control and all decision-making (controlling environment) shows lower player motivation. If the coach includes players in decision-making and gives choices (autonomy-supportive environment), players are happier, more engaged, and their autonomy, competence and relatedness needs are better met, leading to higher self-determined motivation.
Extrinsic motivation isn't always "bad." Moving along the continuum from extrinsic to integrated regulation, motivation becomes more autonomous and self-determined, even though technically still extrinsic.

C.3.3 Motivational climate

The psychological environment shaped by coaches, teachers and parents directly influences athlete motivation through specific, manipulable structural elements.

Key concepts

  • Mastery (Task) climate: Environment focusing on effort, personal improvement, learning and cooperation.
  • Performance (Ego) climate: Environment focusing on social comparison, competition and outperforming others.
  • TARGET approach: Framework outlining six areas coaches/teachers can manipulate to create mastery-oriented motivational climate.
  • TEOSQ (Task and Ego Orientation in Sport Questionnaire): Tool measuring individual's dispositional task- or ego-orientation tendency.

The TARGET approach

Coaches and teachers influence motivational climate by structuring six key areas:

  1. Task: Designing diverse, challenging, self-referenced tasks (maintaining interest, focusing on personal progress).
  2. Authority: Involving students in decision-making and leadership roles (fostering autonomy).
  3. Recognition: Providing private recognition for individual effort and improvement (building competence without promoting social comparison).
  4. Grouping: Using flexible, mixed-ability groups (emphasizing learning and cooperation over ability).
  5. Evaluation: Using self-referenced criteria based on personal improvement (focusing on mastery and effort).
  6. Time: Allowing flexible timing for task completion (supporting mastery, reducing anxiety).
Creating mastery climate in PE: A PE teacher improving ball-dribbling skills could create mastery climate by having students work in mixed-ability groups on self-referenced goals with private effort feedback. Creating performance climate: making the lesson a public race between ability-based groups with recognition only for winning team. The latter may encourage cheating (cutting corners) to win.
Examiner's note: Motivational climate questions rarely involve simple recall. Expect scenario-based problems in Papers 1B and 2 requiring TARGET framework as diagnostic and prescriptive tool. You must identify performance climate elements in scenarios and suggest specific, practical changes using TARGET principles to create mastery climate.
An individual has dispositional goal orientation (natural task- or ego-orientation tendency), but motivational climate can significantly influence state motivation in particular situations. A mastery climate can promote positive outcomes even for ego-oriented individuals.

Adolescent soccer players' motivational characteristics

(a)

Using self-determination theory, explain why the group thinking of stopping playing soccer might have lower scores for relatedness and autonomy compared to persistent players. (4 marks)

Question 7
TEOSQ results for volleyball players
Three volleyball players' task and ego orientation scores.
(a)

Question 9 5 MARKS
Task vs ego orientation and mastery climate
Distinguish between task-oriented and ego-oriented individuals. Explain, using examples, why a coach of a youth sports team would be advised to create a mastery (task-involving) motivational climate.
üìã Model answer
Distinction: A task-oriented individual defines success based on self-referenced criteria. Their main goals are to master tasks, exert maximum effort and achieve personal improvement. For example, a task-oriented swimmer focuses on beating their previous personal best time. In contrast, an ego-oriented individual defines success through social comparison. Their goal is to demonstrate superior ability by outperforming others or winning with less effort. For example, an ego-oriented swimmer focuses on finishing first in the race, regardless of time. [1 mark]

Mastery climate benefits‚Äîpositive outcomes: A coach should create a mastery motivational climate because it yields more positive motivational outcomes. This climate type emphasizes effort, learning and personal progress, leading to more positive attitude, higher satisfaction and increased intrinsic motivation. For example, when athletes are praised for hard work rather than just winning, they're more likely to persist after a loss and see failure as a learning opportunity. [1 mark]

Mastery climate benefits‚Äîprotects motivation: Furthermore, a mastery climate protects athletes' motivation, especially those with lower perceived ability or who are ego-oriented. In an ego-involving climate, athletes doubting their ability to win may become anxious and reduce effort. In a mastery climate, they can still feel successful by trying hard and improving skills. [1 mark]

Practical TARGET example‚ÄîRecognition: A practical example of creating this climate is the coach using the Recognition element of the TARGET model by providing private, individual praise for an athlete who has mastered a new skill, rather than only publicly praising the match winner. [1 mark + 1 mark for quality]

Common errors

Believing extrinsic motivation is always "bad"
Common mistake: viewing all extrinsic motivation as controlling and detrimental. The self-determination continuum shows extrinsic motivation exists on a spectrum. While extrinsic regulation (being paid) is controlling, identified and integrated regulation are autonomous forms of extrinsic motivation where individuals personally value the activity. These forms associate with positive outcomes like persistence and well-being.
Confusing ego orientation with being a bad athlete
Students often assume ego orientation is inherently negative. While problematic if task orientation is low and perceived competence is low, high ego orientation can powerfully motivate elite athletes. Most successful motivational profiles often involve high orientation in both task and ego. The high task/high ego group reports highest perceived sport competence and enjoyment.
Treating task and ego orientations as mutually exclusive
Incorrect to think athletes are either task-oriented or ego-oriented. The TEOSQ measures both independently. Athletes can be high in both, low in both, or high in one and low in the other. The interaction between the two orientations, not just one or the other, predicts motivational outcomes.
Assuming all rewards undermine intrinsic motivation
Cognitive Evaluation Theory explains reward effect depends on perception. If a reward is seen as controlling ("you must do this to get the prize"), it can decrease intrinsic motivation. However, if perceived as informational and affirming competence (e.g., "most improved player" award), it can actually increase intrinsic motivation.
Oversimplifying Self-Determination Theory needs
Students may list three needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) but fail to understand their interplay. SDT posits all three needs must be reasonably satisfied for optimal psychological health and motivation. An environment supporting competence but denying autonomy (e.g., highly structured drill with no player choice) won't lead to most positive outcomes.
Misunderstanding amotivation
Amotivation isn't the same as being lazy or having low motivation. It's a specific state characterized by complete lack of intention to act. An amotivated individual perceives no relationship between their actions and outcomes, often due to feelings of incompetence or belief the activity has no value or relevance.

Linking questions

How does perceived ability (C.3.1) impact injury risk (B.3.1)?
Perceived ability is core to Need Achievement Theory and Goal Orientation Theory. High ego orientation with low perceived ability may cause unnecessary risk-taking to demonstrate competence, potentially leading to injury from poor judgement (e.g., attempting unprepared weightlifting max to impress others). Low achievement motivation may cause challenge avoidance, leading to under-preparation and higher injury risk when eventually forced into demanding situations. Lack of confidence could also lead to tentative movements and poor technique, increasing injury risk.
How can physical environment constraints (A.1.2 HL) impact athlete motivation (C.3.2)?
Physical environment can directly impact Self-Determination Theory's three basic psychological needs. Limited or poor-quality resources (e.g., run-down gym) can frustrate the need for competence, as athletes may feel they cannot train effectively or master skills. Lack of accessible facilities can thwart autonomy by limiting choice of when and how to train. Environmental factors like extreme heat, humidity or altitude can make training feel more like a chore than enjoyable activity, undermining intrinsic motivation and pushing athlete's motivation towards the more controlling continuum end.
How can mastery motivational climate principles (C.3.3) be applied to skill acquisition (D.4)?
TARGET principles of mastery climate align directly with skill acquisition best practices. For Task, coaches could design variable practice drills with diverse challenges instead of repetitive, blocked drills, promoting adaptability. For Authority, involving athletes in decision-making about which skill to work on fosters autonomy and cognitive engagement in learning. For Evaluation, focusing on self-referenced criteria and improvement rather than public comparison reduces anxiety interfering with learning and encourages athletes to focus on skill development process. This environment supports cognitive and associative learning stages by encouraging experimentation without fear of failure.
