Quick Reference Key Terms

Mastering the precise terminology of Sport, Exercise and Health Science is crucial for demonstrating a clear and deep understanding in your IB exams. Below is a list of essential definitions from Topic B.3, grouped by core concept to aid in your revision.

Injury Classification
Acute Injury
An injury that occurs immediately or suddenly due to an excessive application of force.
Chronic (Overuse) Injury
An injury that occurs due to repeated application of force over time, often with a gradual onset.
Musculoskeletal Injury
An injury defined as a reduction or loss of function or structure of a muscle, bone, ligament or tendon.
Trauma
The application of force (either acute or cumulative) that causes a physiological response of pain, discomfort, or loss of function, and leads to injury.
Risk Assessment
Risk of Injury
A concept concerned with recognizing what could potentially happen to cause harm, the likelihood of it happening, and the severity of that harm.
Internal Risk Factors
Factors relating to the person exercising or participating in physical activity that may increase the likelihood and/or severity of injury.
External Risk Factors
Factors relating to the outside environment or conditions, such as the playing surface, that may influence injury risk.
Injury Management & Prevention
Prehabilitation
Proactive interventions, such as programmes or exercises, used to reduce the risk of injury and maximize performance by preparing an athlete's body for the forces and movements of their sport.
Biomechanical Maladaptation
Inefficient movement patterns that can compromise the stability of our joints and place excessive stress on certain body parts, leading to an increased risk of injury.
POLICE Principle
An acronym (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression and Elevation) for the recommended management of an acute musculoskeletal injury.
Concussion
A traumatic brain injury that results in an impairment of neurological function due to direct or indirect biomechanical forces.

B.3.1 Causes of Injury

The risk of injury in sport and exercise is not a matter of simple chance but a complex interaction between factors inherent to the individual (internal) and factors related to the environment (external). Understanding this interplay is fundamental to identifying and mitigating risk, as detailed on pages 367-385 of the course text.

Key Concepts

  • An injury can be defined as a reduction or loss of bodily function or structure p.367.
  • The overall magnitude of risk is a product of the likelihood of an injury occurring and the severity of its consequences, calculated as: likelihood of risk √ó severity of risk p.367.
  • Internal risk factors are those relating to the individual athlete, such as age, previous injury, or sex, while external risk factors relate to the environment, like playing surface or equipment p.370-371.
  • Injuries are broadly classified as acute if they result from a single, sudden application of force (e.g., a ligament rupture), or chronic/overuse if they develop gradually from repeated application of force over time (e.g., tendonitis) p.380-381.
  • Biomechanical maladaptation refers to inefficient movement patterns that place excessive stress on specific body parts, increasing the risk of both acute and overuse injuries p.382.

Mechanisms of Injury Risk

How adolescent growth spurts increase injury risk:

  • The skeletal system grows rapidly, often outpacing the lengthening of soft tissues like muscles and tendons.
  • This discrepancy leads to increased muscle tightness and reduced flexibility.
  • During this period, the epiphyseal plates (growth plates) at the ends of bones are weaker than surrounding ligaments, making adolescents more susceptible to fractures in these specific areas p.371.

How a previous injury increases the risk of re-injury:

  • Following an injury, an athlete may have a reduced range of movement in the affected joint if rehabilitation is not fully completed.
  • Proprioception‚Äîthe body's awareness of its position in space‚Äîis often impaired, and if not fully retrained, it can lead to instability and poor movement control.
  • Both of these factors make the athlete more vulnerable to sustaining the same injury again p.372.
Real-World Example
Studies have shown significant sex differences in the rates of certain injuries. For example, female athletes have been found to have a higher incidence of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries compared to their male counterparts. Furthermore, some empirical data have suggested that biologically female athletes are at a greater risk of incurring a sport-related concussion compared with biologically male athletes p.373. While the reasons are complex, potential contributing factors include anatomical differences, hormonal influences, and neuromuscular activation patterns p.372-373.
TIP:
For exam questions on risk, always distinguish between the likelihood of an injury occurring and the severity of that injury. The overall risk is a product of both p.367.
NOTE:
Biomechanical maladaptation is a key concept linking movement quality to overuse injuries. Be prepared to give an example, like knee valgus in soccer or over-pronation in runners p.382-383.

B.3.2 Interventions Related to Injury

Interventions in sport aim to reduce injury risk by either lowering the likelihood of an incident happening or decreasing the severity of its outcome. These range from proactive strategies like prehabilitation to reactive strategies like acute injury treatment, all of which are essential components of athlete welfare, as covered on pages 386-397.

Key Concepts

  • Protective equipment, such as helmets and padding, is designed to absorb or dissipate the forces applied to the body, thereby reducing injury severity p.387.
  • Rule changes, such as banning certain types of tackles or contact, can fundamentally alter the nature of a sport to reduce the likelihood of dangerous situations occurring p.388.
  • Prehabilitation programmes, like the FIFA 11+, are structured warm-ups designed to proactively reduce injury risk by improving factors like neuromuscular control, balance, and strength p.388-389.
  • Scaling sporting equipment to match the size and strength of younger athletes (e.g., using smaller basketballs) allows for the development of more biomechanically efficient movements, reducing injury risk p.390.
  • The management of acute injuries has evolved, with acronyms like RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) being updated to POLICE (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation) to reflect a more active approach to recovery p.392.
  • Following a concussion, athletes must follow a graduated return to sport (GRTS) protocol, a staged process that ensures symptoms have fully resolved before a return to full competition is permitted p.394.

Mechanisms of Injury Prevention & Treatment

The mechanism behind the FIFA 11+ programme's effectiveness:

  • The programme is multifactorial, targeting several key risk factors simultaneously.
  • It incorporates exercises that improve neuromuscular control, which enhances the body's ability to produce controlled movement through coordinated muscle actions.
  • It includes balance and plyometric exercises to improve joint stability and landing mechanics.
  • It also features strengthening exercises, which help improve the force-generating capacity of muscles to protect joints p.389.

The six stages of the graduated return to sport protocol following a concussion:

  1. Symptom-limited activity: Daily activities that do not provoke symptoms, with the goal of gradually reintroducing work/school activities.
  2. Light aerobic exercise: Activities like walking or stationary cycling at a slow to medium pace to increase heart rate.
  3. Sport-specific exercise: Activities such as running or skating drills to add movement, without head impact.
  4. Non-contact training drills: More complex training drills like passing, which increase exercise, coordination, and cognitive load.
  5. Full contact practice: Following medical clearance, the athlete participates in normal training activities to restore confidence and assess functional skills.
  6. Return to sport: Normal game play p.394.
Real-World Example
A powerful example of an effective intervention is the banning of body checking in junior ice hockey. This rule change, which eliminates a player deliberately making full contact with an opponent, led to a 50% reduction in the overall injury rate and a remarkable 64% reduction in concussion rates, demonstrating how a single rule modification can significantly improve player safety p.388.
TIP:
When evaluating interventions, consider their scope. Rule changes can impact an entire sport, while prehabilitation requires athlete and coach buy-in. This makes a great point for an 'Evaluate' question.
NOTE:
The debate around inflammation is a higher-level concept. Understand that while NSAIDs reduce pain, inflammation is a natural and important part of the healing process p.393.

Question 7 | ERQ 1 6 MARKS
Internal Risk Factors During Growth Spurts
A 13-year-old basketball player is experiencing a rapid growth spurt. Explain two internal risk factors, other than age itself, that increase her risk of injury. For each factor, describe a specific intervention that her coach could implement to help mitigate this risk.
‚úì Model Answer
One internal risk factor that is heightened during a growth spurt is the temporary size difference between bone and soft tissue. The skeletal system can grow rapidly, but it may take time for the soft tissues like muscles and tendons to adapt and lengthen accordingly p.371. This can increase muscle tightness and reduce flexibility, making the athlete more susceptible to muscle strains. To mitigate this risk, a coach could implement a structured dynamic stretching programme as part of the warm-up and a static stretching routine during the cool-down. This focuses on major muscle groups used in basketball, such as the hamstrings, quadriceps, and calves, to improve muscle extensibility and reduce the risk of strain injuries. [3]

A second internal risk factor is related to biomechanical maladaptation as the athlete's body changes p.382. Rapid changes in limb length can alter an athlete's centre of gravity and coordination, leading to less efficient and potentially harmful movement patterns, such as knee valgus (knees collapsing inward) when landing from a jump. This incorrect movement places excessive stress on the knee ligaments, increasing the risk of an ACL injury. A coach could implement a prehabilitation programme, such as the Knee Control Program p.389, which includes neuromuscular training exercises. These exercises, such as single-leg balancing and proper landing technique drills (e.g., "land softly on the balls of your feet with knees bent"), can retrain movement patterns, improve joint stability, and correct biomechanical faults, thereby reducing the strain on vulnerable joints. [3]
Question 8 | ERQ 2 5 MARKS
POLICE Protocol for Acute Injury Management
Describe the POLICE protocol for managing an acute musculoskeletal injury, such as an ankle sprain. Explain the physiological rationale behind two of its components.
‚úì Model Answer
The POLICE protocol is an acronym used for the initial management of an acute musculoskeletal injury. It stands for Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, and Elevation p.392. Protection involves safeguarding the injured area from further damage, for instance by using crutches for an ankle sprain. Optimal Loading refers to the gradual introduction of mechanical stress to stimulate healing, replacing the concept of complete rest. Ice (cryotherapy) involves applying a cold pack to reduce pain and inflammation. Compression, using an elastic bandage, helps to limit swelling. Finally, Elevation means raising the injured limb above the level of the heart to assist fluid drainage. [3]

The physiological rationale for Compression is to reduce oedema, which is swelling caused by fluid leaking from damaged blood vessels into the surrounding tissue. By applying external pressure with a bandage, the pressure gradient is increased, which limits the amount of fluid that can accumulate in the injured area and promotes lymphatic drainage of existing fluid p.392. [1]

The rationale for Elevation is also related to managing oedema. By raising the injured limb, gravity is used to assist the venous and lymphatic systems in draining excess fluid away from the site of injury and back towards the central circulation. This reduces swelling and the pressure on nerve endings, which can help to alleviate pain p.392. [1]

Common Errors

Awareness of common misconceptions and errors can be just as valuable as knowing the correct information. By understanding the pitfalls that other students often fall into on Topic B.3, you can avoid making the same mistakes in an exam.

Confusing Acute vs. Chronic
Stating that an injury like a stress fracture is 'acute'. An acute injury results from a single, traumatic event p.380, while chronic or overuse injuries like stress fractures or tendonitis develop over time from repeated forces p.381.
Oversimplifying Risk Factors
Listing risk factors without explaining how they increase risk. For example, just stating 'age' is a risk factor is insufficient. A full answer should link age to specific physiological events like adolescent growth spurts and their effect on the musculoskeletal system p.371.
Assuming Equipment Prevents All Injury
Claiming that wearing a helmet prevents concussions. While helmets are highly effective at preventing skull fractures, they are less effective at preventing concussions, which are caused by the brain moving within the skull. Evidence shows concussion rates can remain high in helmeted sports p.375.
Treating All Inflammation as "Bad"
Stating that the goal of injury treatment is to eliminate all inflammation immediately. While excessive inflammation can delay healing, the inflammatory process is essential for repair, bringing immune cells to the area to clear damaged tissue p.393. The goal of acute management is to manage, not eliminate, it.
Misunderstanding 'Optimal Loading'
Interpreting the 'OL' in POLICE as complete rest. 'Optimal Loading' is a key evolution from older protocols (like RICE). It involves applying gentle, progressive mechanical stress to tissues to promote better healing and alignment of collagen fibres, in contrast to total immobilization p.392.
Ignoring Biomechanical Maladaptation
Attributing all overuse injuries simply to "too much training." Often, the way an athlete moves (biomechanical maladaptation) is a primary cause. Inefficient movement patterns place repeated, abnormal stress on specific tissues, leading to injury even with reasonable training loads p.382.

By being mindful of these points, you can provide more nuanced and accurate answers. This holistic approach is further enhanced by making connections to other topics in the syllabus.
