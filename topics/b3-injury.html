‚ö†Ô∏è Risk and injury types
Injury
A reduction or loss of bodily function or structure.
Risk of Injury
The likelihood of a hazard causing an injury multiplied by the severity of that potential injury.
Acute Injury
An injury occurring immediately or suddenly due to excessive force application (acute trauma), such as fractures or concussions.
Chronic (Overuse) Injury
An injury developing over time due to repeated force application (cumulative trauma), such as tendonitis or shin splints.
Musculoskeletal Injury
A reduction or loss of function/structure of muscle, bone, ligament or tendon, including lacerations, contusions and strains.
Concussion
A traumatic brain injury from direct or indirect biomechanical forces, which may or may not involve loss of consciousness.
üîç Causal factors
Internal Risk Factors
Factors relating to the person exercising that increase injury risk: age, previous injury, sex differences, congenital conditions.
External Risk Factors
Environmental factors increasing injury risk: protective equipment, playing surface, coaching quality.
Biomechanical Maladaptation
Inefficient movement patterns placing excessive stress on body parts, increasing injury risk (e.g., knee valgus during landing).
üõ°Ô∏è Interventions
Prehabilitation
Proactive programmes preventing injuries by addressing risk factors through improved strength, balance and neuromuscular control.
Cryotherapy
Use of ice or cold for acute injury management, primarily reducing inflammation and providing pain relief.
POLICE Protocol
Acute injury treatment acronym: Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation.

Content summary

Topic B.3 examines the causes, prevention and management of sports injuries through an evidence-based lens, preparing you to analyse risk factors and evaluate interventions.

B.3.1 Causes of injury

Understanding why injuries occur is the first step to prevention; this subtopic examines internal and external risk factors that combine to create injury situations.

Internal risk factors

  • Age: Youth athletes face risks from growth spurts (bone‚Äìsoft tissue mismatch), active growth plates, and developmental variations creating strength mismatches.
  • Previous injury: Incompletely healed injuries or altered biomechanics post-injury increase re-injury risk.
  • Sex differences: Anatomical and hormonal differences (e.g., wider pelvis, Q-angle, hormonal effects on ligament laxity) contribute to higher ACL injury rates in females.
  • Congenital conditions: Structural abnormalities present from birth (e.g., joint hypermobility, limb length discrepancy) predispose to specific injuries.
  • Biomechanical maladaptations: Poor movement patterns (knee valgus, excessive pronation) place abnormal stress on structures.

External risk factors

  • Protective equipment: Inappropriate, poorly fitted or absent equipment increases injury severity and likelihood.
  • Playing surface: Surface type, condition and consistency affect force absorption and traction.
  • Coaching: Poor technical instruction, inadequate warm-up, inappropriate training loads increase injury risk.
  • Rules and officiating: Sport rules and their enforcement directly impact contact levels and dangerous play.
ACL injury mechanism: A female basketball player landing from a jump with knee valgus (internal risk: biomechanical maladaptation + sex-related Q-angle) on a worn court surface (external risk: poor playing surface) during rapid deceleration experiences combined rotational and compressive forces exceeding ligament strength, resulting in ACL rupture.
Injuries rarely result from single factors; they typically arise from complex interactions between multiple internal and external risks‚Äîthe "web of causation."

B.3.2 Interventions related to injury

Evidence-based interventions span prevention, acute management and rehabilitation; understanding their mechanisms and evaluating their effectiveness is essential for optimal athlete care.

Prevention interventions

  • Prehabilitation programmes: Structured neuromuscular training (FIFA 11+, Nordic hamstring exercises) addressing specific risk factors; evidence shows 20‚Äì30% injury reductions.
  • Equipment modifications: Properly fitted protective equipment (helmets, mouthguards, padding) reduces impact forces.
  • Rule changes: Legislative changes banning high-risk actions produce significant injury reductions when enforced.
  • Education: Teaching safe techniques, recognising injury signs, and understanding return-to-play protocols.

Acute injury management: POLICE protocol

  1. Protection: Prevent further damage through rest, splinting or protective gear.
  2. Optimal Loading: Gentle, progressive loading stimulates healing without causing damage.
  3. Ice (Cryotherapy): Reduces inflammation, pain and metabolic demand in injured tissues.
  4. Compression: Bandaging limits swelling by reducing fluid accumulation.
  5. Elevation: Raising injured limb above heart level uses gravity to aid venous and lymphatic drainage.

Rehabilitation and return to sport

  • Progressive loading: Gradual increase in stress applied to healing tissues promotes remodelling and functional recovery.
  • Range of motion exercises: Restore joint mobility and prevent adhesions.
  • Strength training: Rebuild muscle strength and endurance lost during injury period.
  • Proprioceptive training: Re-establish neuromuscular control and joint position sense.
  • Sport-specific training: Gradual reintroduction of movement patterns specific to the sport.
  • Graduated return-to-sport protocols: Structured, stage-based progressions (especially critical for concussion) ensuring safe return only when asymptomatic and functionally ready.
Concussion return-to-sport: A rugby player with diagnosed concussion follows six-stage protocol: (1) symptom-limited activity, (2) light aerobic exercise, (3) sport-specific drills without contact, (4) non-contact training, (5) full contact practice after medical clearance, (6) game return. Each stage requires 24 hours minimum; symptom return requires returning to previous stage. This graduated approach minimises second-impact syndrome risk.
Evaluating interventions: Always consider both effectiveness (does it reduce injuries?) and feasibility (cost, compliance, accessibility). Most effective strategies combine multiple interventions targeting different risk factors.

What is the primary mechanism by which cryotherapy (ice) helps manage acute soft tissue injuries?
  • A. Increases blood flow to promote healing
  • B. Reduces inflammation and metabolic demand
  • C. Strengthens damaged tissue structures
  • D. Prevents scar tissue formation
‚úÖ Correct answer: B
Cryotherapy works by vasoconstriction, reducing blood flow and thereby limiting inflammation and reducing the metabolic demands of injured tissue, which minimises secondary tissue damage. It does not increase blood flow (A), strengthen tissue (C), or directly prevent scarring (D).

Stage 3 introduces sport-specific exercises (e.g., skating drills in ice hockey, passing in soccer) but without any head impact activities. Stage 2 is light aerobic exercise; Stage 4 is non-contact training drills; Stage 5 is full contact practice after medical clearance.

Internal and external risk factors for ACL injury
Explain how internal and external risk factors can combine to increase the likelihood of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries in female basketball players.
üìã Model answer
Internal risk factors (3 marks):
Sex differences: Female athletes typically have a wider pelvis, creating a larger Q-angle (angle between quadriceps and patellar tendon), which increases valgus stress at the knee during landing and cutting movements. [1 mark]

Hormonal factors: Oestrogen affects collagen metabolism and ligament laxity; certain phases of the menstrual cycle may increase ACL vulnerability to loading forces. [1 mark]

Biomechanical maladaptations: Many female athletes demonstrate movement patterns including knee valgus during landing, reduced knee flexion angles, and quadriceps-dominant landing strategies, all increasing ACL strain. [1 mark]

External risk factors (3 marks):
Playing surface: Worn or excessively high-friction surfaces increase traction between shoe and floor, preventing natural foot rotation during cutting movements and transferring rotational forces to the knee. [1 mark]

Footwear: Inappropriate shoe design with excessive grip or poor ankle support can increase injury risk during rapid directional changes characteristic of basketball. [1 mark]

Coaching and training: Inadequate neuromuscular training, poor landing technique instruction, and insufficient emphasis on prehabilitation programmes fail to address modifiable biomechanical risk factors. [1 mark]

Evaluating injury prevention interventions
Evaluate the effectiveness of prehabilitation programmes as an intervention to reduce sports injuries in youth athletes.
üìã Model answer
Strengths of prehabilitation programmes (4 marks):

Evidence-based effectiveness: Multiple large-scale studies demonstrate significant injury reductions. The FIFA 11+ programme reduced overall injury rates by 21% and severe injuries by 40% in soccer players. The Oslo Sports Trauma Research Center programme showed similar reductions in handball. [2 marks]

Multi-factorial approach: These programmes address multiple risk factors simultaneously through neuromuscular training, balance exercises, plyometrics and strength training. This targets the complex, multi-causal nature of sports injuries more effectively than single-intervention approaches. For example, exercises improve muscle strength ratios (hamstring:quadriceps), enhance proprioception, and correct biomechanical maladaptations like knee valgus. [2 marks]

Limitations of prehabilitation programmes (4 marks):

Compliance and adherence: Effectiveness depends heavily on consistent implementation. Studies show injury reduction benefits diminish when programmes are performed less than twice weekly. Many teams struggle with long-term adherence due to time constraints, perceived low injury risk, or competing training priorities. Real-world effectiveness is often lower than controlled trial results. [2 marks]

Resource requirements: Implementation requires coach education, training time allocation (typically 15‚Äì20 minutes per session), and sometimes specialized equipment. This creates barriers for resource-limited organizations. Additionally, programmes may need sport-specific adaptation, and evidence is stronger for some sports (soccer, handball) than others. Individual athletes may respond differently based on baseline movement quality. [2 marks]

Common errors

Confusing acute and chronic injuries
Acute injuries occur suddenly from excessive force (fracture, concussion). Chronic injuries develop gradually from repeated stress (tendonitis, stress fractures). The distinction is about onset mechanism and timeframe, not severity.
Mixing up internal and external risk factors
Internal factors relate to the individual athlete (age, previous injury, anatomy). External factors relate to the environment (equipment, surface, coaching). Previous injury is internal; poor coaching is external.
Oversimplifying injury causation
Injuries rarely result from single causes; they typically involve complex interactions between multiple internal and external factors. Avoid attributing injuries to just one factor when analysing scenarios.
Confusing RICE with POLICE
The current evidence-based protocol is POLICE, not the outdated RICE. The key difference: "Optimal Loading" replaces "Rest," reflecting understanding that appropriate, early loading promotes better healing than complete rest.
Believing all protective equipment prevents injury
Equipment effectiveness depends on proper fitting, appropriate design for the specific sport/injury, correct usage, and maintenance. Poorly fitted or inappropriate equipment can create false confidence or even increase injury risk.
Rushing return to sport after concussion
Each stage of the concussion return-to-sport protocol requires minimum 24 hours, and any symptom return requires dropping back a stage. "Feeling fine" doesn't mean brain has fully healed; premature return risks second-impact syndrome.

Linking questions

How does understanding muscle function (B.1.3) relate to hamstring strain injuries (B.3)?
Hamstring strains often occur during eccentric contractions when the muscle lengthens under tension, such as during the late swing phase of sprinting. Understanding that eccentric contractions generate high forces while the muscle is in a vulnerable lengthened position explains why this is a high-risk scenario. Prehabilitation using Nordic hamstring exercises specifically strengthens muscles in eccentric mode, addressing this mechanism directly and reducing strain risk by up to 51%.
How do Newton's laws and impulse-momentum relationship (B.2.1) connect to injury mechanisms (B.3)?
The impulse-momentum relationship explains injury mechanics: sudden deceleration (large momentum change) over short time results in large forces on tissues. Teaching athletes to increase contact time when landing or absorbing impacts (flexing joints to increase deceleration time) reduces peak forces experienced by bones, ligaments and tendons, directly addressing acute injury risk through biomechanical principles.
How does psychological stress (C.4) link to injury risk (B.3)?
High stress levels correlate with increased injury rates through multiple mechanisms: stress causes muscle tension affecting movement quality, narrows attentional focus reducing peripheral awareness of hazards, impairs decision-making under pressure, and may reduce adherence to prehabilitation programmes. Psychological stress should be considered an internal risk factor that interacts with physical demands to influence overall injury risk.
