üíß A.2.1 Water and Electrolyte Balance
Dehydration
A state of net water loss from the body, leading to a negative water balance that can impair physiological function and performance.
Hypernatremia
A state of electrolyte imbalance characterized by an abnormally high concentration of sodium in the blood.
Hyponatremia
A state of electrolyte imbalance, characterized by low serum sodium, that can occur if water and electrolyte balance is not maintained.
Cardiovascular Drift
A phenomenon caused by water loss or increased core body temperature during prolonged submaximal exercise, characterized by a progressive increase in heart rate to compensate for a reduced stroke volume.

A syndrome that is a consequence of prolonged Low Energy Availability (LEA).

Content Summary

A.2.1 Water and Electrolyte Balance

Maintaining a stable internal environment, or homeostasis, is fundamental to human life. Water and electrolyte balance is a critical component of this, directly impacting everything from cellular function to cardiovascular performance.

üîÑ Regulation of Water Balance

  1. The hypothalamus in the brain continuously monitors the concentration of solutes in the blood.
  2. If blood becomes too concentrated (indicating dehydration), the hypothalamus signals the pituitary gland.
  3. The pituitary gland releases antidiuretic hormone (ADH) into the bloodstream.
  4. ADH travels to the kidneys and acts to increase water reabsorption, resulting in more concentrated urine and conservation of body water.

The case of a 23-year-old soldier illustrates the severe consequences of hydration imbalance. During training in a hot environment (30¬∞C), he collapsed. The likely cause of his subsequent hyponatremia (serum sodium of 132 mmol/l) was the excessive consumption of plain water without sufficient electrolyte replacement during intense exertion. His severe symptoms demonstrate how critical electrolyte balance is for neurological function and survival.

While the sensation of thirst is a signal to drink, it is often an unreliable and delayed indicator of the body's actual hydration needs. For exams, focus on objective measures like body weight change or urine colour.

A.2.3 Energy Systems

The body's capacity to perform any physical task is dictated by its ability to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the universal energy currency of the cell. This is accomplished through three distinct but interconnected energy systems.

All three energy systems (phosphagen, glycolytic, oxidative) are always active. The "energy continuum" concept explains that their relative contribution changes based on the intensity and duration of physical activity.

Question 1
In the case study of the 23-year-old soldier, which clinical finding was the primary indicator of hyponatremia?
  • A. Core temperature of 42¬∞C
  • B. Unresponsive state upon being found
  • C. Serum sodium of 132 mmol/l
  • D. Performing intense drills in full uniform
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: Hyponatremia is clinically defined as a low concentration of sodium in the blood. The soldier's serum sodium level of 132 mmol/l is below the normal range, directly confirming this diagnosis.

Why A is wrong: A core temperature of 42¬∞C is a sign of hyperthermia or heat stroke, not the specific indicator of hyponatremia.

Why B is wrong: An unresponsive state is a symptom that can result from various conditions. It's not the specific clinical measure that diagnoses hyponatremia.

Why D is wrong: Performing intense drills is a risk factor, not a clinical finding or indicator of the condition itself.
Question 2
What is the primary cause of the decrease in stroke volume associated with cardiovascular drift during prolonged exercise in a hot environment?
  • A. A decrease in heart rate
  • B. An increase in blood plasma volume
  • C. Vasoconstriction of blood vessels in the skin
  • D. Peripheral displacement of blood to the skin
‚úÖ Correct Answer: D
Why D is correct: A primary cause of cardiovascular drift is the peripheral displacement of blood to the skin to facilitate heat loss. This reduces venous return, leading to lower stroke volume.

Why A is wrong: Heart rate increases, not decreases, to compensate for falling stroke volume.

Why B is wrong: Blood plasma volume decreases due to sweating, contributing to reduced stroke volume.

Why C is wrong: Vasodilation (widening) of skin blood vessels occurs to promote heat loss, not vasoconstriction.
Question 3
An elite weightlifter performs a single, maximal "snatch," which takes less than 2 seconds to complete. Which energy system is predominantly responsible for producing the required ATP?
  • A. Glycolytic
  • B. Oxidative
  • C. Phosphagen
  • D. Lactic acid system
‚úÖ Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: The phosphagen system is the body's source for immediate, high-power energy production for activities lasting only a few seconds. A single, explosive lift like the snatch falls squarely into this category.

Why A is wrong: The glycolytic system becomes dominant in activities lasting longer, typically 30 seconds to 2 minutes.

Why B is wrong: The oxidative system is for low-intensity, long-duration activities. Its rate of ATP production is too slow for a maximal, explosive lift.

Why D is wrong: The "lactic acid system" is another name for the anaerobic glycolytic system. It is not the dominant system for such a short, maximal effort.
Question 4 HL ONLY
What does the slow component of EPOC primarily address?
  • A. The immediate replenishment of ATP and phosphocreatine stores
  • B. The removal of lactate and reduction of elevated core body temperature
  • C. The re-oxygenation of myoglobin in the muscles
  • D. The initial increase in oxygen uptake at the start of exercise
‚úÖ Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: The slow component of EPOC involves metabolic processes that take longer to complete, including the conversion of lactate to glycogen, lowering the elevated core body temperature, and clearing hormones like epinephrine.

Why A is wrong: Replenishing ATP and phosphocreatine stores is part of the fast component of EPOC.

Why C is wrong: The re-oxygenation of myoglobin is also part of the fast component of EPOC.

Why D is wrong: The initial increase in oxygen uptake at the start of exercise is known as the oxygen deficit, not EPOC.

Part (a)

Using the data, estimate the cyclist's lactate inflection point (LIP).

üìã Model Answer (a):
The lactate inflection point is the point where blood lactate begins to accumulate exponentially. In the data, the increase from 250W to 300W is 0.9 mmol/L, but the increase from 300W to 350W is much larger at 1.7 mmol/L. Explain the physiological significance of training at or near the lactate inflection point for an endurance athlete.

üìã Model Answer (b):
Training at or near the LIP is physiologically significant because it stimulates adaptations that improve the body's ability to clear and tolerate lactate. This enhances the oxidative capacity of muscles, increases the density of capillaries and mitochondria, and ultimately raises the LIP. A higher LIP allows the athlete to sustain a higher intensity of exercise before significant metabolic fatigue sets in, which is a crucial determinant of endurance performance.

üìã Model Answer (c):
One limitation is the lack of ecological validity. A laboratory test on a stationary bike involves a controlled environment with steady power output increases, which does not replicate the dynamic conditions of a real race, such as changes in gradient, wind resistance, drafting behind other riders, and tactical surges in pace. Therefore, while it is a good measure of physiological capacity, it may not perfectly predict how an athlete performs under variable race conditions.

Question 9 4 MARKS
Water and Electrolyte Balance in Endurance Competition
An athlete is competing in a triathlon in a hot and humid environment. Explain two distinct physiological reasons why maintaining water and electrolyte balance is critical for their performance and safety.
üìã Model Answer:
Reason 1: Thermoregulation
One reason is for effective thermoregulation. In hot and humid conditions, the primary mechanism for heat loss is the evaporation of sweat. If an athlete becomes dehydrated, their plasma volume decreases, which impairs their ability to sweat effectively [1 mark]. This can lead to a dangerous increase in core body temperature, significantly impairing performance and increasing the risk of heat-related illnesses such as heat stroke [1 mark].

Reason 2: Cardiovascular Function and Muscle Contraction
A second reason is to maintain cardiovascular function. Dehydration leads to a reduction in blood plasma volume, which lowers stroke volume and causes cardiovascular drift [1 mark]. Furthermore, the loss of key electrolytes like sodium and potassium through sweat is critical, as these are essential for proper nerve impulse transmission and muscle contraction. A severe imbalance can lead to muscle cramps, cardiac arrhythmias, and a significant drop in performance [1 mark].
Question 10 HL ONLY 8 MARKS
Nutritional Planning for Marathon Performance
A coach is designing a nutrition and recovery plan for a long-distance runner in the week leading up to a marathon. Analyse the nutritional strategies the coach should recommend for pre-race fuelling and post-race recovery. Your answer should refer to both macronutrients and micronutrients.
üìã Model Answer:
Pre-Race Fuelling Strategy:

The primary pre-race goal is to maximize muscle glycogen stores, which is the main fuel for a marathon. The coach should recommend a carbohydrate-loading strategy, where the runner consumes a very high carbohydrate diet (e.g., 8-10g per kg of body weight) for the 2-3 days prior to the race [1 mark]. This should be combined with a taper in training volume to allow glycogen to be stored rather than used [1 mark].

The final pre-race meal, consumed 3-4 hours before the start, should be rich in carbohydrates but low in fat and fibre to ensure rapid gastric emptying and prevent gastrointestinal distress [1 mark]. Adequate hydration is also essential, and consuming fluids with electrolytes like sodium can help with fluid retention [1 mark].

Post-Race Recovery Strategy:

Post-race recovery should begin immediately to replenish depleted stores and repair damaged tissue. The coach should advise the runner to consume a source of high-glycaemic index carbohydrates within the first 30-60 minutes post-race to rapidly restock muscle glycogen [1 mark]. This should be combined with a source of protein (approx. 20-25g) to initiate the repair of muscle fibres damaged during the race [1 mark].

Rehydration is critical and should involve consuming fluids containing electrolytes, particularly sodium and potassium, to replace what was lost in sweat and restore water balance [1 mark]. For micronutrients, consuming polyphenol-rich foods like berries can help reduce inflammation, while ensuring adequate iron intake in the long term is vital for restoring haemoglobin levels for oxygen transport, aiding long-term recovery [1 mark].

Common Errors & Misconceptions

‚ùå Error: Energy systems work in isolation
This is incorrect. All three energy systems (phosphagen, glycolytic, oxidative) are always contributing to ATP production. The "energy continuum" concept highlights that their relative contribution changes based on intensity and duration, but they never work completely in isolation.
‚ùå Error: Thirst is a reliable indicator of dehydration
The sensation of thirst is a delayed response. By the time an individual feels thirsty, they are often already in a state of dehydration, and performance may have already been compromised. This is why structured hydration plans are emphasized over simply drinking to thirst.
‚ùå Error: Confusing dehydration and hyponatremia
Dehydration is primarily caused by excessive fluid loss. Hyponatremia is caused by abnormally low blood sodium levels, often from drinking excessive amounts of plain water without adequate electrolyte replacement. While both can occur in athletes, their causes and treatments are very different.
