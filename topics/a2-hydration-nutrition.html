  1. CONTENT SUMMARY

A.2.1 Water and Electrolyte Balance

Maintaining a stable internal environment, or homeostasis, is fundamental to human life. Water and electrolyte balance is a critical component of this, directly impacting everything from cellular function to cardiovascular performance. For an athlete, particularly one competing in challenging environmental conditions, the ability to manage hydration status can be the difference between success and failure, and is essential for safety.

  • Key Concepts
    • Intake and Loss: Water and electrolytes are primarily taken in via the large intestine from food and drink. They are lost from the body through evaporation from the skin (sweat) and respiratory tract, as well as excretion via osmosis in the kidneys.
    • Imbalance States: Failure to maintain this balance can lead to three critical states: dehydration (net water loss), hypernatremia (abnormally high blood sodium), and hyponatremia (abnormally low blood sodium).
    • Measurement: Hydration status can be assessed through various methods, including monitoring changes in body weight, observing urine colour, and measuring urine osmolarity.
  • Mechanism Explained: Regulation of Water Balance The body has a sophisticated negative feedback system to maintain water balance:
    1. The hypothalamus in the brain continuously monitors the concentration of solutes in the blood.
    2. If blood becomes too concentrated (indicating dehydration), the hypothalamus signals the pituitary gland.
    3. The pituitary gland releases antidiuretic hormone (ADH) into the bloodstream.
    4. ADH travels to the kidneys and acts to increase water reabsorption, resulting in more concentrated urine and the conservation of body water.
  • Mechanism Explained: Cardiovascular Drift During prolonged, steady-state exercise, a phenomenon known as cardiovascular drift occurs:
    1. As exercise continues, core body temperature rises. To cool down, the body diverts more blood to the skin (vasodilation).
    2. This peripheral displacement of blood means less blood is available to return to the heart, reducing venous return and ventricular filling time.
    3. Simultaneously, fluid is lost as sweat, which reduces total blood plasma volume.
    4. These factors combine to cause a progressive decrease in stroke volume (the amount of blood pumped per beat).
    5. To maintain a constant cardiac output (blood flow) and supply the working muscles with oxygen, the heart rate gradually increases to compensate for the falling stroke volume.
  • Real-World Example The case of a 23-year-old soldier illustrates the severe consequences of hydration imbalance. During training in a hot environment (30°C), he collapsed. The likely cause of his subsequent hyponatremia (a low serum sodium of 132 mmol/l) was the excessive consumption of plain water without sufficient electrolyte replacement during intense exertion. His severe symptoms, including being unresponsive and having persistent altered mental status, demonstrate how critical electrolyte balance is for neurological function and survival in high-stress situations.
  • Data Booklet Reference While water intake occurs via the large intestine, a detailed diagram of the Digestive System (Section 5) can be found in the SEHS data booklet for anatomical context.

NOTE: Exam Tip Note that while the sensation of thirst is a signal to drink, it is often an unreliable and delayed indicator of the body's actual hydration needs. For exams, focus on objective measures like body weight change or urine colour.

Maintaining fluid balance is critical, but performance also depends on the fuel available to power the body's systems.

A.2.2 Fuelling for Health and Performance

Proper nutrition is the cornerstone of both general health and elite athletic performance. Macronutrients provide the essential fuel for energy production, while micronutrients act as the regulators for countless metabolic processes. Understanding how to strategically manage nutritional intake allows an individual to optimize performance, enhance recovery, and maintain long-term well-being.

  • Key Concepts (SL & HL)
    • Macronutrient Roles: Carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids are the body's primary energy sources, and their relative contribution to energy production depends on factors like an individual’s age, sex, body composition, and activity level.
    • Nutritional Strategies: The timing and composition of macronutrient intake before and during exercise can significantly impact performance and minimize gastrointestinal (GI) discomfort.
    • Low Energy Availability (LEA): This is a state where the body has insufficient energy intake to meet the demands of exercise and support basic physiological functions.
    • Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S): This is a syndrome of impaired physiological function including, but not limited to, metabolic rate, menstrual function, bone health, immunity, and cardiovascular health, which is caused by prolonged LEA.
  • Real-World Example A marathon runner's nutritional strategy is meticulously planned. In the days leading up to the race, they might "carbohydrate load" to maximize muscle glycogen stores. On race day, a pre-race meal high in carbohydrates and low in fat and fibre ensures readily available energy without causing GI distress. During the race, they will consume carbohydrate gels or drinks at regular intervals to maintain blood glucose levels, spare their limited glycogen stores, and delay the onset of fatigue.
  • Mechanism Explained: LEA and RED-S When an individual consistently expends more energy than they consume, they enter a state of Low Energy Availability (LEA). The body responds to this energy deficit by suppressing non-essential physiological processes to conserve energy for survival. If this state is prolonged, it leads to the clinical syndrome of RED-S, where multiple body systems (e.g., endocrine, skeletal, immune) are negatively affected, severely compromising both health and performance.

TIP: Nuanced Application When discussing nutritional strategies, always link your points to the specific demands of the activity (e.g., intensity, duration) and the individual (e.g., age, sex, training status) to demonstrate a nuanced understanding.

Additional Higher Level Content

A.2.2.2 Micronutrients (HL Only)

Micronutrients do not provide energy directly but are vital for facilitating energy transfer and tissue synthesis.

  • Iron: A crucial component of haemoglobin (in red blood cells) and myoglobin (in muscle cells), essential for transporting oxygen for aerobic respiration.
  • Calcium: A primary component of bones and connective tissue, and also plays a key role in the process of muscle contraction.
  • Sodium and Potassium: These key electrolytes are essential for maintaining the body's water balance and are critical for proper muscle and nerve function.
  • Vitamins: Act as regulators of the metabolic reactions that release energy from food and support the synthesis of new tissues.

A.2.2.3 The Gut Microbiome (HL Only)

The gut microbiome, the vast community of microorganisms residing in our digestive tract, is a key influencer of health and performance. Its composition is affected by an individual's genetics, diet, medications, and overall lifestyle. A healthy and diverse microbiome affects the availability and uptake of nutrients from food, thereby influencing an individual's energy levels, immune function, and overall well-being.

The fuel provided by nutrition is converted into a usable form of energy, ATP, through three distinct energy systems.

A.2.3 Energy Systems

The body's capacity to perform any physical task, from an explosive jump to a multi-hour endurance event, is dictated by its ability to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the universal energy currency of the cell. This is accomplished through three distinct but interconnected energy systems, which work together along a continuum to meet the energy demands of any given activity.

  • Key Concepts (SL & HL)
    • Three Systems:
      1. Phosphagen System: Provides immediate ATP from stored phosphocreatine. It is powerful but has a very limited capacity (lasts for seconds) and is dominant in short, explosive activities.
      2. Glycolytic System: Breaks down carbohydrates (glycogen and glucose) anaerobically to produce ATP. It is dominant in high-intensity activities lasting from 30 seconds to about 2 minutes. Its primary limitation is the production of metabolic by-products that contribute to fatigue.
      3. Oxidative System: Uses oxygen to break down carbohydrates and fats to produce large amounts of ATP. It is the dominant system at rest and during submaximal, long-duration activities, with limitations related to the rate of oxygen delivery.
    • Energy Continuum: This concept explains that all three energy systems are always active, but their relative contribution changes based on the intensity and duration of the physical activity.
    • VO₂ max: Defined as an individual's maximal oxygen consumption, it represents the upper limit of the oxidative system and is a key determinant of endurance performance.
  • Mechanism Explained: The Energy Continuum The interplay between the energy systems is dynamic. For example:
    • A 100m sprint is fueled almost exclusively by the phosphagen system due to the immediate, high-power demand.
    • A 400m race begins with the phosphagen system, but the sustained high intensity quickly makes the glycolytic system the dominant contributor.
    • A marathon is performed at a submaximal intensity and is fueled primarily by the oxidative system, which can sustainably produce ATP for hours.
  • Real-World Example A professional soccer player seamlessly transitions between all three energy systems during a 90-minute match. A sudden sprint to intercept a pass relies on the phosphagen system. A sustained dribbling run past defenders is powered by the glycolytic system. The vast majority of the game, including jogging into position and walking, is supported by the oxidative system, which also facilitates recovery between high-intensity bursts.

Additional Higher Level Content

A.2.3.3 Lactate Inflection Point (HL Only)

The lactate inflection point (LIP) is defined as the maximum exercise intensity at which the body can clear or metabolize lactate at the same rate at which it is being produced. For an endurance athlete, exercising at or just below the LIP represents the highest sustainable intensity. Training to increase one's LIP is a key goal as it allows the athlete to maintain a higher pace for longer before fatiguing metabolic by-products accumulate rapidly.

A.2.3.4 Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC) (HL Only)

Following exercise, the body's oxygen consumption remains elevated above resting levels. This is known as EPOC. Its purpose is to restore the body to its pre-exercise state (homeostasis). EPOC is divided into two parts: a fast component, which involves restoring ATP and phosphocreatine stores and re-oxygenating myoglobin, and a slow component, which deals with tasks like lactate removal, elevated body temperature, and circulating hormones. The magnitude and duration of EPOC are dependent on the oxygen deficit incurred during the exercise bout.

  1. MATHEMATICAL SKILLS

While SEHS is a science course, not a mathematics course, the ability to perform key calculations, handle data, and understand quantitative relationships is essential. This skill allows you to interpret experimental results and apply theoretical principles in a practical context. One of the most common calculations you will encounter is percentage change.

  • Formula: Percentage Change Percentage Change = ((Final Value - Initial Value) / Initial Value) × 100
  • Worked Example Scenario: An athlete's body mass decreases from 75.0 kg before a training session to 72.9 kg after the session due to fluid loss. We want to calculate the percentage change in their body mass.
    • Initial Value = 75.0 kg
    • Final Value = 72.9 kg
  • Calculation Steps
    1. Substitute the values into the formula: Percentage Change = ((72.9 kg - 75.0 kg) / 75.0 kg) × 100
    2. Calculate the difference (the change in mass): 72.9 - 75.0 = -2.1 kg
    3. Divide the change by the initial value: -2.1 kg / 75.0 kg = -0.028
    4. Multiply by 100 to express as a percentage: -0.028 × 100 = -2.8%

  • Final Answer with Correct Units: The athlete experienced a 2.8% decrease in body mass.

  • COMMON ERRORS

Awareness of common misconceptions can significantly strengthen your understanding and help you avoid pitfalls in exam questions. Addressing these errors directly in your answers can demonstrate a higher level of insight.

  • Error: Energy systems work in isolation, not as a continuum. Explanation: This is incorrect. All three energy systems (phosphagen, glycolytic, oxidative) are always contributing to ATP production. The "energy continuum" concept highlights that their relative contribution changes based on the intensity and duration of the activity, but they never work completely in isolation.
  • Error: Thirst is a reliable and early indicator of dehydration. Explanation: The sensation of thirst is a delayed response. By the time an individual feels thirsty, they are often already in a state of dehydration, and performance may have already been compromised. This is why structured hydration plans are emphasized over simply drinking to thirst during prolonged exercise.
  • Error: Confusing the causes and symptoms of dehydration and hyponatremia. Explanation: Dehydration is primarily caused by excessive fluid loss, leading to a net deficit of body water. Hyponatremia is caused by abnormally low blood sodium levels, often from drinking excessive amounts of plain water without adequate electrolyte replacement, which dilutes blood sodium. While both can occur in athletes, their causes and treatments are very different.
  • Error: Cardiovascular drift is caused only by dehydration. Explanation: While dehydration is a major contributor, cardiovascular drift is multifactorial. A primary cause is the rise in core body temperature, which leads to vasodilation of skin blood vessels to promote cooling. As stated in the textbook, some researchers also suggest that a reduction in cardiac performance (contractility) may be a contributing factor. The phenomenon cannot be explained by a single cause.
  • Error: RED-S is a condition that only affects female athletes. Explanation: Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is a consequence of prolonged Low Energy Availability and affects athletes of all genders. While some consequences, such as menstrual dysfunction, are specific to females, males can experience impaired physiological functions including reduced testosterone levels, poor bone health, and decreased metabolic rate.

  • Error (HL Only): EPOC is simply "repaying an oxygen debt." Explanation: This is an outdated and oversimplified term. Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC) is a complex process involving multiple physiological functions to restore the body to homeostasis. It includes a fast component (restoring ATP/PCr stores) and a slow component (removing lactate, reducing body temperature, etc.), reflecting a broad range of recovery processes, not just repaying a simple debt.

  • LINKING QUESTIONS

A key skill in the IB Diploma Programme is the ability to make connections between different topics. This demonstrates a holistic and networked understanding of Sports, Exercise, and Health Science, moving beyond isolated facts. The following questions challenge you to link concepts from Topic A.2 with other areas of the syllabus.

  • Linking Question: How does A.2.1 (Water and electrolyte balance) link to Topic A.1.2 HL (The body's response to the environment)? Model Answer: The body's response to a hot and humid environment (A.1.2 HL) directly impacts water and electrolyte balance (A.2.1). In such an environment, the primary mechanism for thermoregulation is sweating, which causes a significant loss of both water and electrolytes like sodium. This makes maintaining hydration status much more challenging and critical for performance and safety, and is a key driver for the long-term adaptations seen with heat acclimatization.
  • Linking Question: How does A.2.2 (Fuelling for health and performance) link to Topic A.3.1 (Qualities of training)? Model Answer: The training principle of periodization (A.3.1), which involves varying training volume and intensity in macro-, meso-, and microcycles, must be aligned with nutritional periodization (A.2.2). An athlete's energy and macronutrient requirements will be significantly higher during a high-volume "overload" microcycle compared to a "recovery" or "taper" microcycle. Failing to match nutritional intake to training demands can lead to Low Energy Availability (LEA), impairing adaptation and increasing the risk of overtraining.

  • Linking Question: How do A.2.3 (Energy systems) link to Topic B.1.3 (Muscular function)? Model Answer: The process of muscular contraction, explained by the sliding filament theory (B.1.3), is fundamentally dependent on the availability of ATP to power the cross-bridge cycle between actin and myosin filaments. The three energy systems (A.2.3) are the metabolic pathways responsible for continuously resynthesizing this ATP. The specific energy system that is dominant dictates the rate and sustainability of ATP production, thereby determining the intensity and duration of muscular work that can be performed.
