Quick Reference Key Terms

In Sports, Exercise, and Health Science (SEHS), the use of precise and accurate terminology is fundamental. Clear scientific communication allows for the effective exchange of complex physiological concepts, which is essential for both practical application in health and sport and for achieving success in your IB examinations. Misunderstanding a key term can lead to a fundamental error in an extended response or data analysis. This section provides a focused glossary of essential terms for Topic A.2 to build your foundational knowledge.

Fluid and Electrolyte Balance
Dehydration
A state that occurs when fluid loss exceeds fluid intake, resulting in a deficit of body water.
Hyponatremia
A condition characterized by abnormally low sodium levels in the blood, often caused by excessive water consumption without adequate sodium replacement.
Hypernatremia
A condition characterized by abnormally high sodium levels in the blood, typically caused by insufficient water intake.
Cardiovascular Drift
The progressive increase in heart rate and decrease in stroke volume that begins after approximately 10 minutes of prolonged submaximal exercise, particularly in warm environments.
Osmolarity
A measure of the concentration of solute particles per unit volume of solution, used to assess hydration status.
Energy for Performance
Macronutrients
The essential nutrients required in large amounts for energy and bodily functions, comprising carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids.
Low Energy Availability (LEA)
A state where the body has insufficient energy to support all physiological functions required for optimal health and performance.
Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)
The syndrome of impaired physiological function including, but not limited to, metabolic rate, menstrual function, bone health, immunity, and cardiovascular health caused by prolonged LEA.
Energy Continuum
A concept describing how the body's three energy systems (phosphagen, glycolytic, oxidative) work simultaneously to produce ATP, with the relative contribution of each system depending on the intensity and duration of the activity.
VO‚ÇÇ Max (Maximal Oxygen Consumption)
The maximum rate at which an individual's body can take in and use oxygen during intense exercise, representing their aerobic capacity.
Lactate Inflection Point (HL Only)
The exercise intensity at which the concentration of blood lactate begins to increase exponentially, as its rate of production exceeds its rate of removal.
Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC) (HL Only)
The increased rate of oxygen uptake following strenuous activity, required to restore the body to its resting state.

Explain (Topic A.2)

Q: Explain the causes of cardiovascular drift during prolonged aerobic exercise in a hot environment.

A: Cardiovascular drift is caused by a combination of factors that reduce stroke volume and prompt a compensatory increase in heart rate to maintain cardiac output. Firstly, as core body temperature rises, the body initiates thermoregulatory responses, causing peripheral vasodilation to increase blood flow to the skin for heat dissipation. This redirects a significant volume of blood away from central circulation, reducing venous return to the heart. Secondly, fluid is lost as sweat, which decreases overall blood plasma volume. The combination of reduced venous return and lower blood volume leads to a decreased ventricular filling and consequently a lower stroke volume. To compensate for this fall in stroke volume and maintain the necessary cardiac output to fuel the working muscles, the sinoatrial node stimulates an increase in heart rate.

Analyse (Topic A.2)

Q: Analyse the relative contributions of the three energy systems for a marathon runner.

A: For a marathon runner, the dominant energy system is the oxidative system, which provides the vast majority of ATP over the multi-hour event by metabolizing carbohydrates and fats in the presence of oxygen. The glycolytic system is crucial at the start, during surges in pace to overtake another runner, or for a finishing kick, where it provides ATP more rapidly but produces lactate as a by-product. The phosphagen system's contribution is minimal, limited to the very first few seconds of the race from the starting line before the other systems can increase their ATP production rate.

Content Summary

A.2.1 Water and Electrolyte Balance

Maintaining a stable internal environment, or homeostasis, is critical for survival and optimal performance. Water and electrolyte balance is a cornerstone of this process, as fluid is the medium for countless metabolic reactions and plays a central role in thermoregulation. For an athlete, even a minor imbalance can significantly impair physiological function, reducing endurance, skill, and cognitive performance. Understanding the mechanisms of fluid regulation and the consequences of imbalance is therefore strategically vital for anyone involved in sports, exercise, and health.

Key Concepts

  • Water and electrolytes are taken in through food and drink and absorbed primarily in the large intestine (A.2.1.1) (p. 112).
  • Fluid and electrolytes are lost from the body through sweat, respiration (evaporation), and excretion from the kidneys (A.2.1.1) (p. 112).
  • An imbalance between intake and loss can lead to dehydration (net water loss), hyponatremia (low blood sodium), or hypernatremia (high blood sodium) (A.2.1.1) (p. 113).
  • Hydration status can be assessed through various methods, including changes in body weight, analysis of urine colour, and measuring urine osmolarity (A.2.1.1) (p. 113).
  • Cardiovascular drift occurs during prolonged submaximal exercise, especially in hot conditions, and is driven by dehydration and increased core body temperature (A.2.1.1) (p. 114).
  • The regulation of water and electrolyte balance is a complex process controlled by the hypothalamus, pituitary gland (releasing ADH), and the kidneys (A.2.1.1) (p. 114).

Mechanism Explained: The Physiological Cascade of Dehydration

When an athlete exercises, particularly in a warm environment, dehydration initiates a series of negative physiological events that progressively impair performance:

  1. Sweat Production and Fluid Loss: The primary mechanism for thermoregulation during exercise is sweating. The evaporation of sweat from the skin cools the body, but it also leads to a significant loss of body water.
  2. Reduced Blood Plasma Volume: As water is lost from the body, the volume of blood plasma decreases. This makes the blood more viscous (thicker), forcing the heart to work harder to pump it through the circulatory system.
  3. Decreased Stroke Volume: The reduction in plasma volume leads to a lower venous return (less blood returning to the heart). This means the heart's ventricles do not fill as completely before contracting, resulting in a decreased stroke volume (the amount of blood pumped per beat).
  4. Cardiovascular Drift: To maintain the necessary cardiac output (Cardiac Output = Heart Rate √ó Stroke Volume) to supply oxygen to working muscles, the heart rate must increase to compensate for the falling stroke volume. This phenomenon is known as cardiovascular drift. It is now understood to be a multifactorial phenomenon, where the initial reduction in stroke volume is caused by both the decrease in blood plasma volume from sweating and the peripheral displacement of blood to the skin for thermoregulation.
  5. Impaired Thermoregulation: As dehydration becomes more severe, the body reduces its sweat rate to conserve remaining water. This compromises its ability to cool itself, leading to a rapid and dangerous increase in core body temperature.
  6. Performance Decline: The combined effects of increased cardiovascular strain, reduced oxygen delivery, and rising core temperature lead to a significant decline in aerobic performance, reduced cognitive function, and an increased risk of heat-related illness.
Real-World Example
A marathon runner competing on a hot day begins to feel their pace falter around the 30 km mark. Despite drinking water at aid stations, their sweat rate has been extremely high. The progressive fluid loss has caused their heart rate to climb steadily even though their pace is slowing (cardiovascular drift). Their reduced blood volume impairs heat dissipation, and their core temperature rises, making them feel dizzy and weak. Their performance is now severely limited not by muscle fuel, but by the physiological consequences of dehydration.
EXAM TIP: Validity of Hydration Measures
Be prepared to evaluate the different methods of assessing hydration. While monitoring body weight changes is considered a highly valid and reliable method in a controlled setting, it can be impractical in the field. Conversely, a urine colour chart is highly practical but less valid, as its accuracy can be affected by diet and vitamin supplements. Knowing the strengths and limitations of each is key for evaluation questions.

This delicate balance of fluids and electrolytes is critically dependent on the fuel the body has available, linking directly to nutrition.

A.2.2 Fuelling for Health and Performance (SL and HL)

Macronutrients‚Äîcarbohydrates, proteins, and lipids‚Äîare the fundamental energy sources that power every bodily function, from basic metabolic processes to explosive athletic movements. The strategic management of macronutrient intake is therefore essential not only for optimizing athletic performance but also for maintaining long-term health. Insufficient energy intake can lead to a cascade of negative health consequences known as Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), highlighting the critical importance of aligning nutritional strategies with the specific demands of an individual's activity level.

Key Concepts

  • The relative contribution of carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids for energy depends on the intensity and duration of activity, as well as individual factors like age, sex, and body composition (A.2.2.1) (p. 115).
  • Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise, while lipids are the main source during rest and low-intensity exercise (p. 115).
  • Nutritional strategies before and during exercise, such as carbohydrate loading or consuming sports drinks, can enhance performance and prevent gastrointestinal distress (A.2.2.1) (p. 116).
  • Low Energy Availability (LEA) occurs when energy intake is insufficient to cover the energy expenditure required for health, daily function, and training (A.2.2.1) (p. 117).
  • Prolonged LEA can lead to Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), a syndrome of impaired physiological function affecting metabolic rate, menstrual function, bone health, and more (A.2.2.1) (p. 117).

Mechanism Explained: Macronutrient Timing for Performance

The timing of macronutrient intake relative to exercise is a key strategy for maximizing energy availability and minimizing gastrointestinal (GI) discomfort.

  1. Pre-Exercise (1-4 hours before): The primary goal is to top off muscle and liver glycogen stores. A meal rich in complex carbohydrates (e.g., pasta, rice, oats) is ideal. Consuming this meal several hours before allows time for digestion and absorption, reducing the risk of GI issues like cramping or bloating during the event. Fat and fibre should be minimized as they slow digestion.
  2. During-Exercise (for events >60-90 minutes): As muscle glycogen is depleted, consuming easily digestible carbohydrates (e.g., from sports gels, chews, or drinks) is crucial to maintain blood glucose levels and provide fuel for the working muscles. This spares the body's limited glycogen stores, delaying fatigue. The focus is on simple sugars that can be absorbed rapidly with minimal digestive effort.
  3. Post-Exercise (within 30-60 minutes after): This is the critical window for recovery. Consuming a combination of carbohydrates and protein is essential. The carbohydrates replenish the depleted muscle and liver glycogen stores, while the protein provides the amino acids necessary to repair muscle tissue damaged during exercise and stimulate muscle protein synthesis for adaptation and growth.
Real-World Example
A competitive cyclist prepares for a 100 km road race. The night before, they have a large pasta dinner (carb-loading) to maximize glycogen stores. On race morning, they eat a bowl of oatmeal 3 hours before the start. During the race, they consume one energy gel every 45 minutes to provide a steady stream of simple carbohydrates, preventing a drop in blood sugar. Immediately after finishing, they drink a recovery shake containing both carbohydrates and protein to kick-start the recovery process.
NOTE: RED-S is not just about performance
A common misconception is that LEA and RED-S only affect elite, lean athletes. This is incorrect. It can affect athletes of any level, age, or body shape, in any sport. It's a health issue first and a performance issue second. Be sure to emphasize the wide-ranging health consequences (e.g., impaired bone health, menstrual dysfunction) in your exam answers.

Additional Higher Level Content

Micronutrients and the Gut Microbiome

While macronutrients provide the fuel, micronutrients (vitamins and minerals) and the gut microbiome act as the body's essential support crew, facilitating energy transfer, tissue synthesis, and overall health.

Key Concepts (HL)

  • Micronutrients like iron (for oxygen transport in haemoglobin), calcium (for bone health and muscle contraction), and sodium/potassium (for nerve function and fluid balance) are critical for performance (A.2.2.2) (p. 118).
  • Vitamins act as co-enzymes and regulators in metabolic reactions that release energy (A.2.2.2) (p. 118).
  • The gut microbiome, the vast community of microorganisms in our digestive tract, is influenced by genetics, diet, and lifestyle (A.2.2.3) (p. 119).
  • A healthy and diverse gut microbiome is crucial for the effective absorption and synthesis of nutrients, thereby impacting both health and athletic performance (A.2.2.3) (p. 119).

Mechanism Explained: The Gut-Brain-Performance Axis

The connection between the gut and athletic performance is complex and bidirectional.

  1. Nutrient Availability: The microbiome breaks down complex carbohydrates and fibres that our bodies cannot digest on their own, producing vital short-chain fatty acids that can be used for energy and to maintain gut health. It also aids in the synthesis of certain vitamins.
  2. Immune Function: A large portion of the body's immune system resides in the gut. A healthy microbiome helps regulate immune responses, potentially reducing an athlete's susceptibility to illness during periods of heavy training.
  3. Stress and Anxiety: The gut-brain axis is a communication network linking the gut and the brain. Psychological stress can negatively alter the composition of the gut microbiome, which in turn can lead to GI distress, affecting an athlete's ability to fuel and hydrate properly during competition.
Real-World Example (HL)
An elite runner suffers from frequent GI distress during races, which compromises their fuelling strategy. Working with a sports dietitian, they focus on increasing the diversity of their diet, incorporating more fibre-rich plant foods and fermented products (like yogurt and kimchi) to support a healthier gut microbiome. Over time, this dietary change improves their digestive function, reduces race-day GI issues, and allows them to fuel more effectively, leading to improved performance.
STUDY TIP (HL): Think Integration
The gut microbiome is an excellent topic for linking questions. Consider how psychological stress (Topic C.4) could impact the microbiome and, consequently, an athlete's ability to absorb nutrients (Topic A.2). This demonstrates a deeper, more integrated understanding of SEHS.

The energy derived from these nutritional sources is ultimately converted into ATP by the body's energy systems.

A.2.3 Energy Systems (SL and HL)

The ability to perform any physical activity, from a powerful weightlift to a marathon, depends on the body's capacity to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the universal currency of energy. The body has three distinct but interconnected energy systems that work together along a continuum to meet the ATP demands of any given activity. Understanding how these systems contribute, their limitations, and how they are measured is fundamental to designing effective training programs and explaining the physiological basis of athletic performance.

Key Concepts

  • The body uses three energy systems to produce ATP: the phosphagen system, the glycolytic system, and the oxidative system (A.2.3.1) (p. 121).
  • These systems work simultaneously, but their relative contribution depends on the intensity and duration of the exercise, a concept known as the energy continuum (A.2.3.1) (p. 121).
  • The phosphagen system provides immediate ATP for very short, maximal intensity efforts (e.g., a 100m sprint), while the oxidative system is the dominant provider for long-duration, submaximal activity (e.g., a long-distance run) (A.2.3.1) (p. 122).
  • Maximal oxygen consumption (VO‚ÇÇ max) represents the maximum capacity of the oxidative system and is a key indicator of aerobic fitness (A.2.3.2) (p. 123).
  • An individual's VO‚ÇÇ max is influenced by factors including age, sex, genetics, and training status (A.2.3.2) (p. 123).

Mechanism Explained: The Energy Continuum in a 400-meter Race

A 400-meter race is a classic example of the dynamic interplay between the energy systems.

  1. Start (0-5 seconds): The instant the starting gun fires, the demand for ATP is immediate and maximal. The Phosphagen System is the dominant energy provider, using stored ATP and creatine phosphate to fuel the explosive acceleration out of the blocks. This system is powerful but extremely limited, lasting only a few seconds.
  2. Middle (5-45 seconds): As creatine phosphate stores are depleted, the Glycolytic System becomes the dominant pathway. It rapidly breaks down muscle glycogen into ATP without oxygen (anaerobically). This allows the athlete to maintain a very high speed, but it also produces metabolic by-products like hydrogen ions, which contribute to the sensation of muscular fatigue and "burning".
  3. Finish (45 seconds onwards): Throughout the entire race, the Oxidative System has been increasing its ATP production. While it is too slow to meet the demands of the initial sprint, it makes a significant contribution in the latter stages of the race. However, the intensity of a 400m race is too high for the oxidative system to be dominant, resulting in a large oxygen deficit and severe physiological distress by the finish line. The athlete's ability to tolerate the metabolic acidosis from glycolysis is a key determinant of their performance.
Real-World Example
A 100m sprinter relies almost exclusively on the phosphagen system for their event, which lasts less than 10 seconds. In contrast, a 10,000m runner derives over 95% of their ATP from the oxidative system, as their event is long and performed at a submaximal intensity. The 400m or 800m runner sits in the middle, relying heavily on a highly trained glycolytic system to sustain a near-maximal pace for a minute or more.
CLARIFICATION: Systems Work in Parallel, Not in Sequence
A common mistake is to think of the energy systems as "switching on and off" one after another. This is incorrect. All three systems are active at the start of any exercise. The concept of the energy continuum is about their relative contribution changing based on intensity and duration.

Additional Higher Level Content

Lactate Inflection Point and EPOC

For HL students, a deeper understanding of the metabolic responses to exercise is required, specifically focusing on the lactate inflection point as a marker of endurance capacity and EPOC as a measure of recovery.

Key Concepts (HL)

  • The Lactate Inflection Point (LIP) is the exercise intensity at which lactate production exceeds lactate clearance, causing lactate to accumulate exponentially in the blood (A.2.3.3) (p. 124).
  • An athlete's LIP is a powerful predictor of endurance performance, as it represents the highest intensity they can sustain for a prolonged period (p. 124).
  • Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC) is the volume of oxygen consumed above resting levels after exercise ceases (A.2.3.4) (p. 125).
  • EPOC consists of a fast component (restoring phosphocreatine stores and re-oxygenating myoglobin) and a slow component (removing lactate, elevated heart rate and ventilation, increased body temperature) (A.2.3.4) (p. 125).

Mechanism Explained: The Importance of Lactate Inflection Point (LIP)

While VO‚ÇÇ max represents the ceiling of aerobic power, LIP represents the usable percentage of that ceiling for sustained periods.

  1. Lactate Production and Clearance: During exercise, lactate is constantly being produced by the glycolytic system and cleared (metabolized for energy) by tissues like the heart, liver, and slow-twitch muscle fibres.
  2. Reaching the Inflection Point: At low to moderate intensities, production and clearance are balanced. As intensity increases, lactate production begins to rise. The LIP is the point where the rate of production surpasses the body's maximum rate of clearance.
  3. Metabolic Consequences: Beyond the LIP, lactate and associated hydrogen ions accumulate rapidly in the blood and muscles. This leads to a decrease in muscle pH (acidosis), which inhibits enzyme function and muscle contraction, forcing the athlete to slow down.
  4. Training Adaptations: Endurance training significantly improves the body's ability to clear lactate and pushes the LIP to a higher percentage of VO‚ÇÇ max. This allows an athlete to work at a higher intensity without accumulating lactate, which is a key adaptation for improved endurance performance.
Real-World Example (HL)
Two marathon runners have the same VO‚ÇÇ max of 65 ml/kg/min. Runner A's lactate inflection point occurs at 75% of their VO‚ÇÇ max, while Runner B, who is better trained, has a LIP at 88% of their VO‚ÇÇ max. This means Runner B can sustain a much faster pace before fatigue-inducing metabolic by-products accumulate, making them the superior marathon performer despite having the same aerobic 'engine size'.
STUDY TIP (HL): EPOC and Intensity
The magnitude and duration of EPOC are directly proportional to the intensity and duration of the exercise. High-intensity interval training (HIIT) creates a much larger oxygen deficit and therefore a significantly greater EPOC than steady-state aerobic exercise, contributing to a higher overall calorie burn post-workout.

Paper 1A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Question 1
Which of the following is the primary cause of the initial decrease in stroke volume that leads to cardiovascular drift?
  • A. A decrease in heart rate
  • B. An increase in blood viscosity
  • C. A reduction in venous return due to peripheral vasodilation
  • D. An increase in blood pressure
‚úì Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: During prolonged exercise, especially in heat, blood is shunted to the skin for cooling (peripheral vasodilation). This reduces the volume of blood returning to the heart (venous return), leading to lower end-diastolic volume and a subsequent decrease in stroke volume.

Why A is wrong: Heart rate increases, not decreases, to compensate for the falling stroke volume.

Why B is wrong: While severe dehydration can increase blood viscosity, the initial and primary cause of the drop in stroke volume is the redistribution of blood.

Why D is wrong: Blood pressure does not typically increase; in fact, mean arterial pressure may slightly decrease as part of the overall cardiovascular response.
Question 2
An athlete collapses during an ultra-marathon after consuming large volumes of plain water and is found to have a serum sodium level well below the normal range. Which condition have they most likely developed?
  • A. Hypernatremia
  • B. Hyponatremia
  • C. Severe dehydration
  • D. Cardiovascular drift
‚úì Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: Hyponatremia is characterized by abnormally low blood sodium levels. This is often caused by excessive consumption of water without adequate replacement of sodium lost in sweat, leading to a dilution of blood sodium concentration.

Why A is wrong: Hypernatremia is high blood sodium, typically caused by insufficient water intake, not excessive intake.

Why C is wrong: While the athlete is likely dehydrated to some degree, the key diagnostic factor is the low sodium level, and the primary cause is over-drinking plain water, not just fluid loss.

Why D is wrong: Cardiovascular drift is a physiological process that would be occurring, but it is not the diagnosed medical condition described.
Question 3
Which macronutrient is the dominant fuel source for an athlete competing in a 50-meter freestyle swimming event (lasting ~25 seconds)?
  • A. Lipids
  • B. Protein
  • C. Simple carbohydrates via the oxidative system
  • D. Stored carbohydrates via the phosphagen and glycolytic systems
‚úì Correct Answer: D
Why D is correct: A 50-meter sprint is a maximal intensity, short-duration activity. The immediate energy is supplied by stored ATP and creatine phosphate (the phosphagen system), followed rapidly by the anaerobic breakdown of stored muscle glycogen (carbohydrate) via the glycolytic system.

Why A is wrong: Lipids are the dominant fuel source for low-intensity, long-duration exercise, not maximal sprints.

Why B is wrong: Protein contribution to energy production during exercise is minimal (<5%) and not the primary fuel source.

Why C is wrong: The oxidative system is too slow to meet the ATP demands of a maximal 25-second sprint; anaerobic systems are dominant.
Question 4 (HL Only)
Which of the following best describes the lactate inflection point?
  • A. The point at which the body begins to produce lactate.
  • B. The highest intensity at which lactate production equals lactate clearance.
  • C. The point where the oxidative system ceases to function.
  • D. The exercise intensity that elicits an individual's VO‚ÇÇ max.
‚úì Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: The lactate inflection point is the maximal steady state where the rate of lactate entering the blood is matched by the rate of its removal. Beyond this point, clearance mechanisms are overwhelmed, and lactate begins to accumulate exponentially.

Why A is wrong: The body produces lactate even at rest; the key is the balance between production and clearance.

Why C is wrong: The oxidative system never ceases to function; in fact, it is working at a very high rate at and beyond the lactate inflection point.

Why D is wrong: The lactate inflection point always occurs at an intensity below that which elicits VO‚ÇÇ max.

Lactate Inflection Point and VO‚ÇÇ Max as Endurance Performance Indicators
Explain how an athlete's lactate inflection point and VO‚ÇÇ max are critical indicators of endurance performance. In your answer, describe how each of these is measured and how they can be improved through training.
‚úì Model Answer

VO‚ÇÇ max and the lactate inflection point (LIP) are both critical indicators of endurance performance, but they measure different aspects of aerobic capacity.

VO‚ÇÇ max is the maximal rate of oxygen uptake and utilization, representing the upper limit or 'ceiling' of the aerobic energy system. [1] A higher VO‚ÇÇ max means a greater capacity to produce ATP aerobically, which is fundamental for endurance events. [1] It is measured in a laboratory during a graded exercise test to exhaustion, where an athlete's expired air is analysed to determine the point at which oxygen consumption plateaus despite an increasing workload. [1]

The lactate inflection point (LIP) represents the highest exercise intensity that can be maintained for a prolonged period without a rapid accumulation of blood lactate. [1] It is arguably a better predictor of performance in steady-state endurance events than VO‚ÇÇ max because it reflects the maximum sustainable percentage of that VO‚ÇÇ max. [1] LIP is measured by taking blood samples at regular intervals during a graded exercise test and identifying the intensity at which blood lactate concentration begins to increase exponentially. [1]

Both can be improved through training. VO‚ÇÇ max is best improved through high-intensity interval training (HIIT) that stresses the cardiorespiratory system, leading to adaptations like increased stroke volume and cardiac output. [1] LIP is improved through both high-intensity intervals and sustained, high-volume endurance training at or near the LIP. This enhances the muscle's capacity to clear and utilize lactate, pushing the inflection point to a higher percentage of VO‚ÇÇ max. [1]

Common Errors

Awareness of common misconceptions and errors is a key step toward avoiding them in your own work. Below are some frequent mistakes students make when discussing topics in hydration, nutrition, and energy systems.

Confusing Dehydration and Hyponatremia
Assuming that all exercise-related collapse in the heat is due to dehydration, when it could be hyponatremia, which requires a completely different treatment (restricting fluids, not providing more water).
Energy Systems as "Switches"
Describing the energy systems as turning on and off sequentially, rather than understanding them as constantly working in parallel with one system being dominant based on intensity and duration.
Oversimplifying Cardiovascular Drift
Attributing cardiovascular drift solely to dehydration, ignoring other key factors like increased core temperature and peripheral vasodilation which occur even in a hydrated state.
Ignoring Electrolytes
Focusing only on water replacement for hydration and neglecting the crucial role of electrolytes (like sodium and potassium) in maintaining fluid balance and neuromuscular function.
Misinterpreting "Fat Burning Zone"
Believing that low-intensity exercise is superior for fat loss because it uses a higher percentage of fat for fuel, while ignoring that higher-intensity exercise burns more total calories (and thus more total fat) in the same amount of time.
"Carbs are Bad" Mentality
Applying general diet trends to athletic performance, failing to recognize that carbohydrates are the most critical fuel source for moderate to high-intensity exercise.
