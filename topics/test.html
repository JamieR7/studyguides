4.1 B.2.1 Newton's Laws of Motion

Overview

This section introduces the fundamental principles that govern all movement. It begins with kinematics, the description of motion (how far, how fast), and progresses to kinetics, the study of the forces that cause motion. These concepts culminate in Sir Isaac Newton's three foundational laws, which form the bedrock of biomechanical analysis.

Core Concepts

Kinematics (Linear and Angular): Motion is described using scalar (size only, e.g., distance) and vector (size and direction, e.g., displacement) quantities.

  • Linear motion (in a straight line) involves displacement (s), velocity (v), and acceleration (a), measured in metres (m), metres per second (m s‚Åª¬π), and metres per second squared (m s‚Åª¬≤), respectively.
  • Angular motion (rotation) involves angular displacement (Œ∏), angular velocity (œâ), and angular acceleration (Œ±), measured in degrees or radians, degrees/radians per second, and degrees/radians per second squared.

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia): This law states: "An object will remain at rest or continue with constant velocity unless acted on by an unbalanced force." For example, an ice hockey puck will glide across the ice at a constant velocity until it is acted upon by the friction of the ice or the force from another player's stick.

Newton's Second Law (Law of Acceleration): This law states: "The acceleration (for a body/object of constant mass) is proportional to, and in the same direction as, the unbalanced force applied to it." This is expressed by the formula F=ma. To illustrate, a much larger force is required to give a massive soccer ball the same acceleration as a lightweight table tennis ball.

Newton's Third Law (Law of Reaction): This law states: "For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction." A clear example is a sprinter in starting blocks. The sprinter pushes backwards and downwards on the blocks (the action), and the blocks push forwards and upwards on the sprinter with an equal and opposite force (the reaction), propelling them out of the blocks.

Momentum (Linear and Angular): Linear momentum (p=mv) is the product of an object's mass and velocity. It can be changed by applying an impulse (J=FŒît), which is a force applied over a period of time. Angular momentum is a measure of an object's rotation. According to the principle of conservation of angular momentum, if no external torque acts on an airborne athlete, their angular momentum remains constant. Athletes can manipulate their angular velocity (speed of rotation) by changing their moment of inertia (how mass is distributed around the axis). For example, a gymnast tucks their body to decrease their moment of inertia, which causes their angular velocity to increase.

Sporting Application

Wrestlers maximize their stability by adopting a wide stance to increase their base of support and lowering their body to bring their centre of mass closer to the ground. This makes them more resistant to being pushed or thrown off balance.

4.2 B.2.2 Fluid Mechanics

Overview

This section explores how objects and bodies move through fluids, which in a sporting context are typically air and water. It examines the predictable path of projectiles and the critical performance-influencing forces of drag and lift. Understanding these forces is key to optimizing technique in sports like swimming, cycling, and discus throwing.

Core Concepts

Projectile Motion: This is the motion of an object projected into the air where only gravity and air resistance act upon it. The pathway of a projectile is determined by three initial conditions:

  • Initial velocity of projection
  • Angle of projection
  • Height of release

The optimal projection angle for maximum range is 45¬∞ only when the projection and landing heights are equal. When projection height is above the landing height (e.g., a shot-put), the optimal angle is less than 45¬∞. When it is below (e.g., a basketball free throw), the optimal angle is greater than 45¬∞.

Drag Force: Drag is the resistive force that opposes an object's motion through a fluid. The three main types are:

  • Surface Drag: Frictional drag caused by the fluid interacting with the object's surface. Athletes reduce this by wearing smooth materials (e.g., swimsuits) or removing body hair.
  • Form Drag: Pressure drag related to an object's shape and frontal area. Athletes reduce this by streamlining their body position (e.g., a cyclist's crouch).
  • Wave Drag: A force created at the interface between two fluids, such as air and water. Swimmers can minimize this by swimming underwater after a start or turn, where permitted.

Lift Force & Bernoulli's Principle: Lift is a force that acts perpendicular to the direction of motion. It is primarily generated according to Bernoulli's principle, which states that "the pressure exerted by a fluid is inversely related to its velocity." An object shaped like an airplane wing (an airfoil) forces air to travel faster over its curved top surface than its flat bottom. This creates lower pressure on top and higher pressure below, resulting in an upward lift force.

Magnus Effect: This refers to the lift force generated by a spinning object. As a ball spins, it drags a layer of fluid around with it. This causes the fluid velocity to increase on one side of the ball and decrease on the other, creating a pressure differential that makes the ball swerve. For example, Panama's Marta Cox can generate sidespin and topspin on a free kick, causing the ball to not only swerve around the defensive wall but also dip downwards, making it difficult for the goalkeeper to save.

Sporting Application

Researchers developed a full-body swimsuit with "turbulators" (wires wrapped around the surface). Similar to the dimples on a golf ball, these turbulators create a turbulent boundary layer of water around the swimmer. This layer "clings" to the body longer, delaying flow separation and reducing form drag. The research found this technology could reduce overall drag by 13%‚Äì16%.

4.3 B.2.3 Movement Analysis and its Applications

Overview

This section provides a systematic framework for analysing sporting skills. By breaking complex movements down into distinct phases, coaches and athletes can identify biomechanical flaws or inefficiencies and develop targeted interventions to improve performance and prevent injury. A key distinction is made between discrete skills, which have a clear end, and continuous skills, which are repetitive.

Core Concepts

Phases of Movement: This approach categorizes a skill into different stages to facilitate analysis. For discrete skills, there are four common phases:

  • Preparatory phase: The initial movements that prepare the athlete for the main action.
  • Force production phase: The synchronized movement of muscles and joints to generate force for the skill.
  • Critical instant: The specific moment of execution that determines the skill's outcome (e.g., ball contact, point of release).
  • Follow-through: The movements that occur after the critical instant to decelerate the body and prevent injury.

Discrete vs. Continuous Skills:

  • Discrete skills are tasks with a fixed beginning and end, such as a tennis serve or a golf swing.
  • Continuous skills are tasks with no clearly defined beginning or end, involving repetitive cycles of movement, such as running, swimming, or cycling.

Skill Analysis Example (Continuous): The arm stroke in front crawl swimming is a continuous skill that can be broken down into four phases for analysis:

  • Entry: The hand enters the water.
  • Pull: The hand begins moving backwards on the horizontal axis.
  • Push: The hand is aligned vertically with the shoulder and pushes water backwards.
  • Recovery: The hand exits the water and moves forward for the next stroke.

Sporting Application

The analysis of a tennis forehand, a discrete skill, can be broken down into phases using video or sequential images. Question 1
According to Newton's second law, if the same unbalanced force is applied to a soccer ball and a table tennis ball, which of the following will be true?
  • A. Both balls will accelerate equally.
  • B. The table tennis ball will accelerate less than the soccer ball.
  • C. The soccer ball will have greater inertia.
  • D. The table tennis ball will accelerate more than the soccer ball.
‚úì Correct Answer: D
Why D is correct: The text states, "To give the soccer ball the same acceleration as a table tennis ball, the player must apply a larger force because the soccer ball has a greater mass than the table tennis ball" p. 316. Therefore, with the same force, the object with less mass (the table tennis ball) will accelerate more, as acceleration is inversely proportional to mass (a = F/m).

Why A is wrong: Acceleration is inversely proportional to mass for a given force. Since the masses are different, they will not accelerate equally.

Why B is wrong: The object with less mass (table tennis ball) will experience greater acceleration from the same force, not less.

Why C is wrong: While it is true that the soccer ball has greater inertia due to its greater mass, this concept relates to Newton's first law (resistance to a change in motion) and does not directly answer the question about the magnitude of acceleration, which is governed by the second law.
Question 2
A competitive cyclist adopts a low, crouched position primarily to reduce which type of drag?
  • A. Surface drag
  • B. Form drag
  • C. Wave drag
  • D. Frictional drag
‚úì Correct Answer: B
Why B is correct: Form drag is determined by the "frontal area of the object or body" p. 345. By crouching, the cyclist significantly reduces their frontal surface area exposed to the oncoming air, thereby minimizing form drag.

Why A is wrong: Surface drag is related to the texture of the surface. While a cyclist's clothing is designed to be smooth to reduce surface drag, the primary purpose of the crouched position is to alter the body's shape and frontal area.

Why C is wrong: Wave drag occurs at the interface of two fluids, like air and water p. 347. It is not relevant to a cyclist moving through the air.

Why D is wrong: Frictional drag is another term for surface drag. The crouched position has a much greater impact on form drag than on surface/frictional drag.
Question 3
A figure skater spinning on the ice pulls her arms in towards her body. What is the immediate effect on her angular velocity and moment of inertia?
  • A. Angular velocity increases; moment of inertia increases.
  • B. Angular velocity decreases; moment of inertia decreases.
  • C. Angular velocity increases; moment of inertia decreases.
  • D. Angular velocity decreases; moment of inertia increases.
‚úì Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: According to the principle of conservation of angular momentum, an athlete's angular momentum remains constant while airborne or on the ice (assuming negligible friction). The text explains that when a gymnast "tucks" by bringing their arms and legs towards the centre of gravity, they reduce their moment of inertia. "Therefore, during flight, if the moment of inertia is reduced, the angular velocity must increase to keep the angular momentum constant" p. 323. The same principle applies to the skater.

Why A and D are wrong: Pulling the arms in brings mass closer to the axis of rotation, which decreases the moment of inertia, not increases it.

Why B is wrong: Because angular momentum is conserved and the moment of inertia decreases, the angular velocity must increase, not decrease.
Question 4
In the analysis of a discrete skill like throwing a javelin, the point of release is referred to as the:
  • A. Preparatory phase
  • B. Force production phase
  • C. Critical instant
  • D. Follow-through
‚úì Correct Answer: C
Why C is correct: The text defines the critical instant as "the specific moment within the skill execution that significantly influences the skill's outcome." It explicitly lists "the point of release, for example, when throwing a javelin" as an example of a critical instant p. 359.

Why A is wrong: The preparatory phase involves the movements before force production, such as the run-up.

Why B is wrong: The force production phase involves the synchronized movements that lead up to the point of release.

Why D is wrong: The follow-through consists of the movements that occur after the point of release to safely decelerate the body.

Common Errors

Certain concepts in biomechanics are nuanced and easily confused. Taking the time to clarify these distinctions is key to building a robust and accurate understanding of the material, preventing simple mistakes on exams.

Distinguishing Mass from Weight
It is a common error to use these terms interchangeably. Mass is the amount of material in an object, measured in kilograms (kg), and it is a constant value regardless of location. Weight, however, is the force of gravity acting on that mass (Fg = mg). An object has the same mass on Earth as it does on the Moon, but its weight is significantly less on the Moon because the force of gravity is weaker.
Distinguishing Displacement from Distance
Distance is a scalar quantity that measures the total path covered by a body, without regard to direction. Displacement is a vector quantity that measures the change in position from the start point to the end point in a straight line, including direction. For example, when an athlete runs one full lap of a 400m track, the distance covered is 400m. However, because they finish exactly where they started, their linear displacement is 0m.
Differentiating Static and Dynamic Friction
Static friction is the force that acts between two surfaces that are not moving relative to each other. This force increases as an applied force increases, up to a maximum value before movement begins. Dynamic friction (or kinetic friction) is the frictional force that acts between two surfaces that are moving relative to each other. As shown in Figure 18 p. 328, the force of dynamic friction is lower than the maximum force of static friction, meaning it takes more force to start an object moving than to keep it moving.
